Guided journey

Entering the thought of Karl Jaspers

From the world to existence, from existence to transcendence

Entering Karl Jaspers's thought is not learning a finished doctrine. It is following a movement.

His major work, Philosophy (1932), crosses three regions: orientation in the world, illumination of existence, then opening onto Transcendence. This journey follows that order.

We begin by knowing what surrounds us. We then discover that all knowledge meets limits, and that no knowledge can decide in our place what we ought to be. The question turns back toward the one who knows: who am I, and how can I truly become myself?

Finally, existence discovers that it is not sufficient unto itself. It opens onto what exceeds it, without ever being able to possess it.

This journey is therefore not a list of concepts. It shows why each question leads to the next.

I · Orientation in the world

Knowing without enclosing the real

Jaspers does not begin by asking human beings to withdraw from the world. He begins with the world as it presents itself: facts, objects, the sciences, techniques, institutions, and other people.

The first philosophical demand is to know as rigorously as possible. But that rigour itself leads to discovering what knowledge can establish — and what it will never be able to turn into an object.

Step 0 · Everyday existence

I am already in a world

Before any philosophy, I live. I have a body, needs, habits, interests, roles and projects. I work, I seek security, I avoid danger, I answer the expectations of my surroundings.

This everyday life is neither false nor contemptible. It is the concrete ground of all existence. But so long as it works, I can live without truly asking who I am. I then identify with what I do, what I own, my character, or the place I occupy.

Philosophy begins when this familiar world ceases to suffice.

Step 1 · The demand to know

Knowing what can be known

Science seeks knowledge that is verifiable, demonstrable, valid for everyone. Jaspers calls compelling knowledge that before which any reasonable person must yield once the proof is established.

Philosophizing does not, therefore, mean replacing facts with impressions. We must distinguish what we know from what we believe, hope, or interpret.

Acknowledging the force of objective knowledge is a school of truth: I must accept that reality may contradict my preferences. But a question arises: can everything real become the object of such knowledge?

↓
Step 2 · The structure of knowledge

The split between subject and object

Every time I know something, an object appears to a subject. I see a tree, I study an organism, I observe a behaviour — or I reflect on myself.

But I can never step outside this relation to contemplate, from without, the subject, the object, and the totality of being. The moment I try to grasp the knowing subject, I turn it in turn into an object before me.

This subject-object split is not an error to be removed: it is the very condition of all knowledge. But it also means that being never lets itself be possessed as a thing set entirely before us.

↓
Step 3 · The limit of total knowledge

The world does not close

Each science illuminates one region of the real. Physics, biology, history and the human sciences do not use the same methods and do not answer the same questions.

We can always widen our knowledge, but we can never turn the whole world into a finished object. A total representation of the real would no longer be knowledge: it would become a world-picture wrongly raised to the rank of the absolute.

The limit of knowledge is therefore no defeat of reason. It is a critical discovery: I know what I can know, and I stop taking a partial knowledge for the totality of being.

At this limit, the question changes: if no science can tell me what I ought to be, how can I become myself?

↓

II · Illumination of existence

Becoming oneself without turning into an object

Orientation in the world studies what can become an object. The illumination of existence now questions the one who knows, chooses and acts.

Jaspers is not trying to manufacture a new theory of the human being. He is trying to awaken in each person the possibility of becoming themselves.

Step 4 · The question of the self

I am not only what I can observe of myself

I can know my body, my biography, my habits, my capacities, my emotions, certain traits of my character. All of that is my empirical reality.

But that knowledge does not yet say what I shall make of what I am. It describes my conditions, not my answer.

Jaspers calls possible existence the possibility by which I can take a position toward what is given to me, decide, become myself. Existence is not a soul hidden behind my properties: it is the non-objectifiable origin from which I think, choose, act and answer for myself.

I am therefore never only a finished result. I remain a possibility entrusted to my freedom.

↓
Step 5 · The inner trap

Self-reflection can illuminate me or dissolve me

Reflecting on oneself is necessary. I can recognise a fear, a jealousy, a contradiction, a desire to dominate, a way of deceiving myself.

But each explanation can in turn be questioned: why do I have this desire? is my choice really free? is this decision not a reaction? behind this motive, is another not hiding?

Reflection can then become endless. By trying to know with certainty what I am before acting, I end up beginning nothing at all. I observe, I analyse, I assess — and I no longer decide.

For Jaspers, lucidity is not yet the self. I do not discover myself entirely before acting: I am also illuminated in what I dare to choose, carry out, and maintain.

↓
Step 6 · The breach

Boundary situations

Some situations can be transformed by knowledge, technique or organisation. Others belong to the very condition of existence.

I must die. I meet suffering. I am engaged in struggle. I act without being able to avoid all guilt. My existence depends on chances and conditions I did not choose.

Jaspers calls these realities boundary situations. I cannot place myself outside them in order to abolish them. I can mask them, flee them, try to reduce them to mere problems. But they return.

They do not automatically make anyone better: they can also crush or close one in. Yet, looked at without illusion, they can awaken the decisive question: how shall I exist within what I can neither avoid nor master?

↓
Step 7 · Decision

A situated and responsible freedom

To be free does not mean being without a body, a past, a character or conditions. A freedom situated outside the world could accomplish nothing.

But acknowledging my determinations does not mean they decide entirely in my place. I can take a position toward them, take them on, resist them, give them a direction.

Freedom is therefore neither caprice nor omnipotence. It becomes real when I answer for what I do in a concrete situation.

Not everything depends on me. Yet what does depend on me can commit me entirely. I do not create myself from nothing — but nor can I hand over to my causes the responsibility for my answer.

↓
Step 8 · Lived time

Becoming oneself within a history

My freedom never exists in general. It is played out in this era, this body, this family, this language, these encounters, these commitments.

Jaspers calls historicity the unity between what is given to me and the way I freely take it up. The past becomes what I can be faithful to. The future remains possibility. The present becomes decision.

Becoming oneself therefore does not mean endlessly changing identity. It also demands maintaining, over time, certain decisions and certain ties. I become historical when I turn what happened to me into a history I answer for.

↓
Step 9 · The necessary solitude

I must be able to stand on my own

True communication presupposes that I do not dissolve into the other. If I seek only their approval, if I repeat the group's ideas, if I give up my own decision to preserve the bond — it is not yet my existence that communicates.

I must become autonomous enough to speak from my own ground, to bear disagreement, and to leave the other free not to confirm me.

But this solitude is not social isolation: it is the availability of a possible existence. If I shut myself absolutely inside myself, my self becomes empty and communication disappears.

I must therefore be able to be alone in order truly to meet the other. And yet I cannot become fully myself by remaining alone.

↓
Step 10 · The meeting of two freedoms

Existential communication

Existential communication is neither a pleasant conversation, nor a permanent confession, nor the search for agreement at any price.

It becomes possible when two people, free enough not to possess each other, agree to show themselves, to question, and to be put in question.

Each wills their own becoming — but also wills that the other become themselves. Neither seeks victory, superiority or obedience.

Jaspers calls this relation the loving struggle: a common struggle for truth, carried by a trust strong enough to bear frankness, resistance, and the risk of being transformed.

My freedom is therefore both a condition and a fruit of communication. I must already be able to stand before the other. But it is also in their answer that I discover the illusions I could not escape alone.

↓
Step 11 · The world between us

To communicate does not mean to merge

Two existences do not meet in a fusion where all differences would vanish.

They communicate through contents: a question, a work, a task, a decision, a truth sought, a responsibility, an action in the world.

Immediate sympathy, attraction, shared emotion, the feeling of understanding each other without speaking — these may open a relationship. They are not enough to make it existential. Without content, the contact remains fragile, impersonal, or merely affective.

Conversely, ideas and tasks become dead when no one carries them. In Jaspers, the self and the world remain inseparable: we truly meet by taking something seriously together.

↓
Step 12 · The forms of truth

Truth does not have a single voice

A scientific proposition can be demonstrated. An existential decision cannot be proven like a formula. That does not mean all opinions are equal.

Jaspers distinguishes several modes of truth. Objective truth, which demands evidence. The truth of spirit, which organises ideas and totalities of meaning. Existential truth, in which a person commits what they will to be. And a metaphysical truth, which can only be communicated indirectly.

These truths must neither be confused nor cancel one another. Objective truth prevents existence from taking refuge in illusion. Existential truth prevents knowledge from claiming to decide the meaning of my life.

I do not, therefore, possess truth as an object. I seek it in knowledge, in action, in communication, and in fidelity to what I answer for.

↓

III · Metaphysics

Opening onto what never becomes an object

Orientation in the world met the limits of knowledge. The illumination of existence met freedom, guilt, communication, and the impossibility of giving oneself one's own foundation.

Metaphysics begins when existence asks after what exceeds it — without turning that question into knowledge of a new object.

Step 13 · Transcending

Transcendence

Transcendence is not an object hidden somewhere behind the world. It can be neither observed, nor demonstrated, nor possessed.

To transcend means to carry out a movement of thought and of existence beyond every determinate objectivity — without thereby leaving the world.

Existence discovers that it did not create itself, and that it cannot be its own absolute foundation. Its freedom remains active, but it also receives itself as a possibility that is given to it.

Transcendence names that toward which existence opens when it refuses both the despair of nothingness and the illusion of possessing the absolute.

↓
Step 14 · The indirect language

The ciphers of Transcendence

Since Transcendence never becomes an object, it cannot be described directly. Yet certain realities of the world can become, for existence, a language.

A work, a landscape, a story, an encounter, a decision, a religious figure, silence or failure can become ciphers.

A cipher is neither a proof, nor a secret message, nor an arbitrary emotion. The reality that becomes a cipher remains a reality of the world. But, in a particular historical situation, it can make perceptible a depth that objective knowledge cannot enclose.

No universal dictionary allows ciphers to be translated. They remain ambiguous. They can be heard, refused, twisted, or turned into superstitions. One must learn to read them without claiming to possess them.

↓
Step 15 · The ultimate limit

Foundering

Everything that exists in time can disappear. Our works, our relationships, our institutions, our certainties, and even the image we have of ourselves remain fragile.

Jaspers calls foundering the inevitable failure of every attempt to turn a finite reality into an absolute foundation.

Foundering does not automatically save. It can lead to bitterness, to nihilism, to flight, or to the search for a false certainty.

But when it is crossed without lying, it can become the ultimate cipher: not the proof of another world, but the destruction of our claim to possess being. I then remain called to act, even when success is no longer guaranteed.

↓
Step 16 · Standing in non-knowledge

Philosophical faith

Jaspers speaks of a faith that rests neither on scientific proof nor on obedience to an imposed revelation.

Philosophical faith remains inseparable from reason, freedom and doubt. It possesses no definitive formulation of Transcendence.

It consists in living without closing the fundamental questions: trusting the possibility of truth, of communication and of freedom — while acknowledging that no knowledge can guarantee their ultimate meaning.

It does not abolish non-knowledge. It learns not to fill it too quickly with images, dogmas, or manufactured certainties.

↓
Step 17 · The return

Returning to the world and answering for one's age

Metaphysics does not lead us out of the world. It brings us back to it with more lucidity.

I must go on knowing the facts, using the sciences, acting within institutions, meeting others, and answering for the consequences of my decisions.

But I now know that no knowledge possesses the totality, that no organisation will abolish the boundary situations, and that no doctrine can replace my freedom.

To philosophize then means: to know without totalising, to decide without believing oneself omnipotent, to communicate without possessing the other, and to remain open to a Transcendence that cannot be turned into an object.

Existence returns into history, into politics, into daily life. Not delivered from its limits — but become more responsible for what it accomplishes within them.

Where on this path am I today: in the security of the everyday world, in the demand to understand, in endless reflection, before a boundary situation, at the moment of deciding, in the risk of a true communication — or before what exceeds every answer?

These steps do not form a staircase to be climbed once. The three movements remain present together.

I must always begin again to orient myself in the world. I must always become myself again in decision and communication. I must always resist the temptation to possess what exceeds me.

That is why, for Jaspers, to philosophize never means to arrive. To philosophize is to be on the way.
