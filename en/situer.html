Where to place Jaspers

After whom, beside whom, against what

In thirty seconds

The essential

A German physician turned philosopher, who lived through both world wars, Nazism and the atom bomb. He is filed among the existentialists; he refused the label. His thesis fits in one sentence: we can know an enormous amount — but never turn the whole, other people, or ourselves into fully masterable objects.

The century

A man in his time

Karl Jaspers was born in 1883 and died in 1969: his life spans exactly the century that shattered every European certainty. He was thirty-one when the First World War broke out; he taught under the Weimar Republic; he was stripped of his chair in 1937 for refusing to divorce Gertrud, his Jewish wife; he lived eight years awaiting her deportation; he witnessed Hiroshima, the Cold War, and humanity's entry into the age in which it can abolish itself.

Hold on to this fact, for it governs everything else: his philosophy was not thought up in a quiet study. Every great question he asks — guilt, freedom under constraint, the human being reduced to a number, the truth one does not possess — was first put to him by his century, and often addressed to him personally.

His life in detail

The genealogy

Whom does he come after?

Three names form the bedrock of his thought. He does not comment on them as a historian would: he thinks with them.

Kant (1724-1804) His master, whom he read all his life. From Kant comes the decisive idea: knowledge has structural limits, and what we cannot know is not therefore nothing. The whole map of the Encompassing descends from there.
Kierkegaard (1813-1855) and Nietzsche (1844-1900) His two “exceptions”, as he calls them — the two men who broke systematic philosophy by thinking from within their own existence. One a believer, the other an atheist, both irreconcilable and both irrefutable: from them his Reason and Existenz (1935) sets out, and to them he owes the very word existence.
Hegel (1770-1831), against the grain The great adversary. Hegel claimed to unfold absolute knowledge and to turn history on a single axis. Jaspers spent his life dismantling that gesture — and his Axial Age, where five civilisations awaken as equals, is a direct answer to Hegelian Eurocentrism.

The contemporaries

Beside whom does he think?

Max Weber (1864-1920) His elder, his friend, his model of a man in Heidelberg. From him comes the demand for scientific rigour — and the idea that science, precisely because it is rigorous, will never tell us which values to choose.
Martin Heidegger (1889-1976) The other great name of the philosophy of existence. A real friendship, then a rupture: in 1933 Heidegger joined the Nazi party. Two neighbouring thoughts, two lives that fork — the demonstration, by the facts, that thinking rightly is not enough.
Hannah Arendt (1906-1975) His student, whose dissertation he supervised; then, until his death, his friend and interlocutor. Their correspondence is one of the finest examples of what he called communication.
Husserl (1859-1938) and Freud (1856-1939) Two neighbours he refuses. From Husserl's phenomenology he borrows tools but contests the claim to found an absolute science. Toward psychoanalysis he is harsher still: the psychiatrist he had been sees in it a method that, by explaining everything, turns the human being into an object — and a belief that presents itself as knowledge.
Jean-Paul Sartre (1905-1980) The younger man who would make the word “existentialism” famous in 1945 — and from whom Jaspers took particular care to distinguish himself.

The label

Is he an existentialist?

The textbooks file him there, and it is not absurd: he speaks of existence, anxiety, choice, death. But the label misses the essential, and he himself insisted on stepping away from it. He preferred to say: philosophy of Existenz.

The difference is no matter of vocabulary. An “-ism” is a doctrine: a body of theses one adopts, teaches, turns into a school. Yet Jaspers wants to found no school and offers no thesis about what man is. His aim is another: to awaken in the reader the possibility of becoming themselves. A doctrine can be transmitted; an awakening cannot — it happens, or it does not.

Two marks separate him sharply from French existentialism. First, Transcendence: for Jaspers, man is not his own foundation, and at the summit of his freedom he experiences receiving himself — where Sartre asserts a man with no author but himself. Second, reason: far from opposing existence to reason, Jaspers holds them inseparable, and he never ceased defending science against those who despise it. Neither a pathos of the absurd nor solitary heroism: a thought that demands rigour as much as courage.

The position

Against what, and for what

The simplest way to place him is by what he refuses — and by what he puts in its stead. None of the left-hand columns is a straw man: these are real positions, held by serious people, and often tempting.

Look at the shape of this list: on every line he refuses both extremes. Neither scientism nor contempt for science. Neither dogma nor closed atheism. Neither possessed truth nor relativism. It is an uncomfortable position, belonging to no camp — and perhaps that is why he is read less than others. This is no lukewarm middle way: it is a refusal, held on eleven fronts, to buy intellectual peace at the price of amputating the real.

The centre

His question, in one sentence

Many philosophers ask: what is man? — and answer with a definition. Jaspers shifts the question, and that is Jaspers entire:

How can I become truly myself — without shutting myself inside myself, in communication with others, and in openness to what exceeds me?

This is why this site exists. One can learn what Jaspers wrote; that is not what he asked for. He asked that we use it: discover the limits of what we know, never reduce anyone to a definition, take on the situations we cannot flee, answer for our choices, seek truth with others, and stay open to what cannot be possessed.
