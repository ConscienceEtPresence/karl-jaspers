The mystery of hominisation

How does a being capable of asking who it is come about?

The question

In thirty seconds

Biology describes the transformation of the body very well: upright stance, the hand, the brain, tools. It does not tell us when someone, for the first time, wondered what they were doing here. Between the highly skilled animal and the being who questions itself lies a threshold we shall never be able to date.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Let us take up what science establishes, and it is considerable: bipedalism frees the hands; hands make things; the brain grows and reorganises; tools improve over hundreds of thousands of years; fire is mastered; groups migrate across the whole planet. All of this is dated, measured, debated among specialists. Jaspers disputes none of it — he learns from it.

But now ask the question that interests him: at what moment, in this long transformation, does a being appear that knows itself to exist? Not a being that reacts, adapts, solves problems — crows and octopuses do as much, remarkably. A being that takes distance from itself. That can judge its own fear. That knows it will die, and, knowing it, changes the way it lives.

We have signs, and they are overwhelming: burials (a body is laid down rather than abandoned); pigments and ornaments (a body is marked with no vital use whatsoever); cave art (people painted in the dark, in places almost no one would see); tools made to make other tools (someone is thinking of tomorrow); objects carried hundreds of kilometres (something is worth more than its weight). Each of these signs says: someone here was not content merely to survive.

And here is the delicate point, where Jaspers demands rigour: none of these signs dates the appearance of inwardness. A sign attests a behaviour, never a consciousness. We would like a border — a before, an after, an administrative date for the first self-awareness. Prehistory stubbornly refuses to fill in that form. No doubt because hominisation is not an event but a very long passage, whose inner side has left, by its very nature, no fossil trace.

What Jaspers draws from this is not a renunciation but a displacement: the origin of man is not a fact we shall one day discover at the back of a cave. It is that from which we search — and the fact that we ask the question is already the only proof we have that the threshold was crossed.

The metaphor

To feel it

Watch a child grow. There is no single day on which they “become” someone — no date, no ceremony, no observable click. And yet, one day, they speak of themselves in the third person, then they say “I”, then they lie, then they feel ashamed, then they ask what happens when you die. Nobody saw the threshold being crossed. Everybody sees that it was.

A concrete situation

The negative hands

In certain decorated caves there are “negative” hands: a hand pressed against the wall, pigment blown around it, and the outline remains, in hollow, for tens of thousands of years. What can honestly be said about them?

The facts first: the gesture requires preparation (grinding, mixing, blowing), it is repeated many times, often in places hard to reach, with no use whatever for feeding or protection. Then the hypotheses — and there are many: rite, signature, apprenticeship, play, prayer, mark of belonging. None imposes itself; prehistorians still debate them.

But notice what all the hypotheses share, and which is the real message: someone wanted to leave a mark of their passage. Someone distinguished themselves from the wall. We shall never know what they were thinking — but we know there was a “they”. This is exactly what Jaspers calls a sign: it proves nothing about the content of that consciousness, and it attests everything about its existence.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Great apes make tools, recognise themselves in mirrors, transmit techniques. Your ‘threshold’ is merely a difference of degree that human vanity calls a difference of nature.”

The objection is solid and the facts are correct — ethology has spectacularly narrowed the gap since 1949. Chimpanzees make things, learn, pass on local cultures; some birds plan ahead; elephants linger by their dead. Any answer denying this would be lying.

Jaspers has no need to deny it — and that is what makes his position tenable. He is not looking for a biological border that would set man apart within nature; he observes one very simple fact: this very discussion. A being that wonders whether it differs from the animal, that weighs the evidence, that fears flattering itself — such a being is doing something of which we have no other example. The question “am I merely an animal?” is not an animal's question.

Degree or nature? Jaspers prudently leaves that biologists' debate open. He shifts it: the difference that interests him is not a box in the classification of living things, it lies in the relation a human being maintains with themselves. And on that point humility remains in order in both directions: do not think yourself outside nature — but do not blind yourself either to what we are doing, right now, in discussing this.

For me

What it changes

There is a very practical lesson in this chapter. For Jaspers, hominisation is not a past and closed event; it is a possibility that stays open at every moment. Every time someone stops merely reacting — to stress, to an insult, to habit — and takes one second of distance from themselves, they repeat, on their own scale, the inaugural gesture: they separate themselves from the wall.

And the reverse is true. Nothing guarantees that threshold once crossed. A whole life can unfold in pure reaction — efficient, busy, never questioned. This is exactly what Jaspers will observe in our technical age, where one can function marvellously without ever asking what one is doing here. Hominisation is not our past: it is our task.

In the past few days, did I once step back from what I was doing — or did I only react, very efficiently, to whatever came up?

Three questions to check

Does a burial prove an awareness of death?

It attests a behaviour — a body laid down, not abandoned — and makes the hypothesis serious. But a sign attests a gesture, never the content of a consciousness.

Why can the birth of inwardness not be dated?

Because hominisation is not an event but a very long passage, whose inner side leaves, by its very nature, no fossil trace.

What does Jaspers reply to “apes make tools too”?

He does not deny it. He shifts the question: the remarkable fact is that we can ask whether we differ from animals. That question is not an animal's question.
