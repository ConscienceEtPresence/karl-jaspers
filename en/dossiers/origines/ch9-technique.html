Technology and the new age

Can a society become extremely efficient while losing the sense of what it is doing?

The question

In thirty seconds

After the Axial Age, Jaspers sees a second rupture: planetary technology. It has doubled our lives, fed billions, connected the whole Earth — and it makes everything calculable, ourselves included. The danger is not the machine: it is the human being absorbed by what they have built.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Jaspers is categorical on this point: we are living through the second great break in human history. The first was the Axial Age, the awakening of consciousness. The second is technology, from the nineteenth century onward — and he judges it of comparable reach, except that, unlike the axis, this one envelops the entire planet from the outset.

What it brings must be said first, and Jaspers says it without reserve: it feeds, heals, transports, educates, lengthens lives, links continents. A physician does not sneer at antibiotics. What he observes next is subtler, and it makes the whole chapter: to organise billions of people, an apparatus is required — administrative, industrial, informational. And that apparatus has one law: to function, it must make everything calculable. So it manufactures functions, profiles, averages, flows. Out of us, among other things.

Here the diagnosis becomes piercing. The apparatus does not oppress us at first: it relieves us. It spares us decisions, fills the void, supplies ready-to-wear opinions and bottomless entertainment. No dictatorship is needed: flight from oneself can be comfortable, even pleasant. This is why Jaspers speaks of “the masses” not as a quantity of people, but as a mode of existence — the one in which we function very well without ever asking who we are.

Extending it to our day — for Jaspers died in 1969: what he described has intensified beyond what he could see. Automation and algorithms no longer merely organise our tasks; they anticipate our desires and shape what we see. Media have become continuous streams calibrated to capture attention. Globalisation has achieved the interdependence he foretold — without producing the communication he hoped for. And two new threats have taken shape: the ecological crisis and nuclear weapons, where humanity has for the first time the means of its own abolition. Note the structure: these are not accidents of technology, they are its successes pushed to the limit.

His conclusion is disarmingly sober: no technical solution will solve a problem that is not technical. What is needed, he says, is a change in man himself — not one more advance, but a conversion of consciousness: that each person become again someone who answers for what they do. It is little, and it is all we have.

The metaphor

To feel it

A smart home regulates the temperature, the light, the shopping, the music — better than you would. You no longer have a single decision to make. One day you notice you no longer know whether you like being cold.

A concrete situation

The day that happened without me

Woken by an alarm. Commute optimised by an app. A day cut into tasks, messages processed on an assembly line, meetings back to back. In the evening, a screen offers exactly what will hold us — and it works. Nothing dramatic: everything is comfortable, efficient, connected.

And yet, switching off at night, that diffuse impression everyone knows: the day went by without me. I functioned. Who lived? No tyrant intervened, no violence was done. Simply, at no moment in those sixteen hours did anyone have to decide anything irreplaceable.

Jaspers described this mechanism in 1931 — before screens, before algorithms, before everything we take to be the cause of the problem. That is what makes his diagnosis so precious: he saw the root, not the symptoms. The root is not in our machines: it is in the relief we feel at no longer having to be someone.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“The talk of a privileged man. Technology has saved more lives than all philosophy put together. Without it, half of us would have died at birth. Easy to criticise it with an antibiotic in your bloodstream.”

A fair objection — and Jaspers shares it. It bears repeating, because he is too quickly filed among the scourges of modernity: he was a physician, he survived an illness that would have killed him without the medicine of his day, and he wrote in black and white that technology is a conquest of reason, not a curse.

His critique bears on something else, and it is a point of logic. Technology is a means. A means cannot set ends; it can only serve them faster. Yet we live in a civilisation where the means has become so powerful that it supplies its own ends: we do because we can, we optimise because it is optimisable, we collect because it is collectable. The question “what for?” has no forum left in which to be asked.

And notice that the answer “technology will solve technology's problems” is exactly the symptom. It presupposes that every question is an engineering problem. But “what do we want to become?” is not one — no algorithm will compute it, not for want of power, but because it is not a computable question. Jaspers does not ask for less technology: he asks that someone remain standing before it.

For me

What it changes

There is a practical question to ask often, almost an examination: in this activity — this news feed, this optimisation, this meeting — am I using the tool, or is the tool using me? The answer is rarely flattering, and always instructive.

And there is a very simple practice: cultivate the acts the machine cannot perform in my place. A decision fully owned, with no poll and no advice (freedom). A true conversation, face to face, without a screen (communication). An hour given to what serves no purpose — a sky, a piece of music, a silence (ciphers, those signs in which the absolute lets itself be sensed). These are not “wellness breaks”: they are the places where one becomes someone again. In the age of algorithms, each such act is a small victory of the human being over the series.

Which decision, this week, did I let a habit, an app or a procedure make — when it was mine to make?

Check and connect

What will happen depends in the end on one thing no apparatus can produce: the human being who changes himself.
After Karl Jaspers, The Atom Bomb and the Future of Man (1958) and Man in the Modern Age (1931) — paraphrase; see the original texts.

Three questions to check

Why does Jaspers speak of a second great rupture in history?

After the Axial Age (the awakening of consciousness), technology constitutes a break of comparable magnitude — except that it envelops the entire planet from the outset.

What is the law of the technical apparatus?

To function, it must make everything calculable — including us: it manufactures functions, profiles, averages. And it does not oppress us at first: it relieves us.

Is Jaspers against technology?

No — he was a physician and owed it his life. What he refuses is that a means should set the ends: “what do we want to become?” is not a computable question, and no technical advance will answer it.
