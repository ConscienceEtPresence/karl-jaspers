Does history have a meaning?

Is history going somewhere?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers refuses both easy answers. No, no law of history guarantees progress — the twentieth century settled that. But no, history is not therefore absurd: it is open. It becomes what human beings make of what they received, in situations they did not choose.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

A distinction must come first, for the whole disagreement rests on a confusion. Technical progress exists; it is cumulative and measurable: we can heal what could not be healed, our machines fly. Moral progress is not cumulative: nothing we have learned about goodness passes automatically to the next generation. Every child born begins again from scratch. Confusing the two — believing that growing power makes people better — is the modern age's costliest illusion. Jaspers's century supplied the demonstration: the most cultivated country in Europe, at the summit of its science, organised Auschwitz.

Hence his firm refusal of philosophies of history that claim to know its necessary course in advance — whether Hegel, dogmatic Marxism, or any mechanism, natural or historical, erected into an absolute law. He sees a double fault in this. An intellectual fault: no one stands outside history to see its plan; we are inside it (this is the Encompassing — we never look down upon the whole). And a moral fault, graver still: whoever believes they know the meaning of history believes themselves entitled to pay its price with other people's lives. The century's great massacres were all committed in the name of a guaranteed future.

But Jaspers refuses, with equal firmness, the opposite conclusion. That history has no law does not mean it is a chaos without significance. He holds a narrow position, hard to maintain: history has an origin we can observe (the Axial Age), a possible unity (the communication of all with all), and a direction we can will — with no guarantee whatever that it will come about.

The right word, for him, is therefore neither “demonstrable meaning” nor “absurdity”: it is openness. The future is not written; nor is it just anything. It depends on decisions — ours. And if we ask where all this finally goes, Jaspers's answer is not to be found in history: the meaning of history will never be an object of historical knowledge. It is sought, sensed, never possessed — exactly like the Transcendence of which it is the vastest cipher.

The metaphor

To feel it

A river did not decide to reach the sea, and yet it does. We are not that river. We are more like people in a boat, on flowing water: the current exists, we did not choose it, it carries us — and yet every stroke of the oar counts, and no one rows in our place.

A concrete situation

March 1945

Heidelberg, early 1945. Gertrud Jaspers's deportation is scheduled for mid-April. Karl and she have had the poison ready for eight years. American troops enter the city on 30 March — two weeks before the date.

What does this story say about the meaning of history? A believer will see providence; an atheist, a coincidence; a historian, the speed of an offensive. None can prove their reading, and Jaspers — who lived the scene — carefully drew no law from it. He never wrote that history had saved him.

But see what, in this story, did not depend on chance: the eight years of refusal. No one guaranteed Jaspers that staying would pay off — it very nearly did not. He did not hold out because history was moving in the right direction; he held out without knowing. This is exactly what Jaspers calls living historically: acting in a situation one did not choose, without guarantee, and answering for what one does all the same.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“If history has no guaranteed meaning, why act at all? We might as well tend our garden. Your ‘openness’ is a polite word for saying nothing matters.”

This is the most tempting objection, and Jaspers turns it around exactly. Look: the argument assumes we act only if the outcome is guaranteed. But that is the reasoning of calculation, not of existence. No one loves, raises a child, treats a patient or tells the truth because success is assured. We do it because it is worth doing — and the guarantee was never part of the deal.

So let us turn the question around: what happens when a guarantee is given? If the meaning of history is known in advance, my decision becomes decorative: I am merely executing a programme already written. Guaranteed meaning does not ground action — it abolishes it. And worse: it authorises anything, since the end is certain. This is what Jaspers saw with his own eyes.

His position is therefore the opposite of a renunciation: it is precisely because nothing is guaranteed that what I do actually counts. The openness of history is not indifference — it is the very site of responsibility. What Jaspers takes away with one hand (the promise of an assured future), he gives back with the other, and it is far heavier to carry: our freedom.

For me

What it changes

This chapter frees us from two opposite weights. From naive optimism, first: nothing will sort itself out on its own, not through progress, not through technology, not through the meaning of History — that belief died in the twentieth century, and it is better not to revive it. From despair, next, and this matters more: if nothing is written in advance, then nothing is lost in advance either. Fatalism is as groundless as the promise.

What remains is what Jaspers calls living historically, and it comes down to three very concrete gestures: knowing what I received (the inheritance, faults included); knowing that my situation does not belong to me (my era, my country, my body); and acting anyway, without guarantee, answering for what I do. No law of history will do it in my place. That may be disappointing. It is also the only dignity we have left — and it is entirely within reach, starting tomorrow morning.

On what, in my own time, have I given up acting because “it won't change anything” — and what does that giving-up really cost me?

Three questions to check

What distinction does Jaspers set out first?

Technical progress is cumulative and measurable; moral progress is not — every generation begins again. Confusing the two is the modern age's costliest illusion.

Why does he refuse philosophies that know the course of history?

For an intellectual reason (no one stands outside history to see its plan) and a moral one: whoever believes they know the meaning entitles themselves to pay its price with other people's lives.

If history has no guaranteed meaning, why act?

Because a guarantee never grounded action — it would rather abolish it: if everything is written, my decision is merely decorative. It is because nothing is settled that what I do counts.
