The unity of humanity

Can there be a common history without erasing cultures?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers seeks a humanity that is one — but the unity he aims at is neither an empire, nor a world religion, nor a single market. It is the unity of an endless conversation: we are one because we can speak to one another, not because we would end up thinking alike.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

The whole book was tending toward this question. If humanity awakened once, simultaneously, in five separate centres (the Axial Age), then a common history is not a utopia: it is a possibility already attested. What remains is to know which unity. And here Jaspers sets aside, one by one, all the false solutions.

It is not uniformity: a humanity in which everyone thought the same thing would not be united, it would be dead — no one left to meet. It is not empire: unity by force is the very negation of what it claims to unite. It is not a universal religion: every tradition writes Transcendence in its own language, none owns the original. Nor is it planetary technology: it has made us interdependent without making us close — one can be connected to everyone and speak to no one.

What, then? One thing only, and it is fragile: communication. We are one, says Jaspers, exactly to the extent that we can truly speak to one another — that “loving struggle” in which each risks their position without seeking to win. The unity of humanity is not a state to be reached, one day, when everyone agrees. It is a process, a task, a conversation that must stay open. Hence his densest formula: truth is what binds us together.

This presupposes a condition Jaspers states without hedging: no one possesses truth as an object. Whoever believes they hold it has nothing left to learn from the other — all that remains is to convert them, or eliminate them. This is why his refusal of dogmatism is not lukewarmness: it is the material condition of peace. And he adds the thing that saves everything: truth can be present in the search itself, without being possessed. We can seek together what none of us holds.

The metaphor

To feel it

An orchestra is not united because everyone plays the same note — that would be a monotonous din. It is united because each holds their own part while listening to the others. Remove the listening: you are left with musicians side by side. Remove the differences: there is no music left.

A concrete situation

The debate that isn't one

Two people from opposing traditions debate in public — religious against atheist, say. Each has prepared their arguments, quotations, objections. Each speaks while looking at the audience. An hour later, each leaves with exactly the convictions they came in with, and the feeling of having won.

Nothing happened. That was not communication: it was two synchronised monologues. Neither risked anything — not their position, not their image, not a single certainty. And one can do this for years, with the clear conscience of duty done.

Now imagine the same pair, with no audience, at a table. One says: “there is one thing I cannot manage to defend in my own tradition.” The other answers: “there is one thing missing in mine.” This is no longer a debate, and there will be no winner. But something true has just entered the room. Jaspers asks for nothing else — and it is almost impossible.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“A nice idea from a Swiss professor. In the real world cultures do not converse: they compete, dominate, kill each other. Your ‘communication’ is wishful thinking.”

First, recall who is speaking. The man who writes this is no sheltered idealist: he was stripped of his chair, forbidden to publish, he lived eight years waiting for his wife's deportation, and he watched his country organise a genocide in the name of a supposedly possessed truth. About the violence of cultures, Jaspers knew more than his critics.

And it is precisely from there that he draws his position. He does not say that communication reigns; he says it is the only alternative to violence, and that it is constantly under threat. He does not claim it is easy: he claims there is nothing else. All the other possible unities have been tried — empire, forced conversion, single ideology — and we know what they produce: he saw the results from his window in Heidelberg.

Finally, it is not a wish: it is a demand addressed to each of us, here and now. Communication does not begin in international institutions; it begins in a conversation where someone stops wanting to win. It is tiny, it is fragile, and it can be tested this very evening — which is more than can be said of most grand political solutions.

For me

What it changes

There is a very concrete invitation here, almost an exercise. This week, someone thinks differently from me about something that matters — politics, religion, education. The reflex is to file them (“he's on that side”) and prepare my arguments. Jaspers's exercise is the opposite: ask them a question to which I do not know the answer, and listen to the end.

This is not niceness: it is method. If my truth is true, it does not fear the test; if it fears the test, then it is already a possession, not a truth. And notice what this changes on the scale of the world: for Jaspers, the unity of humanity is not decreed at summits — it is made, or unmade, in each of these tiny conversations. Including mine, tonight.

With whom, among the people around me, have I given up speaking truly because “we'll never agree anyway”?

Three questions to check

Which false unities does Jaspers set aside?

Uniformity (no one left to meet), empire (unity by force), universal religion (no one owns the original of Transcendence) and planetary technology alone (interdependent without being close).

What, then, does the unity of humanity consist in?

In communication: we are one exactly to the extent that we can truly speak to one another. It is not a state to be reached, but a conversation to be kept open.

Why is the refusal of dogmatism, for Jaspers, a condition of peace?

Because whoever believes they possess truth has nothing left to learn from the other: all that remains is to convert or eliminate them. Truth can be present in the search, without being possessed.
