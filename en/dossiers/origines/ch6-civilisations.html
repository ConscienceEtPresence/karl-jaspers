The first great civilisations

Why were the first civilisations not enough to awaken man?

The question

In thirty seconds

Around 3000 BC, in Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus valley and China, writing, the state, the city and monuments appear. It is an immense achievement — and an ambivalence: these civilisations preserve and transmit, but they also invent the apparatus, the masses and domination. The awakening will come later, and elsewhere.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

What appears then must be measured: writing (a memory outside bodies), the state (an organisation that outlives its rulers), the city (thousands of strangers living together), administration, calculation, the calendar, monuments, organised religion. Within a few centuries, humanity equips itself with instruments we still live by. Our administrations, our offices, our archives are the direct descendants of the scribes of Sumer.

But Jaspers observes these civilisations with a reserve that surprises, and it must be understood. They are powerful, learned, monumental — and yet, he says, man remains within them as if enveloped in myth. The order of the world goes without saying: pharaoh is divine, the gods regulate the seasons, everyone occupies the place the cosmic order assigns them. An enormous amount of thinking goes on — Babylonian astronomy is prodigious — but no one thinks against the received order. The question “what if all this were false?” has nowhere yet to be asked.

And here is the ambivalence that makes the whole chapter, one Jaspers never stops underlining. Those same instruments that make it possible to preserve, transmit and protect also make it possible to dominate. Writing first serves to count stocks and debts — that is, to know who owes what to whom. The state protects and levies taxes. Administration organises and files. The city gathers and crowds. The monument elevates and crushes. Every civilisation is this double thing: a memory and an apparatus.

This is why this chapter prepares directly for the diagnosis Jaspers will make of our own age (see Man in the Modern Age, 1931). The bureaucratic apparatus is not a modern invention: it is born in Sumer with the first scribe who inscribes a debt. What modernity adds is scale and power. The problem itself is five thousand years old: how can a human being remain someone within the organisation they themselves have built?

The metaphor

To feel it

A library is a treasure: it keeps what the death of the living would have erased. It is also a place where someone decides what goes in and what does not. The same building preserves and selects, opens and forbids. The first civilisations are that building, on the scale of a world.

A concrete situation

The first tablet

The oldest clay tablets of Mesopotamia contain no poem, no prayer, no heroic tale. They contain accounts: so many measures of barley, so many sheep, so many jars, and the name of who owes what.

Let us pause there, for it is dizzying. Humanity's most prodigious invention — the one that will make Homer, the Bible, the Upanishads and this very site possible — first served bookkeeping. Not out of baseness: because a city of ten thousand people cannot hold together without knowing who owes what. External memory is born of an administrative need.

And see what comes with it. As soon as a debt can be inscribed, it can be claimed years later, passed to an heir, upheld before a court. Writing makes justice possible — and debt slavery. It makes law possible — and record-keeping on people. Neither of these two faces is an accident of the other: they are the same tool. This is exactly the ambivalence Jaspers asks us to hold, without naivety or resentment.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Isn't this condescending? You say of Egypt, which lasted three thousand years and built the pyramids, that it was ‘asleep in myth’. That is modern Western contempt for whatever is not itself.”

The objection is legitimate and must be taken seriously — all the more since Jaspers himself saw it coming. So let us be clear about what he does not say: he does not say these civilisations were stupid (their knowledge dwarfs ours in many fields), nor spiritually poor (their religious texts are of immense depth), nor inferior. The word “asleep” does not judge a value; it designates a particular relation to the world: one in which the established order is not in question.

And notice against whom the argument is really directed. It is precisely Jaspers who, in this same book, strips the West of the privilege of being the centre of history: his Axial Age arises simultaneously in China, India, Iran, Israel and Greece, and he refuses any hierarchy among them. He wrote that in 1949, emerging from a country that had just believed itself the people chosen by history. His target is not Egypt: it is Hegel's Eurocentrism.

Still, the vigilance is sound. The rest of the dossier will show it: the Axial Age thesis itself can be twisted into a hierarchy between “awakened” peoples and supposedly “backward” ones. Jaspers refused that use. We must refuse it with him — and that is the subject of the critical section in the next chapter.

For me

What it changes

We live inside apparatuses: companies, administrations, platforms, schools, hospitals. This chapter invites a double gaze, and it is liberating. On one side, stop despising organisation — without it, no hospital, no school, no road, no transmission. Anti-institutional romanticism is a luxury of the well protected.

On the other, never forget what the apparatus cannot do: it cannot meet anyone. A file is not a patient, a profile is not a person, a grade is not a pupil. The practical question, in my job, tomorrow morning: at what moment am I serving the organisation — and at what moment am I meeting someone? Both are necessary. Confusing them is exactly what Jaspers calls the danger.

In the organisation where I work, where does the line fall between what the procedure should settle — and what requires me to be there, personally?

The great civilisations create lasting memory and the apparatus of power: they preserve man and enclose him at the same time.

Three questions to check

What do the first civilisations bring, around 3000 BC?

Writing (a memory outside bodies), the state, the city, administration, calculation, monuments, organised religion — the instruments we still live by.

Why does Jaspers say man remains “enveloped in myth” there?

Because the order of the world goes without saying: an enormous amount of thinking goes on, but not against the received order. The question “what if all this were false?” has nowhere yet to be asked.

What is the ambivalence of the first civilisations?

The same instruments preserve and dominate: writing enables law and record-keeping; the state protects and taxes. A civilisation is always a memory AND an apparatus.
