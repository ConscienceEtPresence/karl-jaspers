Dossier · At the Origins of Man and History · Chapter 1 of 10

Before history

What can we really know about human beings before writing?

The question

In thirty seconds

Prehistory covers almost all of our past — hundreds of thousands of years — and has left us nothing but mute objects. We know how those people knapped a stone; we shall never hear what they said to one another. It is not a “primitive” time: it is the vast dark ground out of which history arises late, almost yesterday.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Let us begin with a simple distinction. History, strictly speaking, begins with writing: some 5,000 years ago, in Mesopotamia and Egypt. Prehistory is all the rest — and “all the rest”, here, crushes the imagination: several hundred thousand years for our species alone, millions if we go back to the first hominins. Compress that span into a single twenty-four-hour day, and writing, the pyramids, Greece, the empires, the cathedrals, rockets and the internet all fit into the last few minutes before midnight.

Jaspers insists on this imbalance, and draws from it a conclusion that runs against all our reflexes: prehistory is not a failed antechamber to history, a long stammer waiting for things to begin. It is the ground. Almost everything that constitutes us was decided there — fire, speech, the tool, the family, mourning, storytelling — with no witness, no archive, not a single sentence preserved.

And this is where the question becomes philosophical. We know a great deal about these people: what they ate, how they knapped, where they migrated, sometimes what they died of. We know everything — except what interested them. No object will ever give us back their fear, their joy, their thoughts as they looked at their dead. The remains hand us the human being as object (measurable, datable) and refuse us the human being as existence (what they lived from within). This is the divide that runs through all of Jaspers's thought, and prehistory lets us touch it with our fingers.

His rule of conduct follows, and it is sharp: do not fill that silence with novels. Do not decree that these people were brutes without an inner life — nothing proves it. Nor decree that they were sages living in harmony with nature — nothing proves that either. The silence of prehistory is a fact to be borne, and it teaches us something about ourselves: what we project into that void says more about our age than about theirs.

The metaphor

To feel it

Prehistory is like a room plunged in darkness. On the floor we make out the objects left behind: a tool, a hearth, bones carefully arranged. We can measure those objects, date them, work out how they were made. But the voices of those who held them fell silent very long ago — and no lamp will ever bring them back.

A concrete situation

The burial

An archaeologist uncovers a 70,000-year-old burial: a body laid in a folded position, a few objects beside it, traces of ochre. Can they conclude that these humans believed in an afterlife?

Honestly: no. They can establish solid facts — the body was not abandoned, it was laid down; the gesture is intentional, repeated, costly in time. They can frame a serious hypothesis: something like care for the dead existed. But between “they cared for their dead” and “they believed in an afterlife” lies a gulf no instrument crosses. Perhaps it was a belief, perhaps grief, perhaps a social rule, perhaps all three. The gesture has reached us; its lived meaning will not.

This caution is not academic timidity. It is exactly what Jaspers asks of science: that it be rigorous and that it know its limit. A science that knows where it stops stays honest; a science that fills its gaps with stories becomes a mythology in a white coat.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“All very well, but with ancient DNA, imaging and dating, we shall end up knowing everything. The limit you describe is merely temporary.”

A serious objection, and Jaspers — a physician, trained in the sciences — does not brush it aside. It is true that we know more with every decade: ancient genomes have revealed unsuspected interbreeding; imaging techniques read gestures in a knapped stone. There is no reason for that progress to stop.

But it progresses in a single register: that of observable facts. The limit at issue, however, is not a limit of technical power — it is a limit of kind. No instrument, however refined, measures an experience lived from within: it measures its traces. Even for a living person, here and now: the world's best scanner shows your brain's activity while you listen to music — never what that music does to you. This is Jaspers's founding distinction, born of his practice as a psychiatrist: to explain (causes, mechanisms) is not to understand (lived meaning). The first progresses endlessly; the second requires an access which, for people dead 70,000 years ago, is closed for good.

For me

What it changes

This chapter trains us in something rare: bearing not to know. We hate a void — faced with silence, we immediately manufacture an explanation, and take it for reality. Prehistory is an excellent gymnasium for this discipline, because its resistance is absolute: it will never yield.

But the exercise is not only about fossils. What we do before a 70,000-year-old skull, we do every day before the living: we see traces — a behaviour, a word, a file, a profile — and at once we conclude to a being. “That's just how he is.” Yet the other, exactly like prehistoric man, always overflows what I observe of them. Prehistory is not merely a lesson in humility about the past: it is training in justness toward the living.

Someone close to me: what do I “know” about them that is, in truth, only a trace interpreted by me?

Three questions to check

What exactly separates prehistory from history?

Writing — and therefore the possibility of preserving speech, not merely objects. It is not a difference of intelligence, but of transmissible memory.

Can an archaeologist prove that a prehistoric human believed in an afterlife?

No. They can establish a gesture (a careful burial) and frame a hypothesis. The lived experience of those people cannot be deduced from any remains.

Why does Jaspers refuse to call prehistory a “primitive time”?

Because it is the ground of everything we are — speech, the tool, mourning took shape there. To judge it “primitive” is to project our age onto a silence.
