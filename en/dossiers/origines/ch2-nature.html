Man belongs to nature

Are we merely slightly cleverer animals?

The question

In thirty seconds

Yes, we are living beings among the living: the same chemistry, the same long kinship, the same mortal body. Jaspers disputes none of it — he was a physician. But he asks another question, one biology does not ask: how did this particular animal come to wonder what it is?

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

First, frankly and without reservation: man belongs to nature. He is a body, he gets hungry, he ages, he dies. He shares with other living beings a common history, a common chemistry, common needs. Any philosophy that begins by denying this begins with a lie, and Jaspers, trained as a physician, never felt the slightest temptation in that direction.

Second, the adjustment that makes this whole chapter. To acknowledge that man belongs to nature in no way obliges us to conclude that he is reducible to it. These are two different propositions, and the second does not follow from the first. Biology magnificently describes man as an organism; in doing so, it describes him as an object — that is its work, its rigour, its greatness. But an object does not ask questions about itself. The fact that we can study our own brain is already a fact the studied brain does not entirely explain.

Jaspers calls “scientific superstition” (Wissenschaftsaberglaube) that precise moment when a particular method, legitimate in its own order, proclaims itself the total explanation of man. Careful: he is not aiming at science — he is aiming at its exact opposite. Science says: “here is what my method reaches, and here is where it stops.” Scientific superstition says: “what my method does not reach does not exist.” The first sentence is rigour; the second is a metaphysical belief disguised as a result.

What science knows today — to be distinguished from Jaspers, who was writing in 1949: life on Earth is roughly 3.5 to 4 billion years old; all living beings descend from common ancestors — beware the frequent misreading: we do not descend from present-day bacteria or present-day apes, we share ancestors with them. Primates appear around 65 million years ago; the hominin lineage splits from the chimpanzee lineage 6 to 8 million years ago; Homo sapiens emerges in Africa around 300,000 years ago. Jaspers's data on these points are dated; his question is not — and that is why he is still read.

The metaphor

To feel it

An engineer can take a piano apart down to the last screw: he will know the wood, the tensions, the frequencies, everything. He will be right about all of it. He still will not know why this sonata makes someone in the audience weep. The piano belongs entirely to mechanics — and mechanics does not exhaust music.

A concrete situation

Four gazes on the same person

Take a person — say, you. A doctor knows things about you none of your friends do: your blood pressure, your test results, the state of your arteries. A psychologist knows something else: your mechanisms, your defences, your profile. A teacher, once upon a time, knew something else again: how you learn, where you get stuck, what you are good at. An algorithm, today, knows everything you click, at what hour, for how long.

Each of these knowledges is real and useful — do not consult a philosopher about chest pain. And yet add them all up: the total does not make you. The same thing is always missing — not one more datum that could be collected, but the standpoint from which all of it is lived. The doctor sees a body, the psychologist a psyche, the algorithm a profile; the one who inhabits all this appears on no screen.

A close friend, meanwhile, “knows” none of that — and yet knows something else, which cannot be measured: who you are when you are true. That knowledge is not obtained by observation; it is obtained through communication (that speech in which two people risk themselves). This is already all of Jaspers, in a kitchen conversation.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Isn't consciousness simply produced by the brain? Damage the brain and consciousness is damaged. End of discussion.”

This is the strongest objection, and Jaspers does not sidestep it — he knew the damaged brain better than most philosophers: he had seen it, in the clinic. Yes, consciousness depends on the brain; he never claimed otherwise. He defends no floating soul, no ghost in the machine.

But look at what the objection actually says. It establishes a dependence — and from there leaps to an identity: consciousness is “nothing but” that. That little “nothing but” is not a scientific result: it is a philosophical step, often taken without saying so. That my sight depends on my eyes does not mean that seeing a beloved face is retinal activity. To describe the mechanism is not to describe the experience — and experience, too, is a fact, the only one each of us knows from within.

Jaspers's position is therefore demanding on both sides: against spiritualism, he maintains that we are bodies; against reductionism, he maintains that knowledge about bodies does not exhaust what we are. An uncomfortable position, belonging to no camp — his, as always.

For me

What it changes

The stake is not theoretical for a single second. How we answer this question decides how we treat people. If a human being is nothing but their diagnosis, then a patient is a file, a pupil is a level, an employee is a performance, an old person is a burden. Everyone knows what it feels like to be treated that way — and everyone does it to others, sometimes without seeing it.

The young physician Jaspers drew from his clinic a conviction he turned into a philosophy: a patient is never reducible to their illness. You can apply that principle tomorrow morning, with no diploma whatsoever — speaking to someone as to a being who can still become, and not as to a case already filed. It is perhaps the most concrete gesture on this whole site.

On whom, this week, have I stuck a label — a diagnosis, a “that's just how he is” — that spared me from truly meeting them?

Check and connect

The sciences reach everything man is as an object; but what man authentically is begins precisely where that knowledge stops.
After Karl Jaspers, Philosophy (1932) and Way to Wisdom (1950) — paraphrase; see the original texts.

Three questions to check

Is Jaspers against science?

Not at all — he was a physician and the author of a General Psychopathology still in use. What he fights is “scientific superstition”: the slide from a particular method to a total explanation of man.

“Man descends from apes”: what needs correcting in that sentence?

We do not descend from present-day apes: we share common ancestors with them. The human lineage splits from the chimpanzee lineage 6 to 8 million years ago.

What is the logical flaw in “consciousness is NOTHING BUT the brain”?

The leap from dependence to identity. That consciousness depends on the brain is a fact; that lived experience is reducible to it is a philosophical thesis, not a laboratory result.
