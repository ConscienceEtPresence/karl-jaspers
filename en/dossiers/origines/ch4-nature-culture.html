Nature, culture and freedom

Are we merely the outcome of our biology?

The question

In thirty seconds

We inherit in two ways: through genes, and through culture. The first is not chosen; the second is learned, refused, transformed. And there is a third thing, explained by neither genes nor culture: the relation I maintain with what I do.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Let us distinguish cleanly, for the whole chapter rests on this distinction. Genetic transmission: slow, involuntary, inscribed in the body, it needs thousands of generations to change. Cultural transmission: fast, learned, carried by speech and example, it can swing in a single generation. A child born today has the body of a Palaeolithic hunter and the world of an engineer. Culture, not biology, made that difference.

But Jaspers does not stop there, for culture too can manufacture us without us — the age, the milieu, the language, the habits weigh immensely. One can be the product of one's culture as passively as one is the product of one's genes. Social determinism is no gentler than biological determinism; it is merely more flattering to those who have done well by it.

So where is freedom? It lies in a third term, very simple to state and very hard to reduce: the inner relation I maintain with my own act. An animal flees a danger. A human being, facing the same danger, may flee — but may also stay, protect someone, accept the risk. And above all, whatever they do, they can judge their own fear, recount it afterwards, feel ashamed or proud of it, decide that next time it will be different. This is no longer behaviour: it is what I make of my behaviour.

This is why Jaspers is never interested in the act alone. Two people perform exactly the same gesture — giving money, obeying an order, leaving someone — and it is not the same act, because the inner relation is not the same: one calculates, the other gives themselves; one submits, the other consents. No camera will see the difference. Yet it is the only thing that counts for what we become — this is precisely what he calls Existenz (what I can authentically be).

The metaphor

To feel it

Two musicians play the same score, the same notes, at the same tempo — a recording would make them nearly identical. One executes; the other says something. The notes belong to neither: they were written in advance, like our genes and our milieu. What belongs to the second as their own, no analysis of the sound will find.

A concrete situation

The same gesture, two acts

A colleague is humiliated in a meeting. Two people stay silent, in exactly the same way: same silence, same neutral face, same duration. A camera would see strictly no difference.

The first is silent out of calculation: they weighed the risk, one must not fall out with the boss, there will be time enough to speak when it is safe. The second is silent too — but knows, at that very second, that something has happened inside them: they were afraid, they see it, they tell themselves no story. They will go home with that silence on their heart, and it may be that next time, they speak.

Two identical behaviours; two different human beings. For the first, nothing happened — the calculation worked. In the second, something was seen, and what is seen can become a decision. There, in that invisible gap, lies the whole difference between undergoing your life and inhabiting it.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“That famous ‘inner relation’ is determined too: by upbringing, hormones, social context, the past. You move determinism one notch along; you do not abolish it.”

A formidable objection — the best that can be made, and Jaspers does not claim to refute it. He cannot: freedom is not demonstrable. He concedes this straight away, which is disarming. No experiment will ever prove that an act was free; causes will always be found afterwards — and the better we look, the more we shall find.

But turn the objection around: it is not demonstrable either. That everything is determined is a metaphysical belief, not a laboratory result — one cannot search the infinity of possible causes to verify that none is missing. So we stand before two wagers, neither of which can be proven. The difference lies elsewhere: one of them you cannot live. Try deliberating for a whole day while sincerely believing that your deliberation is already settled in advance — you will not manage it; at the moment of choosing, freedom is there, ineradicable.

This is what Jaspers means when he writes that freedom is not proven but attested: it is not an object to be found in the world, it is what shows itself in the act of choosing. The consistent determinist ought to stop deliberating; no one manages that, not even them.

For me

What it changes

This chapter offers a demanding consolation. The consolation: you are guilty neither of your genes, nor of your childhood, nor of your era. No one chooses their starting point, and much of what we call “our character” was handed to us without anyone asking our opinion.

The demand: there always remains the millimetre of play — what I make of what I was given. No one can do it in my place, and no one can take it from me. Jaspers proved it in his own life: he chose neither his illness, nor Nazism, nor the year of his birth. He chose what he would make of them. That is exactly where freedom begins — not in the absence of constraints, but in the relation I take up with them.

The thing I have always repeated by saying “that's just how I am”: where does it really come from — and what am I making of it?

Three questions to check

What is the difference between genetic and cultural transmission?

The first is slow, involuntary, inscribed in the body; the second is fast, learned, carried by speech — it can swing in a single generation.

Where exactly does freedom lie according to Jaspers?

Neither in genes nor in culture, but in the inner relation I maintain with my own act: judging my fear, owning it, deciding otherwise next time.

Why do two people making the same gesture not perform the same act?

Because the inner relation differs: one calculates, the other gives themselves. No camera sees that difference — and yet it is the only one that counts for what we become.
