The birth of history

Why do animals have no history?

The question

In thirty seconds

Events happen to all living things; that does not make a history. History requires remembering, transmitting, interpreting, and recognising oneself as both heir of what was and actor of what comes. Time passes for everyone. Man alone knows he comes from a past.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

A stubborn confusion must first be undone. “History” does not mean the mere fact that things happen. A forest has a chronology — fires, regrowth, droughts; a species has an evolution. But the forest does not remember its fire, and the species knows nothing of its evolution. Events occur; there is no history.

For history to exist, Jaspers lists precise conditions, and it is worth taking them one by one. A memory: something is retained. A transmission: what is retained passes to others who did not live it. An awareness of the past: I know that what is transmitted comes from a bygone time, different from mine. An interpretation: the transmitted past is not a stock of facts, it asks to be understood, judged, debated. An anticipation: I project myself into a future I may want to be otherwise. And finally, the synthesis of all this: the capacity to recognise myself as both heir — I did not begin the world — and actor — something depends on me.

This is why writing changes everything, without being the origin of consciousness. Before it, memory depended on the living: it died with them, warped, carried no further than a few generations. With it, a dead person can speak to someone not yet born. It is a dizzying threshold: humanity acquires a memory external to bodies.

And Jaspers adds a grave note, wholly his own: this historical consciousness is a burden as much as a power. To know that you come from a past is also to know that you will inherit faults you did not commit and debts you did not contract. To know that you are heading into a future is to bear responsibility for what you leave it. The animal is innocent — it has no history. We have both: memory and guilt, inheritance and responsibility. That is the exact price of our greatness.

The metaphor

To feel it

A river has flowed for thousands of years, but knows nothing of its source or of the sea it is heading for. We are the only place in the river where the water wonders where it came from — and where it would like to go.

A concrete situation

The photograph of a stranger

You open a box in an attic: the photograph of a man you do not know, in uniform, 1917. On the back, a first name, a date. He is your great-grandfather. He never saw you, you never saw him, and yet something tightens.

Look at what has just happened: a dead man, of whom you know nothing, has just altered your present. You discover yourself to be a link in a chain you did not begin. Perhaps you wonder what he lived through, what he did, what he would have thought of you. Perhaps, closing the box, you decide to pass something on to someone not yet born.

An animal facing that photograph would see a rectangle of paper. You see a past that commits you. This is exactly what Jaspers calls historical consciousness: not a knowledge of dates, but the lived experience of being situated — come from somewhere, going somewhere, responsible for both.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Elephants return to the bones of their dead. Crows pass on techniques. Some animal cultures last for generations. Where is the difference?”

The facts are real and command respect — since 1949 ethology has documented lasting cultural transmission in several species. It must be frankly admitted that the border is more porous than classical philosophy imagined.

But make the precise distinction. A crow transmits a technique: the next one performs the gesture, and performs it well. It does not transmit a narrative: no crow tells another what crows did a thousand years ago, nor argues about whether it was right. Animal transmission reproduces a behaviour; historical transmission hands over a past that can be interpreted — hence contested, hence taken up again. We do not merely receive gestures: we receive versions, and we must judge them.

Here again, Jaspers is not out to defend human pride. He simply notes what we are doing right now: reading a man who died in 1969, arguing with him, wondering whether he was right. No animal has this problem — and it is indeed a problem as much as a privilege.

For me

What it changes

Here is a very simple test for one's own life: am I living a chronology, or a history? A chronology is events following one another — weeks pass, things happen, you take the hits. A history is when what happened to me is taken up, understood, connected: when I know where I come from, what I received, what I am making of it, and where I want to go.

Many very busy lives are chronologies. They have no fewer events; they have less meaning, because nothing is taken up. Making a history of your life requires no particular talent: it requires memory — stopping now and then, looking at where you come from, deciding what you pass on. It is within everyone's reach, and almost no one does it.

What did I actually receive from those who came before me — and what will I deliberately pass on to those who come after?

Time passes for all living beings; but man knows that he comes from a past and moves toward a future: there he becomes historical.
After Karl Jaspers, The Origin and Goal of History (1949) — paraphrase; see the original text.

Three questions to check

Is a chronology of events enough to make a history?

No. It requires memory, transmission, awareness of the past, interpretation and anticipation — and the capacity to recognise oneself as both heir and actor.

Why does writing change everything?

It gives humanity a memory external to bodies: a dead person can speak to someone not yet born. Before that, memory died with the living.

According to Jaspers, is historical consciousness merely a privilege?

No: it is also a burden. To inherit is to inherit faults one did not commit; to look ahead is to answer for what one leaves. The animal is innocent.
