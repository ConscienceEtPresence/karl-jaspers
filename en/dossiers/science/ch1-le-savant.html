Science and technology · Chapter 1 of 9

A philosopher who came from science

Can you say what science cannot do, if you have never practised it?

The question

In thirty seconds

No — and Jaspers was convinced of it. Which makes his case rare: when he states the limits of science, he is not speaking from outside. He had spent twelve years inside, on a psychiatric ward, doing real research. His General Psychopathology, published in 1913 when he was twenty-nine, is still cited by psychiatrists today, more than a century later. This is not a philosopher who distrusts science. It is a scientist who discovered, while working, where knowledge stops.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Karl Jaspers begins as a physician. Not as a philosopher in disguise: by a considered choice. He wanted to know the human being, and he thought — rightly — that medicine would tell him more than an armchair and a shelf of books.

He enters the psychiatric clinic at Heidelberg in 1908 and stays until 1915. What he finds there is disorder: every school defends its doctrine, no one agrees on what a mental illness is, and what has been measured is cheerfully confused with what has been imagined. So he does something very simple and very rare: he sets out to put the methods in order. Not to find the right theory — to state, for each way of knowing, what it can and cannot do.

This is where the distinction that follows him all his life is born, the one that has its own page on this site: explaining and understanding. We explain a lesion, a cause, a mechanism — from outside, as a physicist explains a falling stone. We understand a grief, a jealousy, a fear — from inside, by living it again a little. Both are legitimate. Confusing them is an error. And above all: neither ever exhausts the person sitting in front of you.

This is why what he says about science is not a philosopher’s mood. It is a practitioner’s finding. He did not deduce the limits of knowledge: he ran into them, every day, for twelve years, in front of people who were suffering and whom his methods did not entirely reach.

The metaphor

To feel it

A man who has never held a saucepan can always say that cooking does not make you happy. He will be heard politely. But when the chef, after twenty years of service, puts down his knife and says the same sentence — it is no longer the same sentence.

A concrete situation

The patient who fits no category

Picture the young Jaspers, around thirty, facing a man no one knows what to say about. One professor declares: it is hereditary. Another: it is a childhood trauma. A third has an explanation contradicting the first two. Each is certain.

Jaspers watches and notices this: not one of the three realises he is not talking about the same thing as the others. The first is looking for a cause. The second is looking for a meaning. The third is mixing the two without knowing it. They are not even contradicting one another: they are not on the same ground.

And the man is still there. None of the three explanations says who he is. They say something true about him — and miss him.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“He is a philosopher: of course he defends his territory against science.”

That would be a solid objection against many people. Against him it collapses, for a factual reason: he did not leave science because it got in his way. He practised it to the end, and went on defending it afterwards.

You only have to look at what followed. All his life Jaspers demanded that philosophy match the sciences in rigour, and he had contempt for vague thinking that permits itself anything because it escapes verification. He goes so far as to say that someone who has never known the discipline of genuine research will philosophise badly — because they will not know what it means to prove, and therefore not what it means to be unable to prove.

The objection even reverses: it is because he holds science in high esteem that he refuses to let it be made to say what it never claimed. Confusing science with an answer to everything is not respect — it is loading it with a burden that ruins it.

For me

What it changes

There is a lesson here that is not only about Jaspers, and that is uncomfortable for everyone.

We often judge fields we have never practised. People criticise medicine without having treated anyone, teaching without having taught, policing without having patrolled, science without ever having had to defend a result in front of someone hunting for the flaw. That does not make our criticisms false. But it makes them light — and we should know it.

Jaspers does not say you must keep quiet until you have practised. He does something more demanding: he shows what a criticism looks like when it has paid its entry fee.

What do you speak about most confidently, without ever having done it with your own hands?

Three questions to check

Why does the fact that Jaspers was a physician change the weight of his critique?

Because he does not judge science from outside: he practised it for twelve years in a clinic. His critique is a practitioner’s finding, not a mood.

What distinction is born from his work in psychiatry?

Explaining (from outside: a cause, a mechanism) and understanding (from inside: a grief, a fear). Both are legitimate; confusing them is an error.

Is Jaspers an opponent of science?

No — the opposite. He demands the same rigour of philosophy, and refuses to burden science with a role it never claimed.
