Science and technology · Chapter 5 of 9

Finding your bearings in the world

What is knowledge for, if it decides nothing?

The question

In thirty seconds

For finding your bearings. Jaspers calls this world-orientation (Weltorientierung), and devotes the entire first volume of his Philosophy to it — before existence, before transcendence. The order matters: you do not begin with the soul, you begin by knowing where you are. But getting your bearings is not arriving. The map does not walk for you.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

You have to see how his great book is built to understand this chapter. Philosophy (1932) has three volumes, in this order: World-orientation, then Illumination of existence, then Metaphysics. Knowledge comes first. That is not accidental: it is a refusal.

A refusal of two easy paths. The one that would begin with inwardness, skipping the world — spirituality without reality. And the one that would stop at the world, believing there is nothing else — that was chapter 4.

What does world-orientation do? It gathers everything that can be known: physics, biology, history, psychology, sociology. It takes it seriously, entirely. And by taking it seriously it discovers something Jaspers calls the failure of orientation: knowledge, pushed to its end, never closes. It does not form a whole. The sciences do not converge on a single picture of the world; they remain many, and each runs into its own limit.

And here is the reversal: this failure is not a failure. It is through it that existence appears. As long as I believe the world will be fully explained, I have no reason to ask who I am — I am waiting for the answer from outside. The day I see that knowledge will not close, the question comes back to me. Volume II does not come after volume I: it comes out of it.

The metaphor

To feel it

A map, a compass, a weather forecast. Excellent, current, indispensable — setting out without them would be reckless. They tell you where you are, what is around you, what is coming. They will never tell you where you want to go. And you will have to walk.

A concrete situation

The move that never happens

Someone is thinking of moving abroad. So they gather information, and they are right to. Cost of living, job market, schools, climate, accounts from people who did it. Comparison tables. Forums. Two years pass.

They now know everything that can be known. And they have not gone.

It is not that a figure is missing. It is that no figure contains the decision. At some point they will have to say: I am going, or I am staying — and that moment will not feel like a conclusion, it will feel like a leap. The orientation was necessary. It has become a hiding place.

Jaspers sees this mechanism clearly: we gather information in order not to decide. Knowledge then serves the exact opposite of what it should serve.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Then we might as well decide at random, since knowledge does not decide?”

Absolutely not — and this is the exact misreading to be cleared away.

Jaspers does not say knowledge is useless for deciding. He says it does not replace deciding. Those are opposite claims. Deciding without having informed yourself is not freedom, it is frivolity — and Jaspers despises it as much as scientific superstition.

His position is demanding on both sides: you must know everything that can be known, to the end, honestly — and then decide knowing that knowledge is not enough. Whoever decides without knowing is irresponsible. Whoever waits for knowledge to decide is never responsible for anything.

That is why volume I exists, and why it comes first. Freedom does not begin where knowledge stops out of laziness. It begins where knowledge stops after giving everything.

For me

What it changes

There is a way of gathering information that is work, and a way that is flight. From outside they look very alike — and often from inside too.

The test this chapter suggests is simple and uncomfortable: ask yourself exactly what you are missing in order to decide. If you can name it — a figure, a fact, an answer from someone — go and find it, that is legitimate. If you cannot name it, then it is not information you lack. You already know, and you are waiting to be excused from choosing.

What have you been researching for months without being able to say exactly what you are missing?

Three questions to check

Why is world-orientation the first volume of Philosophy?

Because you do not begin with inwardness. You must first know everything that can be known — spirituality without reality is refused.

What is the failure of orientation?

Knowledge, pushed to its end, never closes into a whole. And that failure is not one: it is through it that the question “who am I” comes back to me.

Should we then decide without informing ourselves?

No, the opposite. You must know everything that can be known, then decide knowing that knowledge is not enough. Deciding without knowing is frivolity.
