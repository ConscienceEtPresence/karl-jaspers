Science and technology · Chapter 7 of 9

The apparatus and the mass

Why is no one responsible, when everyone takes part?

The question

In thirty seconds

Because between us and our acts has slipped what Jaspers calls the apparatus (Apparat): the immense organising machine that produces, administers, distributes. It works. It has no centre. Each of us holds a function in it, and a function is by definition replaceable — so each of us is replaceable. The result: a world no one willed as it is, and of which no one feels the author.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

This chapter comes from Man in the Modern Age (1931), the book that made Jaspers famous — one of the few philosophy books of its time to sell like a novel. In it he describes what he sees around him, and what he sees has not eased.

The apparatus has a strange property: it is made entirely of human beings, and it behaves like none of them. Each person, taken alone, is reasonable, honest, tired, well-meaning. The whole produces results that no one, individually, would have chosen — and each can say in complete sincerity: it is not me, I am only applying the rules.

What Jaspers points to is not a conspiracy. It is the opposite: there is nobody at the controls. That is far more disturbing than a plot, because a plot would at least have someone responsible whom you could confront.

Then comes the mass — and the contempt one hears in that word must be cleared away at once. Jaspers is not insulting ordinary people. This is not about vulgar crowds against a refined elite: that contempt he fought, and paid dearly for, having refused to the end the doctrine that divided human beings by nature. The mass, in him, is not a social group. It is a state — the one where we are many and no one is anyone any more. A state into which a university professor falls as easily as a labourer.

In that state we no longer think: we relay. We no longer want: we follow. And you have to see why it works — it is not stupidity. It is relief. Being someone is expensive: you have to decide, be wrong, answer for yourself. Being a function costs nothing. The mass is not a fall you suffer: it is a comfortable seat you take.

The metaphor

To feel it

A station at rush hour. Thousands of people each going somewhere, for reasons of their own. Seen from above, the crowd traces a regular, almost liquid movement that none of them willed and all of them produce. No one is steering. No one is free to stop in the middle.

A concrete situation

The refusal you can address to nobody

You contest a decision: a rejected file, a refused entitlement, an obvious error. You call. The person on the line is pleasant, and right to be — there is nothing they can do. They apply a rule they did not write. They pass you to a department that applies a procedure it did not decide. Somewhere there was a trade-off, years ago, made by people who have left.

You are right. Everyone is polite. Nothing moves. And above all: there is no one to blame — which makes it unbearable, because even the anger has no address.

You have just met the apparatus. It is not a wall: it is an endless corridor where every door opens onto another door, and where everyone is acting in good faith.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“So what? This organisation feeds us, treats us and transports us. Shall we go back to the village?”

No. Jaspers is categorical here, and it is one of the things that separate him from romantic critics of modernity: the apparatus is indispensable. Without it, eight billion of us would starve. The dream of return is a comfortable lie, and he refuses to sell it.

His point is not to abolish the apparatus. It is to refuse to disappear into it. Those are different things, and there is no contradiction in holding a function without taking yourself for it.

So what remains? Little, and he does not pretend otherwise — he has no plan. He has a foothold: within the apparatus there are always moments when someone really decides, and it shows. The official who takes it upon himself to look at the file. The doctor who gives the five minutes that are not scheduled. The engineer who refuses to sign. None of that changes the system. But in those moments someone exists — and Jaspers knows no other place where freedom shows itself.

For me

What it changes

There is a sentence we all say, and Jaspers makes it the central symptom: “there is nothing I can do”.

It is often true. That is what makes it dangerous. It is true ninety per cent of the time — and we stretch it over the remaining ten without noticing. And that ten per cent is exactly where we are someone.

So the question is not “are you responsible for the system” — you are not. It is: within your function, where is there still a margin, and what do you do with it? It is small. It is all there is.

In what you do every day, what is your real margin — and when did you last use it?

In his words · Quiz · To go on

Check and connect

In the apparatus each becomes a function, and a function is replaceable; which is why the human being disappears there without anyone having killed him.

After Man in the Modern Age (1931) — a reformulation, not a literal quotation.

Three questions to check

What is the apparatus?

The organising machine that produces and administers. It is made of human beings and behaves like none of them: it has no centre, and nobody is at the controls.

What does “the mass” mean in Jaspers?

Not a social group, and no contempt for ordinary people: a state where we are many and no one is anyone. A professor falls into it as easily as a labourer.

Should the apparatus be abolished?

No — it is indispensable, and the dream of return is a lie. The point is not to disappear into it: to hold a function without taking yourself for it.
