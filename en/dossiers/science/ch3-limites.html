Science and technology · Chapter 3 of 9

Where science stops

Why does science not answer the questions that matter most?

The question

In thirty seconds

Because it is not built for that, and never claimed to be. Science establishes what is. It never says what is worth anything. No experiment, no dataset, no model will say whether I should stay with this person, whether my life has meaning, whether what I am doing is right. This is not a temporary gap that progress will fill. It is structural: those questions are not its business.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Jaspers states three limits, and it must be seen that none of them is a reproach. They are defining features. Blaming science for not telling us the good is like blaming a thermometer for not weighing anything.

First limit: science knows only objects. It can study only what it can put in front of itself, isolate, measure. But I am never entirely an object — not even to myself. I can study my brain, my reflexes, my social background. The one doing the studying always escapes the study. This is what the dossier on the Encompassing calls the subject-object split.

Second limit: science never says what is worth anything. It can tell me what a bomb does to a body. It will never tell me whether to drop it. It can describe ten thousand couples and their breakup statistics. It will not tell me whether I should leave. The moment of decision is exactly the moment knowledge falls silent.

Third limit: science cannot found itself. Why seek truth? Why not fake the data when it suits? Science cannot answer: its answer would already presuppose that we care about truth. That decision is made before any science, and it is not scientific.

This last point is the deepest, and the dossier returns to it in the final chapter: science rests on a moral choice it is incapable of justifying.

The metaphor

To feel it

A torch in a dark room. The beam is sharp, reliable, and it does not lie. But it does not say where to go. And there is one thing it will never light, however powerful it is: the hand holding it.

A concrete situation

The exact diagnosis and the question that remains

A doctor gives a prognosis. The figures are good — meaning accurate. Life expectancy, success rates, effects of treatment. Nothing there is disputable, and it is precious: without those figures, we would decide blind.

Then the patient asks his question. Not “what are my chances” — he has just heard that. He asks: is it worth it? Should he keep fighting, or stop and live the remaining months differently?

The doctor can offer a view. But if he is honest, he knows that view is no longer medicine. No study in the world contains the answer, because the answer depends on who this man is, what he loves, what he can bear, what he wants to be to the end. That is exactly where science stops, and where someone has to decide.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Hold on: neuroscience will eventually explain decision, love, consciousness.”

Perhaps. And it would change nothing, for a reason that is not a bet on the future but a point of logic.

Suppose the cerebral mechanism of your decision were entirely explained. You would have a description of what happened. You would still have no answer to “what should I do tomorrow?” — because that question is not asking how a brain works, it is asking what is good. No quantity of description ever turns into prescription. Getting from “here is what is” to “here is what ought to be” is a leap, and knowledge does not make it.

So Jaspers is not saying neuroscience will fail. He is saying that even if it fully succeeded, the question would remain untouched. This is not a limit of power, it is a limit of kind.

And the objection deserves fairness: it is right to refuse any decree, made in advance, about what science will never manage. Nobody knows what it will explain. That is not where the question lies.

For me

What it changes

There is a practical consequence, and it liberates rather than frightens.

If you are waiting for some knowledge to tell you what to do with your life, you will wait forever. It is not that you have not read enough, thought enough, not yet found the right book or the right expert. It is that the answer is nowhere to be found. It is to be decided.

Many people spend years gathering information before choosing — and mistake their hesitation for seriousness. Jaspers would say: you are confusing two things. Informing yourself is necessary, and should be done thoroughly. But no amount of information will decide in your place, and at some point it will have to be you.

Which decision are you postponing by telling yourself you do not yet have enough information?

Three questions to check

What are the three limits of science according to Jaspers?

It knows only objects (the subject escapes it); it says what is, never what is worth anything; it cannot itself ground its own choice of truth.

Are these limits reproaches?

No — they are defining features. Blaming science for not telling us the good is like blaming a thermometer for not weighing anything.

If neuroscience explained everything, would the question be settled?

No. You would have a complete description of what happens, and still no answer to “what should I do”. No description ever becomes a prescription.
