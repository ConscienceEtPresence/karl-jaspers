Science and technology · Chapter 8 of 9

The physician in the technological age

What is lost when a patient becomes a case?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers wrote about this from experience: he had been the physician. His thesis is double, and refuses both camps. Technological medicine treats infinitely better than before — denying that would be criminal. And it has lost something that also healed: the meeting of two people, one of whom is not merely a failing organism. This is not a spiritual garnish. It is, literally, part of the treatment.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

This chapter condenses the whole dossier into a single profession — and it is Jaspers’s own. You see at once what science can do, what it cannot, and what happens when that is forgotten.

The physician, says Jaspers, is caught in a contradiction nothing resolves. To treat, he must objectify: see a liver, a level, an image, a protocol. If he does not, he kills. Scientific rigour is not optional, it is the first duty.

But in front of him there is no liver. There is someone who is frightened, who has a history, who has reasons to live — and no examination yields that person. So the physician must do two incompatible things at once: treat an object, and meet someone. Jaspers proposes no balance between them. He says both must be held, entirely, and that it tears you.

What he sees coming is the dissolution of that tearing — not from malice, but through the apparatus of the previous chapter. The physician becomes a technician in a chain. Time is counted, the act is coded, responsibility is spread across eight specialists none of whom sees the whole person. Each does the job well. No one meets the patient. And when that happens, there is nobody to blame: it is normal functioning.

Hence Jaspers’s harshest phrase: the patient treated as a case is left alone at the moment he already is. Technology saved him and left him alone. This is not a lack of kindness. It is what happens when a method made for objects is applied to someone.

The metaphor

To feel it

An excellent mechanic repairs the car. That is all anyone asks of him, and the car has nothing to say about it. The day the car is frightened of the diagnosis, wonders whether it still wants to drive, and waits to be looked at — the best mechanic in the world is no longer enough.

A concrete situation

The four minutes

The doctor is competent. He has read the file, seen the results, the treatment is the right one — genuinely the right one, the one no medicine of the last century could have given. He explains clearly. He answers questions. Nothing is reproachable.

And the patient leaves with the sense of not having been seen. He could not say what was missing. He has nothing to complain of — the doctor was not cold, he was efficient. Simply, at no point were there two people in the room.

The patient goes home with the right treatment, and with his fear intact. The treatment will work. Nobody has touched the fear. And when it has to be decided — continue, stop, accept — he will be alone with it.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Would you have us go back to the kindly family doctor who let people die?”

No, and Jaspers would be the fiercest against that nostalgia. He saw medicine before antibiotics. He knows what kindness was worth at the bedside of a child who could not be saved. No human warmth replaces a molecule that works, and pretending otherwise insults the dead.

So he asks for nothing against technology. His position is harder to hold than either of today’s warring camps: technical competence is the physician’s first duty, non-negotiable — and it is not enough. Both. Entirely. At the same time.

One must add what he did not say, and honesty requires it: today’s physician usually does not have that time. He is no less human than yesterday’s; he is caught in the apparatus. The reproach does not go to him. It goes to an organisation that has made the meeting optional, and therefore, in practice, impossible.

For me

What it changes

This chapter reaches far beyond medicine, which is why it nearly closes the dossier.

The same contradiction runs through every profession where you deal with someone. The teacher must assess a piece of work — and there is a pupil in front of him. The social worker must complete a file — and there is a family. The judge must apply a law — and there is a man. Every time: the method is necessary, and it does not see the person.

Jaspers does not say choose. He says that the day you stop feeling the tear, you have not resolved it — you have simply stopped seeing anyone.

In your work, when do you treat a case when there is someone there?

Check and connect

The physician must treat an organism and meet a human being; he can give up neither, and no technique will ever excuse him from the second.

After The Physician in the Technological Age (1958) — a reformulation, not a literal quotation.

Three questions to check

What is the physician’s contradiction according to Jaspers?

He must objectify to treat — otherwise he kills — and meet someone no examination yields. Two incompatible demands, to be held entirely, at the same time.

Does Jaspers miss the medicine of the past?

No, and he would be fierce against that nostalgia: he watched children die whom no one knew how to save. Technical competence is the first duty, non-negotiable.

Why does this chapter reach beyond medicine?

Because the same tear runs through every profession that deals with someone: teacher, social worker, judge. The method is necessary and does not see the person.
