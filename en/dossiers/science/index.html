Dossier · nine chapters

Science and technology

What knowledge can do, what it never will — from someone who practised it

There are two ways of being wrong about science, and Jaspers refuses both. The first is believing it will eventually explain everything, including what we are and what we should do. The second is distrusting it, setting intuition, feeling or ancient wisdom against it. Jaspers holds the harder position: science is indispensable, and it is insufficient. Both, entirely, at the same time.

This dossier matters, because it is where almost everyone locks themselves in today — on one side those for whom nothing counts unless it is proven, on the other those for whom proof is already a kind of violence. Jaspers has one feature that earns him the right to speak: he did not come to science through reading. He spent twelve years in it, in a white coat, on a psychiatric ward.

The dossier runs from his own scientific practice (chapter 1) to the reversal that always surprises: in Jaspers, science is not the adversary of philosophy — it is its school, because it teaches honesty (chapter 9).
