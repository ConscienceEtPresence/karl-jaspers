Science and technology · Chapter 9 of 9

Science as a demand for probity

What if science were a school of morality?

The question

In thirty seconds

Here is the reversal no one expects after eight chapters of limits. For Jaspers, science is not the enemy of philosophy: it is its condition. Because it teaches a virtue no wisdom teaches as well — probity (Redlichkeit): not calling knowledge what you believe, not paying yourself in words, accepting that you are wrong. Without that discipline, philosophy becomes chatter.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Reread the dossier from the start and you will see this chapter was contained in it. We have said what science can do (2), what it cannot (3), what happens when they are confused (4), what it is for (5), what technology does (6, 7, 8). What remains is the question chapter 3 left open: why seek truth?

Science cannot answer. Nothing in a method obliges anyone not to fake the data. The choice of truth is made before any science, and it is not scientific. It is a moral decision — the first one.

And this is where Jaspers turns everything around. Science does not found that choice — but it exercises it, daily, better than any institution. The researcher learns things few people learn elsewhere: that a conviction is worth nothing without proof; that the result which suits you is precisely the one to distrust; that you must publish the method so others can demolish it; that being wrong in public is not a disgrace, it is the job.

It is a school of honesty of a harshness ordinary morality does not reach. You can be virtuous all your life without ever having had to admit publicly that you were wrong.

Hence Jaspers’s position, which always surprises: whoever has never known the discipline of genuine research will philosophise badly. Not for lack of knowledge — because they will not know what proving means, and therefore not what being unable to prove means. They will take their conviction for knowledge. This is exactly chapter 4, reversed: the superstitious believer in science and the vague mystic share one defect — neither has ever been contradicted by a fact.

And the circle closes. Philosophy does not begin against the sciences. It begins where the sciences, honestly conducted to the end, meet their own limit — and where someone must decide anyway. Without science, it would be laziness dressed as depth. Without philosophy, science would not even know why it is searching.

The metaphor

To feel it

A tightrope walker learns balance on a wire strung a metre above the ground. The wire is not the point; it leads nowhere. But someone who has never climbed onto it will never know what falling is — and on the day he must walk on something that matters, he will have only his self-confidence, which has never weighed anything.

A concrete situation

The result that does not say what you hoped

Three years of work. The hypothesis is beautiful, it holds, you believe in it. And the data say no. Not quite no — enough that by adjusting the cut-offs a little, setting aside two awkward measurements, choosing the other statistical test, it would pass. Nobody would see anything. It would even be defensible.

There is a moment here that Jaspers holds to be properly philosophical, and it looks like nothing spectacular: someone, alone at a screen, decides not to do it. Nothing obliges him. No method imposes it — the method is precisely what he is choosing to respect.

That instant is not science. No data produced it. It is one person’s decision about what he agrees to be. And without thousands of decisions like it, taken by strangers, there would be no science at all.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“So science is moral? It produced poison gas and the bomb.”

That is the decisive objection, and it is entirely right — the next dossier is devoted to it, because Jaspers wrote a whole book on the bomb.

So we must be precise, on pain of lying. The probity science teaches is a virtue of knowledge: it makes you honest about what is true. It does not make you good. A researcher can be exemplary in rigour and work on a weapon. There is no contradiction — and that is precisely what is terrifying.

So Jaspers does not say science is moral. He says it exercises a virtue that morality presupposes without knowing how to teach it: the refusal to lie to oneself. It is one piece, not the whole. Another is missing, which science will never supply — knowing what you are searching for. The honest scientist who does not ask that question is exactly the man of chapter 7: an irreproachable function in an aimless apparatus.

Which is why the dossier does not stop here, and why the next one is called Politics — written after the book Jaspers devoted to the bomb, whose subtitle names the real subject: political consciousness in our time.

For me

What it changes

Probity, as Jaspers means it, is not a scholar’s virtue. It is a daily demand, and rarer than it looks.

It comes down to one question, which must be asked without cheating: do I know this, or do I believe it? It does not ask you to believe nothing — Jaspers believed a great deal. It asks you not to dress a belief as knowledge, because that is where all violence begins: whoever knows has nothing left to discuss with you.

And that is the last word of this dossier. Science will never tell you what to do with your life. But it will have taught you not to tell yourself stories about what you know — and without that, no decision is worth anything, because it rests on air.

Take the conviction that matters most to you. Do you know it, or believe it? And would anything change if you said so honestly?

Philosophy does not begin against the sciences, but where the sciences, honestly conducted, meet their limit; whoever has never known their discipline will not tell what he knows from what he believes.

After Way to Wisdom (1950) and Philosophical Autobiography (1953) — a reformulation, not a literal quotation. ✉️ Contact

Three questions to check

What is the reversal in this chapter?

Science is not philosophy’s adversary but its condition: it exercises probity — not calling knowledge what you believe, accepting that you are wrong.

Does science make you good?

No. It makes you honest about what is true, which is another matter. A researcher of exemplary rigour can work on a weapon — and that is what is terrifying.

Why does philosophy need science?

Without it, it would not know what proving means, therefore not what being unable to prove means: it would take its convictions for knowledge.
