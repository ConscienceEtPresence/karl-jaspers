Science and technology · Chapter 6 of 9

Technology is neither good nor bad

Is the tool innocent?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers says two things that seem to contradict each other, and must be held together. Technology is a means: it wants nothing, decides nothing, everything depends on use — so it is neither good nor bad. And it is never neutral: it does not merely carry out what we wanted, it changes what we want, and what we are. Both are true. This is what “it all depends how you use it” always forgets.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

There is nothing nostalgic about Jaspers. He does not dream of a world before machines; he finds that daydream cowardly and false: technology has torn millions of people away from famine, from pain, from death in childbirth. No serious person wants to go back.

But he also refuses the comfortable sentence — “technology is neutral, it all depends what you do with it”. It is comfortable because it leaves us intact. And we do not stay intact.

His observation is this: technology does not merely serve ends, it transforms them. A tool is invented for a need; once there, the tool creates needs that did not exist, makes obligatory what was optional, and makes unthinkable what went without saying. That is not misuse: it is normal functioning.

A second, graver observation: technology tends to turn everything into an available object. What is technical is what can be measured, stored, reproduced, replaced. Extended to everything, this logic treats human beings like the rest: a resource, a profile, a case, a flow. Not out of malice — out of slope. And this is exactly the subject-object split of the Encompassing dossier, become an organisation of the world.

So what is to be done? Jaspers gives no recipe, and this must be said honestly: he has no solution. He has a demand — that technology remain a means, which presupposes that someone, somewhere, still knows for what end. And the end, as chapter 3 showed, is deduced from no knowledge.

The metaphor

To feel it

A hammer wants nothing. But give someone a hammer for long enough, and the world around them will gently begin to look like nails. The tool did nothing. It only made one thing very easy — and everything else slightly less obvious.

A concrete situation

The phone on the table

Nobody decided that we would be permanently reachable. There was no vote, no decree. A tool appeared, very useful — and it is. Then answering became normal. Then not answering became rude. Then being unreachable became suspicious. Then it became necessary, for work, for a medical appointment, to exist administratively.

At no point did anyone want this outcome. Everyone made only reasonable choices, one at a time. And nobody today can go back alone: whoever tries does not recover the old freedom, they simply become someone who is missing.

This is what Jaspers wants us to see. The tool was never bad at any point. It merely made a behaviour so easy that it became the norm, and then the norm became a constraint. Saying “it all depends how you use it” describes nothing of what actually happened.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“This is a man from 1931 talking about steam engines. What can he say about our technologies?”

The objection is serious, and must be granted what is right in it: Jaspers predicted nothing. He knew neither networks, nor algorithms, nor language models. Claiming he announced them would be an abuse — the kind of false prophecy this site refuses.

But his analysis was not about a machine. It was about a relation: what happens when a logic of means extends into domains that are not its business. That relation does not depend on the technology of the day.

And there is a fact anyone can check without straining him: what he describes — permanent availability, human beings treated as profiles, the end forgotten behind the means — has intensified, not eased. Not because he foresaw it, but because he was looking at a mechanism rather than a device.

Which leaves the work on our own tools to us. He will not do it for us, and he would be the first to say so.

For me

What it changes

The useful question is not “is this tool good or bad”. That leads nowhere, and it leaves us spectators.

Jaspers’s question is different: what does this tool make easy for me, and what does it quietly make a little harder? A tool never forbids you anything. It makes certain things so simple that the others become costly — and after a few years you no longer do them, without ever having decided to stop.

That is where the tool stops being a means, and nobody notices at the moment it happens.

What have you stopped doing, without ever having decided to stop?

In his words · Quiz · To go on

Check and connect

Technology is a means; it demands to be guided by an end it cannot supply. Left to itself it does not become evil — it becomes aimless, which is worse.

After Man in the Modern Age (1931) — a reformulation, not a literal quotation.

Three questions to check

Why do “neither good nor bad” and “never neutral” not contradict each other?

A means wants nothing: so it is neither good nor bad. But it transforms whoever uses it and the ends they pursue: so it is not neutral. Both are true.

What is technology’s own slope according to Jaspers?

To turn everything into an available object — measurable, storable, replaceable. Extended to human beings, it treats them as resources, not from malice but from slope.

Did Jaspers foresee our current technologies?

No, and claiming so would be an abuse. He was analysing a relation — the spread of the logic of means — not a device; which is why his analysis outlives his machines.
