Science and technology · Chapter 4 of 9

The superstition of science

Can you believe in science the way you believe in a religion?

The question

In thirty seconds

Yes, and Jaspers has a word for it: the superstition of science (Wissenschaftsaberglaube). It consists in expecting from knowledge what it cannot give — an answer to everything, an authority that settles matters, a guarantee. The paradox is complete: it is by overrating science that you stop understanding it. The superstitious believer in science has never practised it. He has made a god of it, and a god cannot be refuted.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

This chapter is the hinge of the dossier. So far we have said what science can do (chapter 2) and what it cannot (chapter 3). What remains is what happens when the two are confused.

Jaspers carefully distinguishes science from belief in science. The first is a method that knows its limits and states them. The second is a faith — and a faith unaware of itself, which is the worst kind.

The unmistakable sign: the real scientist knows what he is not talking about. A serious physicist will not tell you whether to divorce. A serious biologist will not deduce a morality from evolution. They know where their competence ends, because they know the method from inside, with its conditions and its margins of error.

The superstitious believer does not know those margins. He has not seen the workshop. He has seen only the result, in an article or a video, presented without its conditions. So he invokes “science” as others invoke a scripture: to close the discussion, not to open it. “It’s proven” replaces “here is the method, check it”.

And Jaspers adds the harshest stroke: this superstition abandons the person who holds it. On the day something really has to be decided — an illness, a death, a choice — he will have nothing. He will have learned to believe nothing unproven, at a moment when nothing that matters can be proven. He will face the question without even the words to ask it.

The metaphor

To feel it

A man who trusts only his scales. They are excellent, accurate to the gram. He carries them everywhere. Then he is asked whether the painting in front of him is beautiful. He puts it on the scales and announces: two point three kilos. He has not lied. He has not answered.

A concrete situation

The very rational colleague

She says she believes nothing without proof. She says it with pride, and it is not ridiculous: she is right to distrust nonsense.

But listen longer. She is convinced life has no meaning — that is not a scientific result, it is a metaphysical thesis, and unverifiable. She is convinced that human beings are fundamentally selfish — that is an anthropology, inherited, disputed. She is convinced one must be useful — that is a morality, and no one has demonstrated it.

None of these three convictions is proven. All three are beliefs, received somewhere, at some point, from someone. She takes them for self-evident because they are wearing the costume of science.

She has no less faith than her religious neighbour. She has the same quantity of faith, plus one advantage: she does not know she is a believer, so she has never had to examine what she believes. This is exactly what Jaspers calls a Gehäuse — a shell you are warm inside, whose walls you cannot see because you take them for the world.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“You are saying science is just another belief. That is the argument of every charlatan.”

No — and this must be sharp, because it is the point at which Jaspers is most often betrayed, including by people who claim him.

Science is not just another belief. It is verifiable, refutable, universal; it corrects itself; it can state its conditions. An opinion, an intuition, a revelation do none of that. Putting the two on the same level is exactly the charlatan’s lie, and Jaspers fights it as hard as he fights the opposite superstition.

What he says is far more precise, and far more awkward: it is not science that is a belief, it is the conviction that science answers everything. That conviction, precisely, is not scientific. No experiment demonstrates it. It is adopted, like a faith. The paradox closes: the superstitious believer in science holds a position that fails his own standards.

In other words, Jaspers is not attacking science in the name of something vaguer. He is attacking it in the name of itself — and of its rigour.

For me

What it changes

The useful thing here is not spotting the superstition in other people. That is an easy game and it leads nowhere.

The real question is this: what do I hold to be obvious without ever having examined it? We all carry inherited convictions that pass themselves off as observations. “You have to succeed.” “People are self-interested.” “Life has no meaning.” “Life has a meaning.” None is proven. All function as facts.

Jaspers does not ask you to give them up. He asks you to know them — to know that you believe, and what. That is all he means by probity: not believing nothing, but not calling knowledge what you believe.

Name one thing you hold to be obvious and have never checked. Where did it come from?

Check and connect

The superstition of science is not an excess of science: it is its absence. Whoever expects knowledge to tell him what he ought to want has understood neither what knowledge is, nor what wanting is.

After Way to Wisdom (1950) — a reformulation, not a literal quotation. ✉️ Contact

Three questions to check

What is the superstition of science?

Expecting from knowledge what it cannot give: an answer to everything. It is a faith unaware of itself — and it is not scientific, since no experiment demonstrates it.

Does Jaspers say science is just another belief?

No, and this is the commonest misreading. Science is verifiable, refutable, correctable. What is a belief is the conviction that it answers everything.

Why does the superstition of science abandon whoever holds it?

Because at the moment of deciding — illness, grief, choice — nothing that matters can be proven. Having learned to believe nothing without proof, he is left without words.
