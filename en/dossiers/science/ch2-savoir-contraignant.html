Science and technology · Chapter 2 of 9

What science really can do

What does science give us that nothing else gives?

The question

In thirty seconds

One thing, and it is immense: knowledge that compels. If I measure correctly, I am obliged to accept the result — whether it suits me or not, whether I am a believer or an atheist, Chinese or Norwegian, of the right or the left. Jaspers calls this compelling knowledge (zwingendes Wissen). It is the only ground on which the whole of humanity can agree without fighting. No tradition, no wisdom, no revelation ever managed that.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Jaspers is often read as a critic of science. That is a misreading, and this chapter exists to dispel it before all the others. Before saying where knowledge stops, you must measure what it achieves — otherwise what follows is merely resentment.

Scientific knowledge has three features that Jaspers lists with admiration.

It compels. It is not an opinion you adopt: it is a conclusion you are forced into. Faced with a valid demonstration, I have no choice. My freedom can do nothing against it, and that is exactly right.

It is universal. It holds for every mind, everywhere, independently of culture, faith or temperament. Two people who agree on nothing — not on God, not on justice, not on beauty — will agree on the boiling point of water. Take the measure of what that represents.

It is methodical, and it knows its own limits. A genuine science knows what it is talking about and what it is not. It states its conditions. It can be refuted. A thought that can never be contradicted by anything is not stronger than a science: it is weaker, because it risks nothing.

Jaspers adds a remark that stings: most of those who invoke “science” have never practised it and do not know that discipline. They have made it an authority — which is precisely what it is not.

The metaphor

To feel it

A standard metre. It does not say what should be built, nor whether the house will be beautiful, nor whether anyone will be happy in it. It says one thing, and it says it to everyone the same way, caring nothing for who holds the ruler. It is little. It is irreplaceable.

A concrete situation

The argument that stops dead

A dinner table. People are arguing about a vaccine, a climate, a figure. Voices rise. Everyone has their camp, their newspaper, their intuition.

Then someone produces a verifiable datum — a measurement, a protocol, a number that can be checked. And something remarkable happens: the argument does not get more violent. It changes in kind. It can now be checked. Someone can be wrong, and know it.

That moment is compelling knowledge. It does not end the disagreement — on what should be done people will go on opposing each other, and rightly. But it takes one patch of ground away from sheer force. In a world settled entirely by intuition and conviction, only the strongest would be right.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Science is wrong all the time. Yesterday butter was bad for you, today it is sugar.”

True — and this is precisely the most misunderstood argument of all.

Science corrects itself. That is what distinguishes it from everything else, and it is its strength, not its weakness. A doctrine that has never been wrong in two thousand years has not been more accurate: it has simply never risked anything verifiable.

Two things must be distinguished that are always mixed up. Established knowledge — that water boils at one hundred degrees under a given pressure — does not move. And research in progress — on nutrition, on the brain — hesitates, contradicts itself, advances. The newspaper presents the second as if it were the first. It is the newspaper that lies, not science.

And Jaspers would add this: what is to be feared is not that science corrects itself. It is that it should stop doing so, because a power, a funding body or a fashion prevents it.

For me

What it changes

Hold on to this first, because the rest of the dossier will qualify things and everything risks getting mixed up: nothing that follows takes anything away from this chapter.

There is in our lives one very small domain where you can be right or wrong for real, where no one can decree the contrary by authority alone. That is infinitely precious, and fragile. Every time someone says “that’s my opinion” about a measurable fact, they eat away at that ground.

The real trouble will begin further on — when we believe that this ground is the only one there is.

When did you last change your mind because a fact forced you to?

Three questions to check

What is compelling knowledge?

Knowledge you are forced to accept: if the method is correct, I must admit the result, whatever my culture, faith or preferences.

Why does Jaspers admire this knowledge rather than belittle it?

Because it is the only ground on which the whole of humanity can agree without a contest of force. No tradition had achieved that.

“Science is wrong all the time” — what is the answer?

That it corrects itself, which distinguishes it from any doctrine. And that established knowledge must be distinguished from research in progress, which the media confuse.
