Politics · Chapter 9 of 9

The turn cannot be planned

How does a book end that announced it would have no solution?

The question

In thirty seconds

On what Jaspers calls the turn (die Umkehr): what man has produced, man must master — and that requires a change in the way of thinking. Then he immediately adds the sentence that forbids making a programme of it: the turn cannot be planned. It cannot be decreed, organised or taught. It begins in an individual, or it does not begin.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

First, what this chapter will not do, because the whole book leads here: there is no solution. Jaspers announced it on page 24, and he keeps his word to the end. Five hundred pages, and no plan. Whoever closes the book looking for what to do on Monday has found nothing.

What he offers is something else: what is produced by man must also be mastered by man. Not technically — we know perfectly well how to build things, that is the problem. Mastered in the sense that someone would know for what end. And that presupposes the turn, of which he says two things at once: it is indispensable, and it is impossible to plan. Both. It is untenable, and he does not make it tenable.

So where does it begin? The answer is the most modest in the book, and it is the only one: in the individual (der einzelne Mensch). Not in an institution, not in a movement, not in a generation. In someone, one at a time, with no guarantee it spreads. It is derisory. It is all he has.

And here is his most disturbing position. We will not hide it.

Jaspers writes that life is not the supreme good. That a humanity accepting total domination in order to survive would have lost what makes it worth saving. And that it may be right to risk annihilation rather than submit. Read quickly, this gives: better all dead than all slaves. Many read it that way, and it earned him, in 1958, violent attacks — including from close friends. Hannah Arendt, who loved him, was troubled by it.

Precision is owed. Jaspers is making no strategic calculation and advises no war: he refuses to let sacrifice be turned into a political argument, and writes so plainly in chapter 3 of his first part. His thesis is philosophical — if survival becomes the supreme value, everything becomes negotiable, including truth, and then what survives is no longer man. That does not make the position less terrible. And what he does not say must be said: it is he who writes this, at seventy-five, from Basel — not those who would die.

What remains true, and cannot be taken from him: he refused both comforts. The one who says nothing is worth dying for, and the one who says there are causes for which others must die. He paid for the first with his career, with poison in his pocket for eight years.

The book then ends on a question, not an answer: where is there still trust? He does not answer that either.

The metaphor

To feel it

You cannot decide to understand. You can read it ten times, grind away, circle it — and then one morning it opens, and nothing explains why that morning. The work achieved nothing, except that no understanding ever comes without it.

A concrete situation

The day someone changes

You have surely seen this once. Someone nothing could reach — everything had been said to them, a thousand times, rightly, with affection, with anger. Nothing.

And one day they change. Not thanks to the right argument: the right argument they had heard three years earlier and brushed aside. Something turned, and nobody knows what — not even them, if you ask.

That is why Jaspers refuses to make a programme of it. Not out of modesty: because it would be false. What happened was not plannable, and if it had been, it would not have been a turn — it would be persuasion, that is, successful training.

And yet: without the thousand times someone spoke to them for nothing, it would not have happened either. That is what this chapter says. You cannot produce the turn. You can only go on speaking to someone without knowing whether it serves.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Five hundred pages to end on ‘it cannot be planned’ and a question. That is an abdication, and he knows it.”

Much must be granted to this objection. He has nothing to propose, he says so on page 24, and a reader who finds that unbearable is entitled to close the book. That is not a lazy reading: it is a legitimate demand, and Jaspers does not meet it.

But you have to see what makes it wrong all the same, and it is the whole book. A solution to this problem would have exactly one shape: someone would know where humanity is going and what must be done. That is chapter 7. That is total knowledge. A book ending on a plan would have refuted its own five hundred pages — and that plan would have been, by construction, the very thing it fights.

In other words, the absence of a solution is not the book’s failure: it is its only honest conclusion. Jaspers refuses to sell what he has just demonstrated cannot be bought.

And one should ask, frankly, what was expected. A man of seventy-five who watched his country become what it became, who was driven out, who waited for his wife’s deportation — if he had ended his book with a ten-point programme, would anyone have believed him?

For me

What it changes

This is the dossier’s last page, and it is not political.

You will change no one. Not your friend locked in his certainty, not your colleague who thinks she is rational, not your father. You can be right a thousand times and it will do nothing — and you already know it, since you have tried.

Jaspers does not say give up. He says something harder and more restful at once: it is not your job. The turn cannot be planned, so it does not depend on your skill, your patience or your arguments. Your part is smaller. Stay connected. Do not close. Go on speaking to someone without knowing whether it serves.

That is the opposite of an abdication. It is what remains once you have stopped believing you will succeed — and you continue.

Whom have you given up trying to understand? What would make you stay connected to them, with no hope of convincing them?

Check and connect

What man has produced must also be mastered by man. But the indispensable turn does not let itself be planned: it begins in the individual, or it does not begin.

After The Atom Bomb and the Future of Man (1958), part three — a reformulation, not a literal quotation.

Three questions to check

What is the turn?

The change in the way of thinking without which what man has produced will not be mastered. Jaspers says two things at once: it is indispensable, and it cannot be planned.

What is his most disturbing position?

That a humanity accepting total domination in order to survive would have lost what makes it worth saving — and that it may be right to risk annihilation. It earned him violent attacks, including from friends.

Why does the book not end on a solution?

Because a solution would have the shape of the total knowledge he has just dismantled over five hundred pages: someone would know where humanity is going. The absence of a plan is his only honest conclusion.
