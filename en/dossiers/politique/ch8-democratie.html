Politics · Chapter 8 of 9

Reason and democracy

Can democracy be reasonable?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers devotes the most uncomfortable chapter of the book to it, and offers no praise. He calls it the knot: democracy presupposes reasonable citizens, and does not produce them. From this he draws two sentences that disturb everyone. The sovereign people is answerable for what it does — you cannot be sovereign and victim. And democracy is an education, or it is only a counting of heads.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

He begins by distinguishing the idea from the ideal. The ideal would be a perfect state to be reached — and he does not believe in it for a second. The idea is something else: a demand that will never be met and that orients. Democracy is never made; it makes itself, badly, every day.

Then he examines the alternative, honestly — and here he becomes interesting. Every criticism of democracy is true. People vote badly. A majority is not right because it is a majority. Demagogues win. The short term crushes everything. Jaspers refutes none of it: he grants the lot. And he asks: and so, what else? Every alternative amounts to entrusting the decision to someone nobody will be able to contradict — that is, to chapter 3.

Hence his first hard sentence: the sovereign is accountable. If the people is sovereign, it answers for what it has done. You cannot say “the people decides” and “the people was deceived” — not both. Jaspers, who saw 1933, knows exactly what he is writing and to whom. His compatriots did not like it.

Then the demagogue, and his distinction between the politician and the statesman: the politician flatters man as he is; the statesman addresses what man can be. That is not a nuance of style. The demagogue takes people for what they are and locks them in — which is what makes him effective. It is very hard to lose by telling people they are right.

Finally the formula: democracy is education. Not in the sense of civics lessons. In the sense that a people becomes capable of governing itself only by learning to hear what contradicts it — that is, by learning chapter 6’s reason. A democracy where nobody listens to what disturbs them has already ceased to be what it thinks it is; only the procedure remains.

And he adds something nobody likes: democracy needs an aristocracy — not of birth or money, that is not the point. Those who demand of themselves more than is asked of them. If there are none, number decides alone.

The metaphor

To feel it

A school where the pupils vote on the curriculum. It is absurd if they know nothing — and that is exactly the argument of those who want to decide for them. The only trouble is that you learn to judge only by having to judge. There is no place where you become an adult before being one.

A concrete situation

The morning after an election

The result comes in, and it is bad. So everyone explains. People were manipulated. The networks, the money, the propaganda, the abstention. Each of these explanations contains truth — real truth, these are not invented excuses.

And notice what they all do together: they take back from the people what was granted to it the day before. Sovereign on Sunday, victim on Monday. It cannot be both — not at once, not from the same mouth.

Jaspers does not say manipulation does not exist; he describes pages of it. He says the explanation by manipulation is comfortable, and that it has a price: it excuses us from asking why it took. If we are manipulable, the question is not who manipulated. It is why it worked.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“This demand for reasonable citizens is contemptuous. It is the old professor’s disdain for the people.”

This is the objection to answer most carefully, because it lands on many authors — and misses him, for reasons of fact.

Jaspers spent twelve years under a regime that divided humanity by nature, and refused to divorce his Jewish wife when doing so would have restored his chair. He never held that human beings are of unequal worth. His “mass” is not the people — the science dossier says it: it is a state, into which a professor falls as easily as a labourer. And his “aristocracy” is not a class: it is whoever demands of themselves more than is asked of them, in any position.

But above all, turn the objection around. Who despises the people: the one who holds it capable of reason and asks it of them — or the one who takes it for a child who can only be flattered? The demagogue is the only one who never asks anything of anyone. That is what makes him so affectionate, and that is what makes him contemptuous.

What remains solid in the objection, Jaspers knows: saying “we would need reasonable citizens” does not manufacture them, and he has no means of producing them. He admits it in the next chapter.

For me

What it changes

There is one question to take away, and it is not about voting.

Every time something goes wrong in a group you belong to — a team, a family, an association — there are two possible answers. “We were badly informed, it was imposed on us, it is the fault of those above.” Or: “what did I let through?”

The first is almost always true. And it removes you from the group: you are no longer a member, you are its aggrieved spectator. The second is almost always partial, unfair to you, and it is the only one that leaves you somewhere to act.

In which group do you complain about a decision you let through without saying anything?

Three questions to check

What is democracy’s “knot” according to Jaspers?

It presupposes reasonable citizens and does not produce them. Every criticism made of it is true — he grants the lot, then asks: and what alternative?

What does “the sovereign is accountable” mean?

That you cannot say both “the people decides” and “the people was deceived”. Sovereign on Sunday, victim on Monday: not both.

Is this contempt for the people?

No: his mass is a state into which a professor falls like a labourer, and his aristocracy is whoever demands of themselves more than is asked. The real contempt is the demagogue’s, who asks nothing of anyone.
