The new fact

What really changed in 1945?

The question

In thirty seconds

One thing, and it is without precedent: humanity can now abolish itself. Not a city, not a people — the species. Jaspers calls this the new fact (der neue Tatbestand). What interests him is not the bomb: it is our reaction. We know, and we live as though we did not. He has a word for it: not-wanting-to-know (Nichtwissenwollen).

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

The book opens on a cold finding, in four steps.

What has happened: the researchers say so, with figures, and no one seriously disputes them. What is not known: the exact effects, the possible chain reaction — and Jaspers immediately notes the politicians’ tendency to reassure, which rests on nothing. Distinguishing the dangers: he refuses global panic and demands that the probable be separated from the possible. The veiling: the fact is covered over with language, doctrines, reassuring numbers, fatigue.

Then comes the sentence that governs the whole book: the new fact has not yet been appropriated. We know it; we have not made it our own. And Jaspers, who was a psychiatrist, knows exactly what he is looking at — this is not ignorance, it is an active refusal. You cannot ignore what you refuse to know: you have to know it a little first, in order to arrange not to think about it.

Why the refusal? Because knowing would oblige. As long as the fact stays one item of news among others, between the weather and the sport, I owe nothing. The day it becomes my fact, something has to change in the way I think — and that is what nobody wants.

And he announces at once what the book will not do: no solution (keine Lösung). No plan, no treaty, no programme. Two ways of thinking can approach the fact, he says — the understanding, which calculates, and reason, which will open onto something else. The whole book lies in that gap, and chapter 6 returns to it.

The metaphor

To feel it

A letter you do not open. You know what is in it — which is precisely why you do not open it. It stays on the table, you move it aside to lay the places, you live around it. It says nothing more than it did on the first day. Only, as long as it is closed, there is nothing to be done.

A concrete situation

What is not discussed at dinner

Try it one evening. Say at the table, without emphasis: “there is enough to destroy all human life, and it has never been dismantled.”

No one will contradict you. That is what is striking: no debate, no argument — the fact is granted in three seconds. And immediately someone will move on to something else. Not from indifference, not from stupidity. Because there is nothing to say, and above all nothing to be done from that table.

This is exactly what Jaspers describes, and it is not a reproach: it is the normal mechanism. We do not deny the fact. We file it. A fact with no possible action becomes scenery — and scenery stops being seen within days.

So his question is not “why are people so unconscious”. It is more honest: what do you do with a knowledge that opens onto no gesture?

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“So what? Nothing has gone off in seventy years. Deterrence works — it is the only thing that has worked.”

The objection is strong, and its point must be granted: nothing has gone off. That is a fact, it weighs, and Jaspers cannot invoke it — he is writing in 1958, he does not know how it will turn out. On this point the objection knows more than he does.

But it contains a leap that must be seen. From “it has not happened” we draw “it cannot happen” — and that step is not a deduction, it is a hope dressed as an argument. A system that has held for seventy years is not a system that cannot fail; it is a system that has not failed yet. Those are not the same sentence.

And above all, the objection changes the subject. Deterrence, supposing it works perfectly, describes a balance of fear: each abstains because the other can destroy everything. That may be effective. But Jaspers was not asking whether it is effective. He was asking: do we want that to be the ground humanity stands on? Answering “it works” is not an answer.

He would add this, which ought to make both camps uncomfortable: whoever answers “it works” and whoever answers “we are all going to die” have something in common. Neither needs to change anything.

For me

What it changes

Not-wanting-to-know is not a disease of crowds. It is a daily behaviour, and you know it.

The test result you do not go and collect. The statement you do not open. The conversation you have been putting off for six months with someone you love. It is never ignorance: it is knowledge held at a distance, precisely because it would oblige.

And the mechanism is exactly the same small as large. What protects the refusal is not doubt — it is the absence of an immediate gesture. As long as I do not know what to do, not knowing stays comfortable.

Jaspers does not ask you to act. He asks the one thing that comes before: appropriate the fact. Let it become yours. The rest comes after, or does not — but nothing comes without it.

What fact do you know perfectly well, and live as though you did not?

The new fact is known to all and appropriated by none: we have the information, and we do not know it — for to know would oblige us to change our way of thinking.

After The Atom Bomb and the Future of Man (1958), introduction — a reformulation, not a literal quotation. ✉️ Contact

Three questions to check

What is the “new fact”?

Since 1945 humanity can abolish itself. Not a city, not a people: the species. It is without precedent in all of history.

What is not-wanting-to-know?

An active refusal, not ignorance: we have the information and we arrange not to think about it, because appropriating it would oblige us to change.

What does Jaspers promise in this book?

No solution — he says so in the introduction. No plan, no programme: only to illuminate a situation until you can no longer act as though you had not seen it.
