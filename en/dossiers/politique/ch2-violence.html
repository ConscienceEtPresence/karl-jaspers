Politics · Chapter 2 of 9

Violence is a limit situation

Can there be politics without violence?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers does something here almost no one does: he treats violence as a limit situation — like death, like guilt. That is, a wall, not a problem. You do not solve it; you go through it with open eyes or by veiling your face. The result: neither the pacifism that condemns it from above, nor the cynicism that submits to it. Both are ways of not looking at it.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

The argument unfolds in four steps, and each is unpleasant.

One. Violence is a fundamental situation of life, not an accident that could be abolished. To want to abolish it by decree is to want to abolish a limit situation — exactly as if one decreed the end of death.

Two. Inside a state a dam exists: the rule of law. Violence has not disappeared — it is monopolised, framed, subjected to rules. That is an immense achievement, and a fragile one.

Three. Between states, that dam does not exist. There is international law; there is no force to impose it. Jaspers does not rejoice in this, he observes it — and draws the consequence most people avoid: in those conditions, the self-preservation of a free people may require force.

Four. Then comes Gandhi, and Jaspers does not brush him aside. He admires him, devotes pages to non-violence, and grants that it worked. Then he asks the painful question: against whom? Gandhi faced an adversary who cared about his own moral image, who had a press, a parliament, an uneasy conscience. Non-violence acts on someone who can still feel shame. Before a power determined to eliminate without witnesses, it has no object.

And sacrifice? Jaspers sees in it the highest thing there is — and he insists on asking: sacrifice for what? A sacrifice that saves nothing and that no one sees is no less beautiful; it is something else. Here he calls on his old friend Max Weber and his ethic of responsibility: in politics you answer for consequences, not for the purity of your intentions. Whoever keeps clean hands by letting things happen does not have clean hands.

The metaphor

To feel it

A dyke. It has not abolished the sea, and no one asks it to. It holds the sea back on one side only, and it must be repaired every year. Forget it, and you end up believing the sea has gone.

A concrete situation

The school playground

One child hits another. An adult arrives, separates them, punishes. Violence has not ceased to exist — it has met a superior force that stopped it, and that force obeys rules.

Remove the adult. You do not get a peaceful playground: you get a playground where the strongest decides. Nobody has become nastier. The dam has gone.

Between states, there is no adult. That is all Jaspers wants us to see, and there is nothing cynical in it: it is the exact description of what is left when the dam was never built. So the political question is not “how do we abolish violence”, but “how do we manufacture an adult nobody has ever managed to manufacture”.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“This is the old warmonger’s excuse: ‘violence is human, so let us resign ourselves.’”

No — and this is precisely the misreading the notion of limit situation protects against, provided it has been understood.

A limit situation authorises nothing. Before death, you do not “resign yourself”: you go through it with open eyes, or you lie to yourself. There is no permission to be drawn from it. The same here: saying violence is a limit situation is not saying it is allowed. It is saying no formula will excuse us from it, and that whoever resorts to it remains entirely guilty of what he does.

Jaspers’s whole position is in that word. The warmonger says: it was necessary, so I have nothing to reproach myself with. Jaspers says: it may be necessary, and you answer for it. Both, entirely. That is what he elsewhere calls guilt — and it is no accident that his book on German guilt is this one’s counterpart.

What remains true in the objection must be said: this argument has served to justify massacres, and will again. A thought that grants violence may be necessary is a dangerous thought. Jaspers knows. He answers that the thought which denies it is no less dangerous — it merely leaves others to do the work.

For me

What it changes

This chapter reaches beyond politics, which is why it comes so early.

There are moments in any life when you cannot stay clean. Where silence is complicity and speaking is brutality. Where protecting one person requires wounding another. There is no pure option — and the experience is that we hunt for the pure option for days before admitting it does not exist.

Jaspers does not console. He says: choose, and answer for it. Do not tell yourself you had no choice, and do not tell yourself you did no harm. Both lies are ways of withdrawing from your own life.

In what situation did you do harm while being right — and what have you told yourself about it since?

In his words · Quiz · To go on

Check and connect

Violence is a limit situation: no doctrine abolishes it. Whoever resorts to it without necessity is a criminal; whoever believes himself exempt leaves others to carry the guilt.

After The Atom Bomb and the Future of Man (1958), part one — a reformulation, not a literal quotation.

Three questions to check

Why treat violence as a limit situation?

Because it is a wall, not a problem: you do not solve it. You go through it with open eyes or by veiling your face — but no doctrine abolishes it.

What does Jaspers say about Gandhi?

He admires him and grants that it worked. Then he asks against whom: non-violence acts on an adversary who can still feel shame. Before a power that eliminates without witnesses, it has no object.

“Violence is human, so let us resign ourselves” — is that his position?

No, the exact opposite. A limit situation authorises nothing. It may be necessary, and you answer for it entirely: both, as in Max Weber’s ethic of responsibility.
