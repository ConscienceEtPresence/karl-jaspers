The lie on principle

What really separates a free regime from a tyranny?

The question

In thirty seconds

Not the virtue of its rulers — everyone lies. Not the absence of violence — it is everywhere. Jaspers points to something else, and it is formidably precise: the lie on principle (die Lüge im Prinzip). In a free regime people lie, and the lie can be exposed — that is a failure of the system. Under total domination, the lie is the system: there is no longer an outside from which to check it.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Jaspers writes this having lived twelve years under a regime that drove him from the university and put his wife on a deportation list. This is not a professor’s typology.

His distinction is not about the quantity of lying, which is what makes it useful. It is about its status.

In a free regime a minister lies. It is demonstrated, a newspaper prints it, he falls — or he does not, and that is a scandal. Either way something worked: there existed an outside instance before which the lie is a lie. The regime can lie, but it cannot decree that the true is false.

Under total domination that outside point is abolished. There is nowhere left to check from — the press, the courts, the archives, science, statistics, all are inside. Then the lie stops being a lie in the ordinary sense: it becomes official reality. And the mark of recognition is terrible in its simplicity: you are no longer required to believe it. You are required to repeat it. Repeating what you know to be false, in front of people who know that you know: that is submission. Believing would almost be less grave.

And Jaspers then states the opposite principle, the one he places among the conditions of a possible peace: unlimited communication — publicity of acts, the principle of truthfulness, and above all the always-open possibility of revision. What must protect itself from discussion in order to survive is already false. That is his criterion of truth, the one from the dossier on truth, become here a political criterion. This is not a metaphor: it is the same demand.

The metaphor

To feel it

Two courts. In the first, the judge is sometimes corrupt — and when it is proven, he falls. In the second, the judge is never corrupt, because corruption has become the law. The second is infinitely cleaner. There is nothing left to prove, and no one left to prove it to.

A concrete situation

The meeting where everybody knows

You do not need a regime to know this. A meeting. The figure announced is false, and the twelve people around the table know it. Each knows the other eleven know it. Nobody says anything.

And tomorrow each will repeat the figure. Not because they believe it — they do not, and they know the person they are telling does not either.

Look at what has just happened: there is no longer an outside point in that room. It does not take much — twelve people suffice. It is no longer a lie, it has become the working reality, and whoever said the true thing would not be contradicted: they would be improper.

Jaspers would say: you have just seen in miniature what, at scale, is called total domination. The difference is of scale, not of kind. Which is why accusing tyrants is not enough.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Our governments lie too, our media are owned. This distinction is Cold War propaganda.”

The premise is true and Jaspers does not contest it for a second: our regimes lie, our presses have owners, our powers bury what embarrasses them. He is selling no camp — he spent his life criticising his own, and finally left Germany for Basel in 1948, sickened by what his country would not look at.

But the objection erases the only thing that matters. How do you know your government lied? Because someone established it, published it, and you were able to read it. That because is the whole difference. Your objection is living proof of what it denies: it was possible.

Jaspers’s test does not ask whether there is lying. It asks: what happens to the person who demonstrates it? In one case they are published, contested, sometimes rewarded, often ignored. In the other they disappear. That is not a difference of degree, and confusing the two is not lucidity — it is the comfort of someone who has never had to check.

And he would add that saying “it is the same everywhere” is exactly what a regime of lies wants said. Not that it is right: that there is no longer any difference.

For me

What it changes

The question is not which regime you live in. It is nearer.

Are there, in your work, your family, your circle, things everybody knows and nobody says? Not secrets — shared obvious facts, maintained by tacit agreement. And what happens to whoever says them?

The answer to that last question tells you everything. If they are contradicted, argued with, shouted at: all is well, the outside point exists. If they are simply set aside — not refuted, set aside — then communication is already closed somewhere, and nobody needed a tyrant for that.

What does everyone around you know, and nobody say? What would happen to whoever said it?

Three questions to check

What is the lie on principle?

Not a quantity of lying but its status: in a free regime a lie can be exposed, which is a failure of the system. Under total domination, it is the system.

What is the mark of recognition?

You are no longer required to believe it, but to repeat it — in front of people who know that you know. Believing would almost be less grave than repeating.

“Our governments lie too” — what is the answer?

That it is true, and that the question is different: what happens to whoever demonstrates it? Published in one case, disappeared in the other. The objection proves what it denies.
