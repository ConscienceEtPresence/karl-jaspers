Politics · Chapter 6 of 9

Understanding and reason

Why do very intelligent people drive us into the wall?

The question

In thirty seconds

Because intelligence is not reason. Jaspers takes up a distinction from Kant and makes it the pivot of his book: understanding (Verstand) calculates, plans, optimises — it is excellent and blind to ends. Reason (Vernunft) is something else: the will to unlimited communication, the refusal to close. A man can be formidably intelligent and without reason. That is the portrait of our age.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Here is the chapter that governs everything, and the most misunderstood thing in Jaspers — because “reason” suggests to us coldness, calculation, the opposite of the heart. In him it is almost the reverse.

Understanding is the faculty that divides, measures, deduces, organises. It works by objects. It is indispensable: without it, no science, no administration, no bridge that stands. But it has one property that must be seen: it is perfect inside a frame, and it can never step outside the frame. Give it a goal and it will reach it brilliantly. Ask it whether the goal is worth anything: silence.

Reason is not one more faculty alongside. It is a fundamental disposition — Jaspers says a basic constitution. It is the will to close nowhere: to listen to what disturbs me, to let the other contradict me to the end, to keep revision always open. Its concrete form is unlimited communication — the same demand as in chapter 3, the same as in chapter 4. It is not a method. It cannot be learned. It is willed.

And Jaspers adds what makes reason so unattractive, with disarming lucidity: it seems soft, because it does not conclude; it seems slippery, because it is never univocal; it repels, because it looks haughty. It has nothing going for it. Against a doctrine that settles matters, it always loses.

Then comes the community of the reasonable — and note he does not say of the intelligent. What founds it is not intellectual level: it is fidelity in communication. Staying connected to whoever thinks otherwise, not breaking off, not filing them under a category that excuses you from listening. That is all, and it is enormous.

Finally the political consequence, very concrete: censorship or freedom. The public struggle of the mind is where reason exists or vanishes. Closing the discussion is not winning: it is moving over to chapter 3’s side.

The metaphor

To feel it

An engine, and a steering wheel. The engine is magnificent: more power, less fuel, endlessly improvable. It will never know where we are going. And improving the engine does not bring you a millimetre closer to the answer — it only gets you somewhere faster.

A concrete situation

The very intelligent meeting

A room full of brilliant people. The whiteboard fills: scenarios, projections, trade-offs, figures. Everything is correct. Each argument holds, each objection gets a solid answer.

Three hours later the decision is taken — and it is absurd. Not wrong: no one made an error in a calculation. Absurd, meaning perfectly optimised for an end nobody ever put on the table.

One sentence would have been enough, and it was not said: why exactly do we want this? It was not said because it is awkward, because it has no calculable answer, and because whoever asks it wastes the time of eleven competent people.

That is understanding without reason. Nobody is stupid. Nobody is acting in bad faith. And the whole room advances perfectly towards a place it never decided to go.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“This ‘reason’ that never concludes and proposes nothing is the philosopher’s comfort. At some point you have to decide.”

It is the most serious objection in the dossier, and Jaspers makes it against himself: reason seems soft, it repels, it seems utopian. He gives it a whole chapter. He does not defend himself.

But it misses the point. Jaspers never says one should not decide — the opposite: his whole philosophy hangs on decision, and he despises hesitation that mistakes itself for depth. He does not ask you to suspend judgement. He asks that revision remain possible after you have decided.

The difference is simple and checkable: both men decide. One decides and goes on listening to what contradicts his decision. The other decides and closes — from then on whatever contradicts him is an enemy, a naivety, a manoeuvre. The first is reasonable in Jaspers’s sense. The second is perhaps more effective, and he is on his way to chapter 3.

And one thing must be added that stings: whoever says “at some point you have to decide” has usually already decided, and only wants to stop being disturbed.

For me

What it changes

The test comes down to one question, and you can ask it tonight.

Take the conviction you hold most dear. Now: what would make you change your mind? If you can name it — a fact, an experience, a precise argument — you are reasonable in Jaspers’s sense, whatever your view. If nothing could make you change, then it is not a conviction: it is a shell, and you are inside it.

Note carefully: the question is not about what you think. Two men opposed on everything can both be reasonable. And two men who agree on everything can both be closed. Reason is not a content, it is a way of holding what you think.

Take your strongest conviction: what, concretely, would make you change your mind? If nothing, what do you make of that?

Check and connect

Understanding calculates inside a frame it cannot cross; reason is the will to unlimited communication. One can be extremely intelligent and without reason.

After The Atom Bomb and the Future of Man (1958), part three — a reformulation, not a literal quotation.

Three questions to check

What is the difference between understanding and reason?

Understanding calculates, measures, optimises: perfect inside a frame, blind to ends. Reason is the will to unlimited communication, the refusal to close. You can have one without the other.

Why is reason so unattractive?

Jaspers says it himself: it seems soft because it does not conclude, slippery because never univocal, haughty and so repellent. Against a doctrine that settles things, it always loses.

What founds the community of the reasonable?

Not intellectual level: fidelity in communication. Staying connected to whoever thinks otherwise, without filing them under a category that excuses you from listening.
