Politics · Chapter 4 of 9

The demand for the supra-political

What is left when politics has done all it can?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers does the arithmetic honestly, to the end — abolish the bomb, international control, a world state — and every route fails for reasons he sets out. His conclusion is not despair: it is the demand for the supra-political (das Überpolitische). An ethos without which humanity is lost, which belongs to no institution, which no treaty produces — and which cannot be planned.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

The order must be followed, or this chapter reads as piety.

Jaspers begins with the obvious solution: abolish the bomb. He shows why it fails, and the argument is purely technical — the failure of control. A ban is worth only what can be verified; and no one can verify inside another state without an intrusion no one accepts. One side’s renunciation becomes the other’s monopoly.

So he lists the conditions of a real world peace, and their lucidity has not aged. There must be legal bonds no one can break: renouncing arbitrariness, the veto, absolute sovereignty. There must be unlimited communication: publicity of acts, the principle of truthfulness. And revision must remain always possible — no frozen order.

Then he does what almost no one does after writing so fine a list: he observes that these principles are in fact rejected today. Not debated: rejected. No state renounces its sovereignty.

He also rules out the world state, and not for reasons of feasibility: he sees in it a fundamental error. A power with no outside is a power before which nothing can be checked any more — that is the previous chapter, at planetary scale. Peace by world state would be total domination, successful. He sets against it only something modest: a confederation, peace in freedom, an unstable state that must be remade endlessly.

So the supra-political remains. And Jaspers does not sell it: he states its two weaknesses himself, which is rare. It cannot be planned — no ministry will produce it. And he raises the doubt he never lifts: can the human being become other? He does not answer yes. He says no one can know in advance what reason is capable of, because it exists.

A last warning, very much his own: the dilution of ethos into morality. As soon as this demand is turned into rules, codes, charters, ethics committees, it is lost. It becomes conformity — something you can comply with while being nobody.

The metaphor

To feel it

A common language. No treaty instituted it, no ministry manufactured it, and you cannot decree that two peoples understand each other. It comes, or it does not. And without it, every treaty in the world is paper signed by people who do not speak.

A concrete situation

The couple and the contract

Two people are tearing each other apart. So they do what people do: they make rules. Who speaks when, what is not said, how tasks are shared, an appointment with someone on Tuesdays. The rules are good. They hold for a few months.

And nothing changes, because one thing is missing that no rule contains: that each still really wants the other to exist. If that is there, the rules are almost unnecessary. If it is not, they only organise the ending tidily.

This is exactly what Jaspers says of treaties between nations. They are necessary — he never says otherwise, he lists their conditions over pages. But they do not produce what would make them effective. And that thing can be neither voted, nor decreed, nor bought.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“This is wishful thinking. He observes that politics fails, and appeals to a moral conversion of humanity — he might as well pray.”

The objection is fair, and the astonishing thing is that Jaspers makes it himself, before you, in the book. A whole chapter is titled: reason seems utopian. He does not defend himself, he sets out the arguments against him — the average nature of human beings, the absence of any progress in ethos, the powerlessness of a few reasonable people against the mass.

His answer has two points, and it is subtler than hope.

First: since reason exists, no one can know in advance what it can do. That is not optimism, it is a point of logic. To predict reason’s impotence is still total knowledge about the future — exactly what chapter 7 dismantles. Whoever asserts “it will never change” claims to know something no one can know.

Second, and more important: what would be a utopia as a programme can be a cipher — a sign that awakens. Jaspers is not proposing a plan for converting humanity. He is describing a demand the mere sight of which changes whoever sees it. Confusing the two is reading him as a politician when he is writing as a philosopher.

The objection keeps its bite, and it must be granted: he has nothing to propose. He says so in the introduction — no solution. Whoever comes looking for a plan will leave empty-handed, and that is deliberate.

For me

What it changes

There is a very concrete use for this chapter, and it is not political at all.

Every time something goes wrong — in a team, a family, a school — the reaction is always the same: make a rule. A procedure, a charter, a reminder. And it sometimes works, because rules are necessary; this is not about despising them.

But ask Jaspers’s question: what would have to be there for this rule to be unnecessary? If you can name it, you know what is really missing. And you also know the rule will not produce it — at best it protects in the meantime, at worst it lets everyone pretend the problem has been dealt with.

What rule have you introduced to replace something that should have been there?
