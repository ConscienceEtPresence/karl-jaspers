Politics · Chapter 5 of 9

What do the researchers think?

Is a scientist responsible for what his knowledge makes possible?

The question

In thirty seconds

Jaspers devotes an entire excursus to one precise fact: in April 1957, eighteen of Germany’s most eminent physicists publish a declaration at Göttingen. They refuse to take part in any way in the manufacture or use of atomic weapons. Jaspers does not merely applaud: he examines what the act is worth, what it can do, and what it cannot.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

The setting: the Federal Republic, barely twelve years after the war, is considering equipping its army with tactical nuclear weapons. Eighteen physicists — several of whom had taken part in Germany’s wartime uranium research, which is not a detail — publish a short text. In it they declare that none of them will take part in any way in such weapons.

Jaspers cuts the act into pieces, without indulgence.

What falls within their competence: spreading knowledge. They know what such a weapon does, and the public does not. There they speak as experts, and they are right to speak — it is a duty.

What does not: political judgement. Should the Federal Republic have them or not? On that a physicist has no particular authority. He is a citizen like any other. This is chapter 3 of the science dossier, applied to a real case: competence about facts confers no competence about what should be wanted.

The ethical sense of the refusal: here Jaspers salutes. A man who says “I will not take part” is not invoking expertise. He is staking his person. It is not an argument, it is an act — and it is the only one of its kind in the whole book.

And the step into the dark: Jaspers does not hide that this refusal has no guarantee. If they do not do it, others will. The act does not calculate its effects. It may have none.

Then comes what interests him most: the researchers themselves call for a new way of thinking (eine neue Denkungsart). They call for it — and they cannot supply it. Their method does not produce it. They are the first to run into the limit of their own knowledge. Jaspers sees in this the demonstration of his whole book, made by others and against their will.

The metaphor

To feel it

The locksmith who made the key. He knows better than anyone what it opens — indeed he is the only one who really knows. That gives him no special right to say what should be done with the house. But he can say: that key, I will not cut. And that is not an opinion, it is him.

A concrete situation

The email you do not sign

You are asked to produce something you know how to produce, and whose use you do not approve of. A figure presented out of context. A file that will be used to push someone out. A report whose conclusion is written in advance.

You have the competence. That is precisely why you are being asked. And you have no authority to decide the use — it is not your decision, it was taken above you.

One thing remains, only one, and it is exactly the eighteen’s: saying “I am not doing it”. It probably changes nothing — someone else will do it on Monday, and you know that as you say it. It has no guaranteed effect. But at that precise moment, someone exists, and it is the only place in the dossier where Jaspers sees freedom show itself.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“A fine gesture, no effect. The Bundeswehr never got the bomb, but America stationed its own there. They bought themselves a clear conscience cheaply.”

On the facts, the objection scores a point Jaspers could not know in 1958: American weapons were indeed stationed in Germany, and still are. The eighteen’s refusal did not prevent that.

But “cheaply” is false, and checkable. These men had careers, funding, chairs; some already carried the suspicion of having worked for the Nazi regime. Signing meant exposure, in a country that wanted to hear none of it. Adenauer reacted furiously. It was not free.

And in substance, the objection judges the act by its effects — that is, by Max Weber’s ethic of responsibility, the one Jaspers defends in chapter 2. So it has right on its side. Jaspers would nevertheless say that “it changed nothing” does not exhaust the question. A man who says “not me” is not claiming to change the world: he is refusing to be the instrument. Those are two things, and confusing them amounts to saying nothing counts but the result — which is exactly the thinking he fights.

The honest thing is to grant that both hold. The act did not have the effect hoped for. And it had to be done.

For me

What it changes

This chapter contains a distinction that serves daily, and that we confuse constantly.

What I know gives me no authority over what should be done. The doctor on end-of-life, the economist on justice, the engineer on climate, the professor on what society should be: all know things others do not, and that does not give them a vote that counts double. Confusing the two is the reign of experts — and Jaspers sees in it an abdication dressed as competence.

But the same distinction has a reverse side, and it is the more demanding one. If my knowledge does not give me the right to decide for others, neither does it excuse me from deciding for myself. I do not have to say what the world must do. I have to say what I am doing.

What are you good at, that you are asked to do, and that you do while telling yourself someone else would do it anyway?

What falls within their competence (spreading knowledge: yes, a duty); what does not (political judgement: they are citizens like any other); and the personal refusal, which is not an argument but an act.
