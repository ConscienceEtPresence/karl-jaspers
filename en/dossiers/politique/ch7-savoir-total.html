Politics · Chapter 7 of 9

Total knowledge

Why do those who know where history is going never have anything to do?

The question

In thirty seconds

Because that is exactly what it is for. Jaspers calls total knowledge (Totalwissen) the claim to know the overall process — where we come from, where we are going, what is really at stake. And he states its real use, which is always the same: it is a flight from the human task. Whoever knows where history is going no longer has to decide. He only has to accompany.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

This chapter is the summit of the book, and it is chapter 4 of the science dossier — superstition — turned political.

Total knowledge is recognised by one thing: it offers a fundamental process that explains the whole. The name changes with the era and the camp — class struggle, the decline of the West, the meaning of history, the clash of civilisations, the great replacement, the coming collapse, the march of progress. The structure is identical. A single key, a known direction, and a place assigned to each of us.

Jaspers has a word for it, and it is cruel: it is a gnosis — das gnostische Wissen. A revelation presenting itself as knowledge. It has the form of science, borrows its vocabulary, and has none of its properties: it is not refutable. Everything contradicting it becomes one more proof — you have not understood, you are blinded, that is exactly what the system wants you to believe.

He then examines the thinking of the end (das Denken des Endes): the idea that we are living the last days, that everything is going to collapse. And he draws the decisive distinction. Observing a real danger, with figures, is knowledge — that is what this whole book does. Decreeing the end is something else: it is a myth, and a comfortable one, because it liberates. If everything is finished, nothing remains to be decided.

Against which he sets one modest thing: orientation, or total knowledge. Get your bearings honestly, knowing the limits of your forecasts — or claim to hold everything. The one leaves the future open, so it obliges. The other closes it, so it rests.

And he adds the one thing left to whoever has renounced total knowledge: the cipher. At the limit of knowledge, thinking continues — but now knowing that it does not know. The cipher speaks without proving. Total knowledge proves without saying anything.

The metaphor

To feel it

A master key. It opens every door — which is what is asked of it, and very convenient. Then comes the day you find a locked door. You never think the key is bad: you think the lock has been tampered with.

A concrete situation

The man who has understood everything

You know him. He explains the world to you, and it is impressive: everything fits. The war, inflation, your boss, the elections, the climate — one cause, and he has found it. He has facts, dates, often more than you.

So you object. And watch what happens: your objection does not shake him, it confirms him. It proves the propaganda worked, that you are naive, that you have not yet seen. There is nothing you can say that would count as a contradiction. You have just met a thought nothing can reach.

And notice the most important thing, the one Jaspers points to: this man does not act. He explains. He comments, shares, predicts — and does nothing, because there is nothing to do when the process is already written. His immense knowledge has bought him exactly one thing: the right to be responsible for nothing.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“So we should think nothing large? Analysing a society, an era, a trend — is that forbidden?”

No, and that would be absurd: this entire book is a large-scale analysis of his era. Jaspers spends five hundred pages interpreting the world. He forbids himself nothing.

The difference is not in the scale of the claim, it is in its status — and it is tested by one question: can your analysis be wrong?

An honest interpretation says what would refute it. It gives its conditions, its margins, the facts that resist it. It advances knowing it is a hypothesis. Total knowledge can never be wrong — that is its signature, and precisely why it is worthless. As in chapter 2 of the science dossier: a thought nothing can contradict is not stronger, it is weaker, because it risks nothing.

So Jaspers does not ask you to give up thinking big. He asks you to know you are interpreting, and to say so. That is not a limit of intelligence: it is the previous dossier’s probity, applied to history.

For me

What it changes

Total knowledge is not reserved for ideologues. It is intimate, and it is recognised by the fact that it rests.

“People are like that anyway.” “It always ends the same way.” “I know how this is going to turn out.” Each of these sentences is total knowledge in miniature. Each may be true. And each does exactly the same work: it excuses you from trying.

The test is the same as the previous chapter’s, and it is merciless: what could prove you wrong? If the answer is “nothing”, you do not know. You are resting.

What sentence do you repeat about “how it always ends” — and what does it save you from attempting?

Three questions to check

What is total knowledge?

The claim to know history’s overall process: where we come from, where we are going. Its real function is to excuse you from acting — whoever knows the direction need only accompany it.

Why does Jaspers speak of gnosis?

Because it is revelation disguised as knowledge: it has the form of science without its essential property — it is not refutable. Every objection confirms it.

Is analysing an era forbidden?

No — the whole book does it. The difference is status: an honest interpretation says what would refute it. Total knowledge can never be wrong, and that is why it is worthless.
