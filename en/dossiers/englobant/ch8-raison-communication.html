Dossier · The Map of the Encompassing · Chapter 8 of 8

Reason and communication

What keeps the map from falling to pieces?

The question

In thirty seconds

Six modes, and one risk: that each should shut itself inside its own. Two things link them. Reason — the refusal to stop at a single mode. And communication — the test between two, without which no truth holds. Without them, the map is only a chest of drawers.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Let us recapitulate the map. The Encompassing that we are: empirical existence (the living creature that wants to last), consciousness in general (the mind that proves), spirit (the world of meaning), Existenz (what decides and answers). The Encompassing that being is: the world (the horizon never closed) and Transcendence (what carries everything). Six modes, all real, all simultaneous — I am the first four at once, this very second.

But a map is not a chest of drawers. If the modes stayed separate, each would absolutise itself in its corner — and we should be back to the disease of chapter 1: “everything is matter”, “only the proven counts”, “everything is culture”. So we need what runs through and links them. Jaspers names two things, and they are inseparable.

Reason (Vernunft) first — on no account to be confused with the understanding that calculates (that is consciousness in general, chapter 4). Reason, in Jaspers, is a will: the will to stop at no mode, to stay open to all, to refuse every closure. It produces no knowledge of its own; it is the movement that prevents each mode from taking itself for the whole. It is a permanent refusal to be satisfied — the exact opposite of a system.

Communication next, and here is why it is indispensable rather than merely nice. How can I know whether I am absolutising my own horizon? Alone, it is impossible: my horizon, by definition, is what I do not see as a horizon. I need someone looking from elsewhere — not to contradict me for sport, but to make me feel the limits of my window. This is why Jaspers writes that truth is what binds us together: it is not a pretty phrase, it is a structural consequence of the Encompassing. A conviction never exposed to another is already becoming a possession.

And now we see what this map really is. It is not a classification to be learned by heart; it is an instrument of orientation, and it comes down to one question that can be asked at any moment: which mode am I speaking from? Am I discussing a fact (consciousness in general), a vital interest (Dasein), a shared meaning (spirit), or a decision that binds only me (Existenz)? Half of all human quarrels come from a mixing of modes. Knowing how to distinguish them — then to link them through reason and communication — is all that Jaspers means by philosophizing.

The metaphor

To feel it

Six instruments in a room. Each can play alone: it will be in tune, and it will be poor. Each can also play louder than the others: that will be a din. Reason is the will to hear them all; communication is the fact that there is someone else in the room. Without both, there is no music — only instruments.

A concrete situation

Four voices at the same table

A family is deciding whether to move an elderly parent into a care home. Listen to the four voices: “we cannot afford it, and no one can stop working” (Dasein); “here are the figures, life expectancy, the studies on care homes” (consciousness in general); “in our family we have never abandoned the old” (spirit); and a lower voice: “I cannot do this to my father” (Existenz).

None of these voices is wrong. They simply are not speaking from the same level — and that is exactly why the discussion goes round in circles, each believing the other is arguing in bad faith. The opposite is true: they are all sincere, and they do not inhabit the same mode.

Reason, here, does not consist in finding the winning mode. It consists in holding them all open: acknowledging that money really counts, that the figures really count, that family loyalty really counts — and that in the end someone will have to decide, with none of the three doing it in their place. Communication means they say this to each other's faces, without trying to win. There will be no computable solution. There will be a decision owned — or a flight.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Six modes, two links: this is still a system. You reproach Hegel for putting everything in boxes, and you hand us a six-drawer chart.”

This is the most intelligent objection that can be made — and Jaspers made it to himself, which is a good sign. The danger must be conceded: yes, a map can become a doctrine; yes, the six modes can be recited like a lesson; yes, textbooks do it, and the result is dead.

But look at what this map does, precisely, compared with a system. A system explains: it gives every thing its place within a completed whole, and its pride is to leave nothing outside. This map does the opposite: it serves to prevent any total explanation. It has no summit — no mode crowns the others. It has no closure — two of its modes (Existenz and Transcendence) are by definition unknowable. And its central concept is precisely what cannot go into a box, since it is not an object. It is a system that denounces itself in stating itself.

The test is simple, in any case: what do you do with it? With Hegel's system, you conclude. With Jaspers's map, you never conclude: you locate where you are, and you set off again. It says nothing about what to think; it merely recalls that we always think from somewhere. A map is not a territory — and this one, more than any other, says so itself.

For me

What it changes

Here is the most concrete use of this whole dossier, and it fits in one question to keep in your pocket: which mode is this, right now? A family disagreement, a row at work, a public debate: ask yourself whether people are talking about facts (to be proven), interests (to be negotiated), meaning (to be understood) or personal decisions (to be owned). You will watch half the conflicts dissolve — not because someone was wrong, but because no one was speaking from the same level.

And the other use, more demanding: actively seek out the person who thinks differently. Not out of politeness, not out of tolerance — out of necessity. Since I cannot see my own window, someone must show it to me. This is why, in Jaspers, everything ends there: the map of being leads to a conversation. There is no solitary wisdom — there are only people who truly speak to one another, or who stop searching.

In my last real disagreement: which mode was the other speaking from — and which was I?

In his words · Quiz · To go on

Check and connect

Truth is what binds us together.
Karl Jaspers, Vom Ursprung und Ziel der Geschichte (1949) — translation of “Wahrheit ist, was uns verbindet.”

Three questions to check

What are the six modes of the Encompassing?

What we are: empirical existence (Dasein), consciousness in general, spirit, Existenz. What being is: world, Transcendence. All real, all simultaneous.

Is reason, in Jaspers, the faculty of calculating?

No — that is consciousness in general. Reason is a will: the will to stop at no mode, to stay open to all. It produces no knowledge: it prevents each mode from taking itself for the whole.

Why is communication structurally necessary?

Because my horizon is, by definition, what I do not see as a horizon. Alone, I cannot know whether I am absolutising it. I need someone looking from elsewhere — hence: truth is what binds us together.
