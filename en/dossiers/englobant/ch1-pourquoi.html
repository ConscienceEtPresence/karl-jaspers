Dossier · The Map of the Encompassing · Chapter 1 of 8

Why this concept?

Why can I never step outside the whole in order to look at it?

The question

In thirty seconds

Everything I know, I know as an object set before me, within a horizon. But I can always push the horizon back — and never step out of it. The whole is therefore never an object: it is that within which I already am. Jaspers gives it a name: the Encompassing.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Let us begin with a very simple thought experiment, which anyone can do in three seconds. Think of something: this table. You see it as an object, bounded, against a background — the room. Now think of the room: it in turn becomes an object, against a wider background — the house, the city. Keep going: the country, the Earth, the galaxy, the universe. Each time, what was background becomes object, and a new background appears behind it.

Now the question that makes everything: can you reach the last background? The one with nothing behind it? Try: the moment you think it, you make it an object — and so there is something “around” it. This is structural; it is not a lack of effort. Our knowledge works by cutting out: it can grasp only what stands out against a background. The whole stands out against nothing.

Jaspers calls this the Encompassing (das Umgreifende: literally, “that which grasps all around”). Beware the immediate misreading: it is not the universe, nor the sum of everything that exists — that would still be an object, a very large one. It is what never itself appears, and within which everything appears. The background that never becomes a figure.

Why forge such a strange concept? Because Jaspers sees in it the remedy for the intellectual disease he fought all his life: taking a particular perspective for the whole. The materialist says: everything is matter. The idealist: everything is spirit. The enthusiastic biologist: everything is life. Each has grasped something real — and each has turned their horizon into a totality. The concept of the Encompassing is a vaccination against that gesture: it recalls that no position is God's point of view, because there is no outside.

And note carefully that this in no way condemns us to scepticism. Jaspers does not say that nothing can be known: he says that all knowledge is situated. We can know an enormous amount — provided we know where we are looking from, and do not mistake our window for the landscape.

The metaphor

To feel it

A fish can explore the whole ocean. It can know its currents, its depths, its light, its salt. It will never see the ocean — because to see it you would have to leave it, and to leave it is to die. We are that fish: what carries us is exactly what we cannot look at from outside.

A concrete situation

The map and the territory

A cartographer sets out to map the country he is in. He does admirable work: every road, every river, every house. One day a child asks him: “and you, where are you on the map?”

He can draw himself in, of course — a small dot. But then he must also draw the dot that draws the dot, and so on. There is always a cartographer who remains outside the map: the one making it. The map can contain everything except the act of mapping.

This is exactly our situation. Our knowledges are magnificent maps: physics, biology, history, psychology. None contains the one who establishes them, in the act of establishing them. Not because he is hiding, but because he is the place from which one looks. The Encompassing is that: the territory that makes the map possible and that no map exhausts.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“It is just a word for saying ‘we don't know’. You baptise our ignorance and turn it into philosophical profundity.”

The objection is healthy, and must be answered precisely. No: the Encompassing does not designate what we do not yet know. A scientific unknown — dark matter, the origin of life — is an object not yet reached: it is a candidate for becoming knowledge, and perhaps tomorrow it will. Nothing of the sort here. The Encompassing is not a distant object: it is what is not an object, and no progress will change that, since there is no possible progress toward what is not a thing to be found.

Next, this concept is not empty: it works. It supplies a very concrete criterion for spotting a frequent and costly error — absolutisation. Every time someone says “man is nothing but…” (nothing but neurons, nothing but relations of production, nothing but genes, nothing but desire), the concept of the Encompassing lets us name exactly what has just happened: a mode of being has been taken for being itself. It is not a word for ignorance: it is a tool of rigour.

Finally, notice that the objection itself comes from somewhere. To say “only objective knowledge counts” is not a result of objective knowledge — it is a philosophical position, never proven by any experiment. It is formulated from within a horizon, like everything else. That is precisely what the Encompassing lets us see.

For me

What it changes

This concept, apparently Jaspers's most abstract, has a very everyday consequence: humility of position — not modesty as a pose, but exactness. I always look from somewhere: my era, my language, my trade, my history. That does not make my gaze false; it makes it situated. And someone looking from elsewhere does not see the same thing — not because they are wrong, but because they are elsewhere.

This is the opposite of limp relativism, note it well. The relativist says: all viewpoints are equal, therefore nothing matters. Jaspers says: no viewpoint is the whole, therefore listen to the other — they may hold a piece your window does not show. The Encompassing does not dissolve truth: it makes it common. In a word, it grounds our need for one another.

On what subject am I certain I am right — and from which window, exactly, am I looking?

The Encompassing is what always announces itself without ever becoming an object: that in which we are, and which we cannot look at from outside.
After Karl Jaspers, Reason and Existenz (1935) and Von der Wahrheit (1947) — paraphrase; see the original texts.

Three questions to check

Is the Encompassing the universe?

No. The universe, however immense, remains an object I can think. The Encompassing is what never itself appears and within which everything appears — the background that never becomes a figure.

Why can the “last background” not be reached?

Because the moment you think it, you make it an object — and a new background appears behind. Our knowledge proceeds by cutting out: it grasps only what stands out against a background.

Which error does this concept remedy?

Absolutisation: taking a particular perspective for the whole. “Man is NOTHING BUT neurons / nothing but genes / nothing but desire” — each time, a mode of being is taken for being itself.
