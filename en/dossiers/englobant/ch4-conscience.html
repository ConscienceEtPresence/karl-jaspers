Consciousness in general

Who thinks, when I think that two and two make four?

The question

In thirty seconds

Second mode: the mind that knows. When I calculate, I do not think as myself — anyone in my place would obtain the same result. That is what makes science strong: it holds for everyone. It is also its limit: it can say nothing about the person I am.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

The second mode of the Encompassing that we are. Jaspers calls it consciousness in general (Bewusstsein überhaupt) — a slightly stiff phrase, but the idea is limpid: it is the part of me that thinks, calculates, demonstrates, and which, precisely because it does so well, has nothing personal left about it.

Look at what happens when you solve an equation. You follow rules; if you follow them correctly, you get the result. Your history, your mood, your country, your sore knee change nothing — and just as well: a theorem false on Monday and true on Tuesday would be no theorem. At this level, I am replaceable: any properly instructed mind would do exactly the same.

And here Jaspers reverses the usual view. This interchangeability, which one might take for a poverty, is in fact the greatness of this mode: it is the condition of the universal. Because I can bracket what I am, mathematical truth holds for a twelfth-century Chinese scholar as for an engineer today. Nothing we have in common with all humanity — science, logic, law in its ideal — would exist without this mode. To destroy it in the name of “authenticity” would be a catastrophe, and Jaspers says so bluntly: he was a physician; he owed his life to that mode.

But here is its limit, exact and impassable. Consciousness in general knows objects — that is its definition. It can therefore know everything about me as an object: my body, my brain, my behaviour, my statistics. It can know nothing about me as me, since the I that knows is never inside what is known (remember the split, in chapter 2). It is sovereign in its own order — and mute on the one question that concerns me personally: who am I, and what should I do?

Hence the superstition Jaspers fought all his life: believing that this mode is the only valid one. It is called scientism, and it must not be confused with science. Science says: “here is what my method reaches, and here is where it stops.” Scientism says: “what my method does not reach does not exist.” The first sentence is rigour; the second is a metaphysical belief disguised as a result — and one which, incidentally, could not itself be proven by any experiment.

The metaphor

To feel it

A calculator never makes a mistake, and it is perfectly replaceable: a thousand calculators give the same result. That is exactly what makes it reliable. Now ask it whether you should leave your job. It is not that it lacks power: the question is not of its order.

A concrete situation

The diagnosis and the decision

A woman learns that she carries a genetic mutation which sharply raises her risk of cancer. Medicine does magnificent work here: it gives precise figures, probabilities, options — close monitoring, preventive surgery, nothing. All of it is true, verified, universal: any competent doctor would say the same.

Then comes the moment when the doctor falls silent and says: “it is your decision.” And there, abruptly, consciousness in general has nothing left to offer. No statistic says what risk is worth against the integrity of one's body; no calculation weighs anxiety against mutilation. Yet those are the real terms of the choice.

Two women, the same figures, opposite decisions — and both may be right. Not because science is relative: the figures are the same. But because the decision does not belong to the mode that supplied the figures. Here, in one scene, we see why Jaspers needs to distinguish the modes: at a very precise moment, someone must answer, and no one can do it in their place.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Science progresses. One day it will explain decision, consciousness and values too. Your ‘modes’ are just the provisional borders of our ignorance.”

Everything that must be conceded should be: yes, science will explain the mechanisms of decision better and better — which circuits fire, which biases govern us, how values form in a social brain. That progress is real and has no reason to stop. Jaspers is not betting against it.

But follow precisely what then happens. That knowledge will describe decision as an object: an observable process, in a given subject, seen from outside. Yet the decider's question is not “what mechanism is making me choose?” — it is “what should I do?”. No description, however complete, answers a question that is not descriptive. Even knowing the mechanism of my decision by heart, I shall still have to decide. Knowledge can place itself before me; it cannot place itself in my place.

And notice that this is not a provisional border but a logical one. It is not “we do not know yet” — it is “this is not a matter of knowing”. Asking science what I should do is like asking it whether a painting is beautiful: it will measure the pigments perfectly, and the question will remain untouched. The border does not retreat with progress, because it does not separate the known from the unknown: it separates two orders.

For me

What it changes

There is a very practical use for this chapter: stop expecting answers from where they will not come. How many people search the data for what is not in it? We compare happiness statistics before having a child, read studies on divorce before deciding, ask a personality test which career to choose. It is not absurd — it is simply searching in the wrong mode. No datum will carry my decision, because no datum is me.

And the reverse is true, and just as important: do not despise this mode when it is the right one. On a bridge, a vaccine, an accounting record, a historical fact, consciousness in general is sovereign — and “personal intuition” has no business there. Confusing the two in one direction produces scientism; in the other, conspiracy thinking. Knowing which mode a question calls for is perhaps the most useful skill in this dossier.

Which decision am I postponing by seeking still “more information” — when no information will make it in my place?

Three questions to check

Why is consciousness in general “interchangeable”?

Because it brackets what I am: any instructed mind obtains the same result. This is not a poverty — it is the very condition of the universal.

What is its exact limit?

It knows objects. It can therefore know everything about me as an object (body, brain, behaviour), and nothing about me as me — the I that knows is never inside what is known.

What is the difference between science and scientism?

Science says: here is what my method reaches and where it stops. Scientism says: what my method does not reach does not exist — a metaphysical belief disguised as a result.
