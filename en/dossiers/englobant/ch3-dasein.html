Dossier · The Map of the Encompassing · Chapter 3 of 8

Empirical existence (Dasein)

What am I, as a mere living creature?

The question

In thirty seconds

The first mode of what I am: a living being. I am hungry, I am afraid, I want to last, I age, I shall die. Nothing shameful in that — it is the ground of everything else. The trouble begins only if I believe that is all I am.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

We now enter the map proper. Jaspers distinguishes four modes of the Encompassing that we are — four ways of being ourselves, all real, all simultaneous. The first, the most obvious, is Dasein: a German word meaning literally “being-there”, rendered here as empirical existence — the living being I am, here, now, with this body.

This mode has a very simple logic: to last. The living creature wants to preserve itself, feed, protect itself, reproduce. Its law is vital interest; its horizon is the world as environment — what threatens, what feeds, what is useful. Seen from this mode, everything is resource or danger. And it must be said plainly: this is legitimate. A being without that drive would be dead; any morality claiming to ignore it is a liar's morality.

Jaspers, a physician, never despised Dasein — he knew illness far too well for that. He simply notes its limits, and they are precise. Dasein does not know truth, it knows the useful: “good” is whatever serves life. Nor does it know choice, only preference — it always leans toward the less costly. And it cannot give meaning: it can make a life last, never answer the question of why that life is worth living.

Hence the danger, which is the chapter's real subject. Not having a Dasein — we all have one, and just as well — but being nothing more than that. A whole life can unfold in this single mode: earning, consuming, protecting oneself, being entertained, avoiding pain, prolonging time. Nothing immoral in it, and that is precisely what makes it insidious. It is simply… empty. Jaspers observes that our technical age is organised precisely to make that life comfortable, complete, and unquestioned.

One last word, to avoid a frequent confusion: Jaspers's Dasein is not Heidegger's. For Heidegger, Dasein is the human being in all their depth, open to being. For Jaspers, it is the first, most elementary mode — life that wants to last. Two philosophers, two uses of the same word: when reading one, do not apply the other's vocabulary.

The metaphor

To feel it

The foundations of a house. Without them nothing stands — and nobody despises them. But nobody lives in their foundations either, receives friends in their foundations, looks at the sky from their foundations. The tragedy is not having foundations: it is believing that the house is that.

A concrete situation

Sunday evening

A Sunday evening. The week was good: decent work, shopping done, children healthy, a bit of sport, a series in the evening. Nothing was lacking. No catastrophe, no conflict, no deprivation.

And yet an objectless sadness rises, which everyone knows and no one can name. It is not tiredness — you have slept. It is not a lack — nothing is missing. It is rather a question that cannot manage to form itself: “everything is fine… and so?”

Jaspers would say: Dasein has been satisfied, and nothing else. All vital needs are met — which is precisely why the question rises. It can appear only when survival no longer fills the whole space. That sadness is therefore not a pathology to be treated: it is a signal. Something in me is not fed by what feeds my life. And that something has no other name than me.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“When you are hungry you do not philosophise. Your ‘beyond Dasein’ is a well-fed bourgeois luxury: try telling it to someone with nothing to eat.”

The objection is powerful, and partly right: hunger, fear, exhaustion do indeed close the space of reflection. Jaspers does not deny it — he even adds that making a decent life possible is a political demand, and he campaigned for it throughout his post-war life. No one should have to choose between eating and existing.

But two things force a qualification. The first is a fact, awkward for the objection: those who have philosophised most deeply often did so in destitution, prison, illness or deportation — not in drawing rooms. The question of meaning does not arise when all is well; it arises when comfort can no longer serve as an answer. It is even the reverse of the objection: satiety is rather an excellent anaesthetic.

The second is graver. Saying “when you are hungry you do not philosophise” can slide into: “the poor do not need meaning, they need bread.” That is exactly the reasoning used to treat whole populations as cattle to be fed. Jaspers, who saw where reducing man to Dasein leads, maintains that hunger is an emergency and that the one who is hungry remains an existence, not a stomach. Both together, or nothing.

For me

What it changes

There is a very simple examination to make of your own week. How many of my hours served to last — earning, maintaining, protecting, distracting myself in order to hold on? And how many served something else? This is not a moral question; there is no good score. Most of everyone's hours go to Dasein, and that is how we stay alive.

The question lies elsewhere: is there still a space where something else breathes? A true conversation, a decision owned, an hour given to what serves no purpose. If none remains, life will not collapse — it will carry on perfectly well. That is precisely the problem: Dasein, left alone, never signals that something is missing. Only you can notice.

Of the last hundred hours of my life, how many served to last — and how many to be someone?

Three questions to check

What is Dasein in Jaspers?

Empirical existence: the living creature I am, with this body, here and now. Its law is to last; its criterion is the useful; its horizon, the world as environment — resource or danger.

Is Jaspers's Dasein the same as Heidegger's?

No — a frequent trap. For Heidegger, Dasein is the human being in all their depth; for Jaspers, it is the first, most elementary mode: life that wants to maintain itself.

Where does the danger lie, according to Jaspers?

Not in having a Dasein — we all have one. But in being nothing more: a whole life can unfold in this single mode, comfortable and unquestioned. It is not immoral; it is empty.
