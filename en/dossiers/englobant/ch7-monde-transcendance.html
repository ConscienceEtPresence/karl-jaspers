Dossier · The Map of the Encompassing · Chapter 7 of 8

World and Transcendence

And on the other side: what encompasses me?

The question

In thirty seconds

So far the map described the Encompassing that we are. But we are also encompassed. Two modes here: the world — that horizon which always recedes and never closes; and Transcendence — what carries everything without being carried by anything.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Let us change slopes. The four preceding modes described the being that we are. But we are not alone: something surrounds us, precedes us, will outlive us. Jaspers calls this the Encompassing that being is, and distinguishes two modes within it: the world and Transcendence.

The world first. Beware the false friend, it is a big one: the world, here, is not the physical universe, nor the sum of all objects. It is the horizon in which every object appears — and that horizon has a troubling property: it never closes. Remember chapter 1: each time science establishes a framework, one can ask what lies around it. The world is therefore not a thing we shall one day know entirely; it is the very fact that there is always an “around”. No science will ever be the science of the whole — not through lateness, but by structure.

Transcendence next. It is what carries everything without being carried by anything. Let us say at once what it is not, for the misreadings are automatic: it is not a supreme being somewhere at the back of the universe (that would be an object, a very large one, but an object); it is not a spatial “beyond” behind the clouds; it is not the supernatural, one more strange phenomenon. It is not in the world at all. That is even its definition: the Encompassing of the Encompassing, what never becomes an object for anyone.

Hence a question that must occur to any honest reader: if it appears nowhere, how can Jaspers speak of it at all? His answer is of rare prudence. By two indirect paths, never by proof. First through Existenz (chapter 6): in the highest freedom I experience receiving myself, not being my own foundation — that experience proves nothing, but it points. Then through ciphers: the whole world — a starry night, a piece of music, an old story, the very failure of our constructions — can be read as a script of which Transcendence would be the text. Always suggested. Never demonstrated.

And here is what makes this page precious for any reader, believer or not. Since Transcendence never becomes an object, no one can possess it. The Christian encounters it in Christ, the Muslim in the Qur'an, the Jew in the Law, the atheist in the unconditional demand of truth or justice — and none holds the original of which the others have copies. Jaspers invites no blending of traditions: he merely withdraws from each the right to say that it possesses what, by definition, cannot be possessed.

The metaphor

To feel it

Look at a painting: you see figures, colours, a scene. You will never see the canvas — it is that on which everything appears, and it disappears beneath what it carries. Scrape the paint away to reach it: there is no painting left. The world and Transcendence are that canvas, and we are in the paint.

A concrete situation

The night and the telescope

Two people look at the same starry sky. The first is an astrophysicist: she knows the distance of those lights, their composition, their age, the fact that some no longer exist. Her knowledge is immense, precise, verifiable — and it bears entirely on objects.

The second knows none of that. But she is seized: something, this evening, “speaks” without words. She could not explain what. The next day she will be unable to convey to anyone what was said — the message does not detach from the sign.

Who is right? Both — and that is Jaspers entire. The astrophysicist has the world as an object of science; the other has met a cipher. Nothing is opposed here: they are two modes, and nothing prevents them from being the same person, on the same evening. The only error would be to believe that one cancels the other — that knowledge dissolves wonder, or that wonder excuses us from knowledge.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“Why add Transcendence? The world is enough. You introduce an unverifiable entity we need for explaining precisely nothing.”

A clean objection, and Jaspers does not dodge it: yes, Transcendence explains nothing. It is not a scientific hypothesis competing with physics, it plugs no gap in our knowledge, it advances no research. If the criterion is explanatory usefulness, the objection wins — and Jaspers concedes it without flinching.

But it is not an explanatory need his philosophy observes; it is an experience. That of someone who, in a grave choice, does not feel the author of their own demand: “I could not do otherwise” — not through constraint, but because something imposed itself on them unconditionally. One may say this is an illusion produced by the brain; that is a respectable thesis. Just notice that it too is unverifiable: no one will ever prove that there is nothing behind that experience. Two wagers, no proof.

What Jaspers asks is therefore modest: do not decree the question closed in either direction. The dogmatist says “God is, I know it”. The inverse dogmatist says “there is nothing, I know it”. Both claim the same impossible knowledge. Between them, his position holds in a single sentence: God is for faith, not for knowledge. This is not a middle-of-the-road trick — it is the refusal of a knowledge no one has.

For me

What it changes

This page offers shelter from two kinds of violence, and that is perhaps its most concrete use. That of dogmatism, which wants to impose its image of the absolute as the only true one. And that of closed materialism, which decrees that my deepest presentiment is a mere secretion. Jaspers equips a tenable position: I can take my experience of the “more than everything” seriously without giving up a gram of rigour — provided I accept that it will never be proven.

And it changes how we see other people's belief. If every image of Transcendence is a cipher — a sign, never the thing itself — then the other's tradition ceases to be an error to correct: it becomes another script of the same inaccessible. One can inhabit one's own path with fervour and inner freedom, and read the other's with an attention that is no longer mere tolerance. In an age of crossed fanaticisms, this may be Jaspers's most useful bequest.

Have I ever experienced something that exceeded me — and what did I do with it: deny it, lock it in a dogma, or leave it open?

Three questions to check

Is the “world”, in Jaspers, the physical universe?

No. It is the horizon in which every object appears — and which never closes: one can always ask what lies around. No science will be the science of the whole, not through lateness, but by structure.

Is Transcendence a supreme being somewhere?

No: that would be an object, a very large one, but an object. It is not in the world at all — it is what carries everything without being carried by anything, and never becomes an object for anyone.

By what paths, then, does Jaspers speak of it?

Two indirect paths, never proof: Existenz (in the highest freedom I experience receiving myself) and ciphers (the world read as a script). Always suggested, never demonstrated.
