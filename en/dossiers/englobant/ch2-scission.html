The subject-object split

Why does everything I am conscious of always divide in two?

The question

In thirty seconds

The moment I think something, there is I who think and the thing thought. This cut is the very structure of consciousness — inescapable. But it is not the ground of things: it is what occurs inside the Encompassing, which is neither subject nor object.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

Let us look at what happens whenever we are conscious of anything. There are always two poles: on one side something aimed at (the object — this cup, a memory, an equation, my own hand); on the other the one who aims (the subject — me). Never one without the other. Jaspers calls this the subject-object split (Subjekt-Objekt-Spaltung).

Try to escape it; it is instructive. Think of yourself: there you are, become an object for yourself — and there is a “you” looking at that you-object. Make that looker an object in turn: a new looker appears behind. You can go on indefinitely; there always remains a pole that is not caught, the one from which you catch. The split never closes.

And here is the decisive point, the one this whole chapter exists for: this structure belongs to our consciousness, not necessarily to being. Nothing proves that the real is itself cut into subjects and objects; what is certain is that we cannot grasp anything otherwise. Confusing the two — believing that reality is made the way our way of grasping it is made — is the error Jaspers hunts everywhere.

Hence the Encompassing. Since subject and object always appear together, at one stroke, there must be something within which this split occurs. That something is neither subject nor object — otherwise it would already be one of the two sides. That is the Encompassing: not one more thing, but that of which the split is the manifestation. It is never seen; it is recognised by the fact that the cut exists.

An immediate consequence, decisive for the rest of this dossier: what is not an object can never be known the way a thing is known. Neither the Encompassing, nor Existenz (what I can authentically be), nor Transcendence. Not because they are obscure or mystical — because they are not on the right side of the split. Philosophy therefore does not reach them by proof: it signals toward them, indirectly. Jaspers calls this illumination, not knowledge.

The metaphor

To feel it

A spotlight lights a stage. You see the actors, the sets, every detail. You will never see the light itself — you see only what it lights. Turn the spotlight on itself: you light up its casing, not the fact that it illuminates. Our consciousness is that spotlight, and the Encompassing the light that never shows itself.

A concrete situation

The other's gaze

You are talking with someone you love. For a second, something passes — you are truly together, effortlessly, with no self-monitoring.

Now stop and try to observe that moment: “look, we are having a real moment.” What happened? The moment has just died. By taking it as an object, you stepped out of it: there is once again you on one side and the experience on the other. You observe it only because you are no longer in it.

This is not a curse, it is the split at work. It is the price of consciousness: one cannot both fully live a thing and hold it before oneself. But notice: the moment did take place. Something happened that was neither “me” nor “that”, and of which I have only the memory — objectified, and so impoverished. What took place was the Encompassing, for one second.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“This is old metaphysics. Neuroscience describes the subject perfectly: it is a self-model produced by the brain. Your split is just a cerebral mechanism.”

Seriously objected, and what must be conceded should be: yes, the feeling of being a subject has cerebral correlates; yes, it can be disrupted by damaging certain areas. Jaspers, who spent fifteen years in a psychiatric clinic, knew this at first hand — he saw patients whose self was disintegrating.

But see exactly what the objection does. It takes the subject and turns it into an object of study — a model, a mechanism, a function. That is perfectly legitimate, and it produces real knowledge. Only, in doing so, it places itself on the subject side: someone observes the brain and concludes. The neuroscientist cannot eliminate the subject pole; they merely shift it onto themselves. The pole from which one looks is never in the picture.

And that is the whole thesis: the split cannot be overcome by knowledge, because all knowledge takes place inside it. One can objectify a particular subject (me, you, this patient); one cannot objectify subjectivity as such, for that would require a subject to do it. This is not old metaphysics: it is a remark about structure, as valid in a 2026 laboratory as in 1935.

For me

What it changes

This page has an unexpected practical reach. It explains why certain things slip away the moment we aim at them: sleep, happiness, spontaneity, love. Wanting to fall asleep keeps you awake; watching yourself being happy spoils the happiness; “being natural” on command is impossible. This is not a failure of your will: it is the split. Those things are not objects to be reached — they are states one is in, or not.

The consequence is liberating: there are domains of life where aiming is exactly the wrong method. They are obtained only by doing something else — by giving oneself to a task, to someone, to a moment. This is also why Jaspers offers no self-development technique: there is no method for becoming yourself, because the self is not an object to be manufactured. There are only situations in which one answers — or does not.

What am I chasing relentlessly, which moves away precisely to the extent that I chase it?

Three questions to check

What is the subject-object split?

The structure of all consciousness: the moment I think something, there is the one who aims (subject) and what is aimed at (object). Never one without the other, and never closed.

Is this split the structure of reality?

Nothing proves it. It is the structure of our consciousness — of our way of grasping. Confusing the two (believing the real is made the way our grasp of it is made) is the error Jaspers hunts.

Why can the Encompassing not be known?

Because it is neither subject nor object: it is that within which the split occurs. But we know only objects. Philosophy does not reach it by proof — it signals toward it.
