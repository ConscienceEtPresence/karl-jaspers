Existenz

What, in me, is not replaceable?

The question

In thirty seconds

The fourth mode, and the only irreplaceable one. My body, my reason, my culture: someone else could have all of that. But the one who decides, who answers, who becomes true — no one can be that in my place. And it never becomes an object.

Jaspers’s thought

What he makes of it

We have three modes, and a lack. Dasein (empirical existence) makes me last: but another body would do the same. Consciousness in general makes me know: but any instructed mind would obtain my results. Spirit makes me understand: but another person, in my culture, would understand as I do. At every storey, I am replaceable. Yet there are moments when someone must answer, and no one can do it in their place.

That someone, Jaspers names Existenz — and makes it the fourth mode of the Encompassing that we are. Beware, here, of the central difficulty: this word does not designate a new part of me, a hidden layer to be found by digging. There is nothing to find. Existenz is not something I am the way I am tall or dark-haired: it is something I can become, or miss. Hence Jaspers speaks of possible existence.

It has one property that radically distinguishes it from the other three: it never becomes an object. The first three modes can all be studied — a doctor studies my Dasein, a logician my thinking, a historian my culture. Existenz cannot: the moment I describe it, I am describing a character trait, a behaviour, a psychological fact — that is, something other than it. Remember chapter 2: it is not on the right side of the split. It is not known; it is decided.

How, then, does it show itself? Never in comfort. It awakens in boundary situations (those walls that can be neither fled nor changed: death, suffering, struggle, guilt), in the choice where I myself am at stake, in true communication where someone stands up to me while loving me. And it has a recognisable sign: at such moments, I can no longer take shelter behind a rule, a datum, a role. The famous “I was only obeying” is exactly where Existenz slips away.

One last trait, the strangest and most important. At the moment when I am most myself — not when I do as I please, but when I do what I must — I do not feel that I have manufactured myself: I feel that I receive myself. Jaspers insists: Existenz is not its own origin. It is through that fissure, and through it alone, that his philosophy opens onto Transcendence — of which we shall speak in the next chapter. That is why this mode is the hinge of the whole map: it is the only one in me that touches what is not me.

The metaphor

To feel it

Have a scan: your organs appear. Take an IQ test: your logic appears. Take a cultural test: your world appears. Spend your whole life on the examination table: at the end, the one who agreed to climb onto the table will still be missing.

A concrete situation

The form

A civil servant must sign a decision he knows to be unjust. Everything, in the first three modes, argues for signing. Dasein: it is his salary, his family, his career. Consciousness in general: the procedure is regular, the file is compliant, no rule is broken. Spirit: in the culture of his office, this is how it is done, everyone would sign, making a scene would be out of place.

Three real modes, three reasonings sound in their own order. And yet something in him says no — without being able to lean on any of the three. He can neither prove he is right, nor invoke a rule, nor appeal to his culture. If he refuses, he will refuse alone, without guarantee, and probably at his own expense.

That is exactly where Existenz stands — in that no which leans on nothing objective. And notice: it does not tell him what to do; it supplies no rule of conduct. It simply puts him in a position to answer. He can sign. Many sign. The mode exists precisely as a possibility — never as an automatism.

The honest objection

“Yes, but…”

“This ‘mode that cannot be observed’ is very convenient: nothing proves it, nothing can refute it. It is unverifiable metaphysics — exactly what philosophy was supposed to abandon.”

A severe objection, perfectly sound in its own terms: yes, Existenz is unverifiable; yes, no experiment will bring it to light; yes, Jaspers grants this from the outset, without defending himself. If experimental verification were required in order to speak of something, one would have to fall silent.

But let us examine the rule the objection applies. “Speak only of what can be verified” cannot itself be verified — it is a philosophical decision, not a result. And above all, let us try to hold it to the end: we should then have to stop saying that the civil servant ought to have refused, stop holding anyone responsible, stop making any difference between the one who signed and the one who risked their post. No one goes that far — not even the most rigorous, when it concerns their own life or their own children.

Jaspers's point is therefore subtler than a belief: he observes that we all live, in practice, presupposing Existenz — we judge, we answer, we demand. He does not ask us to believe in it; he asks us to notice that we already live by it. And he certainly does not claim to know it: he illuminates it, he says; he does not demonstrate it. That may be the only honest position on the matter.

For me

What it changes

There is a very simple, almost brutal test flowing from this chapter: among my decisions this year, which would have been exactly the same had someone else been in my place — with my job, my milieu, my information? Almost all of them, no doubt. That is normal: life is largely execution, and there is no shame in it.

But if none remains — not one in which I answered for myself, without guarantee and without a rule to cover me — then something has not yet begun. And here is the most disturbing thing in Jaspers's thought: nothing will signal it to me. My life will carry on perfectly well, useful, informed, cultivated. Existenz never complains. It waits.

Which decision in my life could have been made only by me — and when was the last one?

Three questions to check

Why are the first three modes insufficient?

Because at every storey I am replaceable: another body would last, another mind would calculate the same, another member of my culture would understand as I do. Yet there are moments when no one can answer in my place.

Is Existenz a hidden part of me?

No — there is nothing to find by digging. It is not something I am the way I am dark-haired: it is what I can become, or miss. Hence the phrase: possible existence.

Why can it never be studied?

Because it is not on the right side of the subject-object split. The moment I describe it, I describe a character trait or a behaviour — something other than it. It is not known: it is decided.
