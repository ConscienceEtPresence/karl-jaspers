Jaspers in everyday life

“I am afraid of dying”

It comes at night, without warning, and it is not a thought: it is a coldness. Then it passes, and you go back to living as though nothing had happened — which is perhaps the strangest part.

What Jaspers will not say

That death is nothing, that you should not think about it, or that you should think about it constantly. That wisdom means getting used to it. Jaspers never said you get used to it — he lived sixty years with a heart that was supposed to kill him at thirty.

What he offers

A distinction nobody makes, and it changes everything. There are two anxieties, and we confuse them. The anxiety of no longer being here — the world going on without me, the table being cleared. That one no philosophy removes, and he does not claim to. And the anxiety of not having been — of having missed it, of having lived a life that was not mine. That second one is not a fear of death: it is my life demanding me, now. It is not calmed by thinking about death. It is calmed by living differently. And Jaspers adds this, not as consolation but as observation: the death of someone close and my own death have nothing in common. The first is a tear in the world. The second is not an event in my life — I will not be there to attend it.
