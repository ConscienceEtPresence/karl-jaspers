Jaspers in everyday life

“I am ill”

The body decides, and you follow. Plans are put away, people become awkward, time changes texture. And somewhere, a small voice asking: why me.

What Jaspers will not say

That it is a trial sent to make you grow. That it has a hidden meaning. That you attracted it, or that you will recover if you think correctly. Jaspers was a physician: he saw what those sentences do to the sick, and he holds them to be cruelties dressed as comfort.

What he offers

First a useful refusal: suffering has no meaning. It is sent by no one. It is there, that is all, and it is not your doing. Then an observation he makes without selling it: it can awaken. Not always — it also crushes, and he says so. But sometimes it decants: the false urgencies fall away by themselves, you stop postponing, and you suddenly see who is really there. Finally the one thing he genuinely offers, and it must be taken at its word: appropriate it. Not accept it — the word is too soft, and it sounds like surrender. Stop living as though it were a parenthesis before real life. It is your life, right now. That is not a consolation. It is what allows you still to be someone inside it.
