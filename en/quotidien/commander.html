Jaspers in everyday life

“He is the one who decides”

At work, or at home. He decides, you follow. It is not violent, it is often pleasant. Only, you know in advance how it will end.

What Jaspers will not say

That this is necessarily tyranny on one side and weakness on the other. Jaspers does not turn the observation into an accusation — he shows something else, more disturbing for both.

What he offers

He examines what ruling and serving does to both. The one who commands no longer hears anything that contradicts him: he is protected, and that protection encloses him — he ends up not knowing what is true, since everyone agrees with him. The one who serves no longer has to decide: he is relieved, and that relief costs him exactly what he thought he was sparing himself. Neither is anyone before the other any more. And here is what makes the chapter hard: it is not always imposed. People often take that place, because it rests. Obeying is comfortable, and so is commanding. What costs is standing in front of someone who can contradict you.
