Jaspers in everyday life

“My boss humiliates me”

One thing first, very clearly

This is not therapy, and Jaspers would be the first to say so — he was a psychiatrist before he was a philosopher, and he never confused the two. If something is heavy, lasting, if you no longer sleep, if you are thinking of ending it: it is not a philosopher you need, it is a doctor, and now. Philosophy does not heal. It illuminates — which is not the same thing, and replaces nothing.

In front of the others, often. And the worst is not the anger — it is what comes after: the shame of having taken it, of having said nothing, of going home with that.

What Jaspers will not say

That you should take a step back. That it isn't serious. That it says more about him than about you. Those phrases are true and perfectly useless — they ask you not to feel what you feel.

What he offers

Jaspers looks elsewhere. What was done to you, he would say, is that you were treated as an object — a function, an output, a cog. And your anger is not touchiness: it is the part of you that knows it is not one. He calls it dignity: it depends neither on your position, nor your results, nor his judgement — that is its very definition. Then comes the real question, and it is hard: in the second that follows, what do I do? Staying silent out of calculation and staying silent while knowing it are not the same act — even if the camera sees no difference. The second one, one day, speaks.
