Jaspers in everyday life

“I say nothing”

You could have said it. The sentence was right there. And you did not say it — and tonight you no longer know whether that was wisdom or cowardice.

What Jaspers will not say

That silence is always a strength. That speech is vulgar and silence deep. Jaspers saw too many prudent silences, in a country that was keeping quiet, to find that elegant.

What he offers

He distinguishes two silences, and everything is there. There is the full silence: two people say nothing because there is nothing to say — they are there, and the bond holds without words. That one is a high form of communication. And there is the silence that withdraws: I do not say it, because saying it would cost. It looks exactly the same. From outside, no one can tell them apart — and often the one keeping silent cannot either. The test Jaspers offers comes down to one question, and you can ask it tonight: if the other could hear it at no cost, would I say it? If yes, your silence is not full. It is waiting for something.
