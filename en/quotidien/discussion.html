Jaspers in everyday life

“We argue, and nobody listens”

Two hours of debate. Solid arguments on both sides. And at the end each leaves with exactly what they arrived with — only better armed.

What Jaspers will not say

That it would be enough to listen more, or to act in good faith. The people around that table are in good faith, and that is precisely the problem.

What he offers

One observation explains almost everything: people can argue in several registers, and the registers do not communicate. You can argue for an interest — then the best negotiator wins. You can argue by pure reason — arguments clash, impeccably, and nobody is engaged: you can be wrong at no cost, so you are never wrong. You can argue for a shared cause, and that is already better. And there is a last register where someone risks something: there, being contradicted hurts, because you did not put an argument on the table — you put yourself. It is the only register in which anyone changes their mind. The sign is infallible: ask yourself what you are risking in this discussion. If the answer is nothing, you are not arguing — you are training.
