On to the books

Reading Jaspers

Where to start, and in what order

One piece of advice first

One piece of advice first

Do not start with the major work. Philosophy (1932) runs to three volumes and presupposes everything else: you drown, you close it, you conclude that Jaspers is unreadable. He is not — he even wrote, on purpose, for the radio, the simplest book a philosopher of that rank has ever given. Start there.

Three doors, depending on who you are

Three doors, depending on who you are

You have never read philosophyWay to Wisdom (1950) — a hundred pages, twelve talks written for radio, without a word of jargon. Then The Question of German Guilt (1946), just as short, and it hits hard. With those two you already have the essential of the man.
You want to enter the thoughtReason and Existenz (1935): three lectures, the shortest door to the heart of it. Then Man in the Modern Age (1931) for the diagnosis, and The Perennial Scope of Philosophy (1948) for the relation to Transcendence. Only then Philosophy (1932) — and begin with volume II, the illumination of Existenz, the most alive of the three.
You are a student or a teacherVon der Wahrheit (1947) is the massif: a thousand pages on truth and the Encompassing, still never translated in full into English. The Origin and Goal of History (1949) for the philosophy of history. And General Psychopathology (1913) if you want to see where it all started — the distinction between explaining and understanding was born there.

The whole work, in order

The whole work, in order

The dates are those of German publication. English titles vary between editions; the original title is given underneath to remove any doubt.

1913
General Psychopathology
Allgemeine Psychopathologie

Written at thirty, still used by psychiatrists. It is not philosophy — but all his philosophy comes out of it: here he separates explaining from understanding.

1919
Psychology of Worldviews
Psychologie der Weltanschauungen

The book of the crossing. Boundary situations and shells are already there. It caused a scandal: a physician had no business writing this.

1931
Man in the Modern Age
Die geistige Situation der Zeit

Short, devastating, written before screens: the masses, the apparatus, the human being who functions without existing. The diagnosis still holds.

1932
Philosophy (three volumes)
Philosophie

The major work: world orientation, illumination of Existenz, metaphysics. The massif — not the door you enter by first.

1935
Reason and Existenz
Vernunft und Existenz

Three lectures, the shortest door to the heart of it. He sets out from Kierkegaard and Nietzsche, his two “exceptions”.

1938
Philosophy of Existence
Existenzphilosophie

Three lectures given just before he was banned from publishing. Brief and dense.

1946
The Question of German Guilt
Die Schuldfrage

A hundred pages that shaped how collective responsibility is thought to this day. Readable by anyone, with no prerequisites.

1947
On Truth
Von der Wahrheit

Over a thousand pages on truth and the Encompassing. A life's work — and a book one never finishes.

1948
The Perennial Scope of Philosophy
Der philosophische Glaube

Six lectures. What can be believed without dogma, and why faith that mistakes itself for knowledge turns into fanaticism.

1949
The Origin and Goal of History
Vom Ursprung und Ziel der Geschichte

The Axial Age, the unity of humanity, technology. Written in the ruins by a man who had just seen where the myth of a chosen people leads.

1950
Way to Wisdom
Einführung in die Philosophie

Twelve radio talks. A hundred pages. The best book to begin with — and probably the hardest to write.

1957
The Great Philosophers
Die großen Philosophen

Socrates, Buddha, Confucius, Jesus, then Plato, Kant, Augustine, Nietzsche… Not doctrines: forms of human greatness.

1958
The Atom Bomb and the Future of Man
Die Atombombe und die Zukunft des Menschen

His political summa. No technical solution will solve a problem that is not technical.

1962
Philosophical Faith and Revelation
Der philosophische Glaube angesichts der Offenbarung

The great book of his dialogue with theology. Demanding.

1970
Ciphers of Transcendence
Chiffren der Transzendenz

Posthumous: his 1961 lectures, the last ones. Short, and among the most beautiful.

One last thing

One last thing

Jaspers did not write to be known. He wrote to be used — and he says so himself: to read a philosopher is not to repeat their answers, it is to enter their questions. If a book falls from your hands, never mind: take another. There is no wrong order — only the order that sets you on the way.
