Concept · The heart of his thought

Existenz

Existenz — possible existence

1 · In one sentence

The essential

Beneath all my roles, habits and labels lies what I can be: someone who decides, who answers for their life, who becomes true. Jaspers calls this Existenz — and no one can be it in my place.

2 · The experience

A moment everyone knows

Make a list of what you are: an age, a job, a nationality, a family role, a temperament, a medical file, a purchase history. All of it is true. All of it can be filed, measured, compared. And yet a discomfort remains: you can feel that this list, however complete, is not you. Something always overflows.

That something shows itself at precise moments. The day everyone expected one thing of you, and you said no — unable to prove you were right, but knowing that to give in would have been to lose yourself. The day you forgave what seemed unforgivable, or kept a promise that cost you dearly. In those instants it was not your "personality" acting: it was deeper. It was you in the act of becoming you.

3 · The thought

What Jaspers makes of it

Jaspers distinguishes the levels of what we are. I am first of all Dasein (literally "being-there"): a living creature that wants to last, to eat, to be safe. I am also consciousness in general: a mind that thinks, calculates, knows — interchangeable with any other mind, since two and two make four for everyone. I am furthermore spirit in the sense of ideas: a member of a culture, a bearer of shared values. Science can study all three levels; they are real, necessary — and insufficient.

For none of them explains the moment of the irreducible no, of the choice that could not be calculated, of the faithfulness that brought no reward. There stands Existenz: not a thing I have, but a possibility I am. Jaspers insists on this: Existenz never becomes an object — the moment I describe it, I am describing only a character trait, a role, a psychological fact. It cannot be proven; it can only be decided. That is why he speaks of "possible existence": I can spend my whole life missing it, content with merely functioning.

How does it awaken? Rarely in comfort. It awakens in boundary situations (those impassable walls: death, suffering, struggle, guilt), in true communication (that speech in which two beings reveal themselves to one another), and in choices fully taken on. And it does not entirely belong to itself: at the very moment I am most myself, I have the paradoxical feeling of receiving myself — as though what is most my own were given to me. Through that breach, Existenz opens onto what Jaspers calls Transcendence (that which exceeds every object and all knowledge — some will say God, others Being, others will leave it unnamed).

4 · Resonance

Living with this idea

This idea first changes the way we look at ourselves. It forbids two kinds of laziness: reducing ourselves to our labels ("I'm anxious, that's just how I am", "I'm too old to change") and dreaming ourselves into a pure free spirit floating above our life. Existenz is neither the label nor the dream: it is what, in a real situation, with this body and this history, can still answer and choose.

It also changes the way we look at others. If every person is a possible existence, then no one — not the struggling pupil, not the narrow-minded colleague, not the old man repeating his stories — is ever reduced to what I see of them. To treat someone as a possible existence is to speak to them as to someone who can still become. Here we recognise Jaspers the physician, who refused to reduce his patients to their diagnosis; each of us can do the same, every day, without any diploma.
