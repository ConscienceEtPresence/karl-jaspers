Concept · Metaphysics

Transcendence

Transzendenz

1 · In one sentence

The essential

Beyond everything that can be seen, proven and possessed, there is that toward which existence transcends itself — which some name God, others Being, others leave unnamed. Jaspers calls it Transcendence: never an object, always a call.

2 · The experience

A moment everyone knows

On certain evenings — before a starry sky, a piece of music, the birth of a child or the face of a dying person at peace — a silent evidence passes through us: "there is more than what I see." Not a proof — a presentiment. A second later, the critical mind reclaims its rights: illusion? emotion? brain chemistry? And there we stand, torn between two shames: the shame of believing too quickly, and the shame of betraying what, for one instant, was the most real thing in the world.

This tension is not a weakness: for Jaspers it is the metaphysical situation of modern human beings. We can no longer take religious images as literal descriptions; nor can we amputate human experience of its highest dimension. We must think between the two — and that is exactly where Jaspers builds.

3 · The thought

What Jaspers makes of it

The starting point is almost logical. Everything I know, I know as an object: a thing set before me, within the world. But the whole of Being cannot be one object among others — what would contain it? Jaspers names the Encompassing (das Umgreifende) that horizon which contains everything and is contained by nothing. And at the ground of the Encompassing, that which carries everything while being carried by nothing: Transcendence. By definition it can never become an object of science — not because it is irrational, but because all science bears on objects, and it is not one.

How, then, to approach it? Not by demonstration — Jaspers regards all "proofs of God's existence" as instructive failures: if they proved anything, God would be an object, and therefore not God. One approaches it by two paths. Through Existenz first (what I can authentically be): in the highest freedom I experience receiving myself — not being my own foundation; that experience points toward what founds me. Through ciphers next: the whole world — the starry night, the work of art, even the foundering of our constructions — can be read as a script of which Transcendence would be the text, always suggested, never demonstrated.

Hence a consequence Jaspers fully accepts: each person's Transcendence remains historical — lived from within their situation, their language, their tradition. The Christian encounters it in Christ, the Muslim in the Qur'an, the Jew in the Law, the atheist in the unconditional demand of truth or justice — and none possesses the original of which the others would own mere copies. This is the foundation of his philosophical faith (that trust without dogma which runs through all traditions): no one holds a monopoly on Transcendence, because it is no one's property.

4 · Resonance

Living with this idea

This thought first offers shelter from two kinds of violence. That of dogmatism, which wants to impose its image of the absolute as the only one; and that of closed materialism, which decrees that my deepest presentiment is nothing but a secretion of the brain. Jaspers equips a tenable position: I can take seriously my experience of the "more than everything" without giving up an inch of intellectual rigour — provided I accept that it will never be proven.

It also transforms our relation to other people's beliefs. If every image of Transcendence is a cipher (a sign, never the thing itself), then the other's tradition ceases to be an error to correct: it becomes another script of the same inaccessible. One may remain rooted in one's own path — Jaspers invites no soft syncretism — while listening to the other as to someone who, from another slope, is looking at the same mountain. In an age of fanaticisms and crossed contempts, this position is perhaps one of the most precious a philosopher has bequeathed us.
