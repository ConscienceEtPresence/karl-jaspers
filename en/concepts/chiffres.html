Concept · Metaphysics

Ciphers

Chiffren der Transzendenz

1 · In one sentence

The essential

The world can be read like a script: the starry night, a work of art, a myth, a failure gone through — so many signs in which the absolute lets itself be sensed without ever letting itself be proven. Jaspers calls these signs ciphers.

2 · The experience

A moment everyone knows

One and the same mountain can be, for the geologist, a fold of datable rock; for the hiker, a goal for effort; and on one particular evening, for that very same hiker, something else entirely: a presence, a gravity, something that "speaks" without words. Nothing has changed in the mountain. Something has changed in the reading.

Everyone knows those instants when the world becomes eloquent: a piece of music that seems to hold more truth than any speech, an old story — biblical, quranic, mythological — that grips us though we do not "believe" it literally, the gaze of a newborn. And everyone knows the instant after: it is impossible to explain to someone else what it said. The message cannot be detached from the sign. It is exactly this strange property that Jaspers thought through.

3 · The thought

What Jaspers makes of it

Recall the problem: Transcendence (that ground of Being which never becomes an object in the world) can, by definition, neither be seen nor proven. How then could we speak of it at all, even among ourselves? Jaspers's answer: through ciphers. A cipher is a reality of the world — a thing, a work, a story, an event — that begins to signal toward what exceeds the world. Neither a proof nor a mere decorative symbol: a script whose complete key we shall never possess.

Anything can become a cipher: nature (the starry sky, the sea, the silence of deserts), history, art — Jaspers regarded certain works as metaphysics made sensible —, the myths and religious figures of every tradition, and even foundering itself (that final failure of every human construction which, faced squarely, becomes the deepest cipher). But take care: nothing is a cipher in itself. The geologist's mountain remains an object; it becomes a cipher only for an Existenz (what I can authentically be) that reads it from its own depth. The cipher is an encounter, not a property of things.

From this follows Jaspers's golden rule, his permanent warning: never turn the cipher into a thing. When a tradition takes its images literally — when the sign becomes dogma, proof, an object of possession — Jaspers speaks of undue embodiment: the finger pointing at the moon gets worshipped in its place. For him this is the common root of superstition and fanaticism. Conversely, whoever throws away all images because "none of them is true" deprives themselves of the only language human beings have for their relation to the absolute. The right way: keep the images, and hold them in suspense — speaking, never possessed.

4 · Resonance

Living with this idea

This idea first restores to daily life its possible depth. It is not a matter of adding practices to one's life, but of letting, now and then, a second reading open up: this sunset I photograph mechanically — can I let it speak to me for one minute, without commenting on it? This music that overwhelms me — can I take it seriously as a truth, and not merely as a pleasure? Availability to ciphers is a form of attention — almost a hygiene of the soul, open to everyone, with no membership required.

It also yields a remarkably just position toward religious traditions — one's own and other people's. Their stories, figures and rites cease to be either literal truths to defend or fables to liquidate: they become again what they were for their greatest spiritual minds — ciphers, a script of the unsayable. One can then inhabit one's own tradition with fervour and inner freedom, and read the other's with a respect that is no longer mere tolerance, but attention to the same mystery differently written.

Not to be confused

What it is not

  • Numbers or codes: "cipher" translates the German Chiffre — a sign to be read, nothing to do with mathematics, cryptography or numerology.
  • Proofs: a cipher suggests and signals; it never demonstrates anything.
  • Decorative symbols: it does not prettily "represent" an idea — it makes Being speak, for the one who reads it in depth.
  • A code to be cracked once and for all: there is no decryption table — the cipher stays open, or dies.

What it is: a reality of the world — nature, work, story, failure — that becomes eloquent and lets us sense what exceeds the world.
