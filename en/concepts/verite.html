Concept · Reason and Existenz

Truth

Wahrheit — Von der Wahrheit

1 · In one sentence

The essential

Truth is not a treasure one possesses: it is a way one travels — and never travels alone. Whatever presents itself as total and final truth is always, already, a lie.

2 · The experience

A moment everyone knows

"It's been proven." "Everyone has their own truth." We swing endlessly between these two slogans, and neither holds. The first forgets that the greatest questions — how to live? what is worth what? what may I hope? — have never been settled by any proof. The second makes all discussion pointless: if your truth and mine are equal on principle, why should I still talk to you?

And yet everyone has lived moments when "true" meant something higher than both slogans. The moment we finally admit to ourselves what we knew without wanting to know it. The moment when, in a demanding conversation, a shared evidence arises that neither of us brought in. Truth, in those moments, is not a content we recite: it is an event that changes us. That is the truth Jaspers starts from.

3 · The thought

What Jaspers makes of it

Jaspers's guiding idea — he devoted a book of more than a thousand pages to it, Von der Wahrheit (On Truth) — is that truth does not have one meaning but several, according to the level of ourselves that is speaking. For the living creature I am (Dasein, the being-there that wants to last), "true" is what serves life: useful, effective. For consciousness in general (the mind that thinks and calculates), true is what can be proven and holds for everyone: mathematics, the sciences. For spirit (the member of a culture), true is what makes sense within a whole: a right work of art, an institution faithful to its idea. Three legitimate truths — and none suffices.

For at the level of Existenz (what I can authentically be, that ground which decides and answers), truth changes nature: it is no longer what I observe, but what I become. An existential truth cannot be demonstrated; it is lived — you recognise it by the fact that it commits and transforms the one who carries it. And here Jaspers sets his most important lock: this truth is never private property. It becomes true only when tested in communication (that "loving struggle" in which two beings question each other without complacency and without tactics). Hence his formula: truth is what binds us together. A conviction I refuse to submit to another's testing is already rotting into fanaticism.

A dizzying but liberating consequence: total truth is not available to us. We are historical beings, situated, on the way — the whole truth would be the end of the way, and so the end of us. Jaspers does not say that anything goes (each level has its rigorous criteria, and error remains error); he says that the ultimate is given to us in movement, never in stock. That is why the motto of this whole site is his: to philosophize is to be on the way.

4 · Resonance

Living with this idea

First fruit, very concrete: sorting our debates. How many quarrels come from mixing the levels? A scientific datum is pitted against a lived meaning, a dogma against a proof, an emotion against a statistic — guaranteed dialogue of the deaf. Simply asking "which truth are we talking about here?" defuses half of all conflicts on its own — in the family as on social media.

Second fruit: a personal hygiene of truth. Distrust those of your certainties that have never been exposed to anyone. Actively seek out the interlocutor who thinks otherwise — not to convert them, but because my truth needs them in order to ripen. And accept being on the way: do not demand of yourself the perfect, final position on everything; it is not part of our condition. Jaspers's truth does not make one sceptical: it makes one a seeker — firm on the demand, humble about possession. Here we recognise the man who wrote a thousand pages on truth and refused to the end to turn them into a system.

Not to be confused

What it is not

  • Relativism ("everyone has their own truth"): each level of truth keeps its rigorous criteria — error remains error.
  • Scientism ("only the proven is true"): the most vital questions have never been settled by any proof.
  • A definitive possession: whatever presents itself as total, closed truth is already a lie.
  • A private affair: a conviction never exposed to others rots into fanaticism.

What it is: a way with several levels, culminating in what I become — and true only when tested in communication.
