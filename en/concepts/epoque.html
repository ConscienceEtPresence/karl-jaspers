Concept · Diagnosis of the present

The spiritual situation of the age

die geistige Situation der Zeit

1 · In one sentence

The essential

Technology, mass organisation and entertainment make life easier — and human beings more interchangeable. The danger of our age is not first of all catastrophe: it is functioning so well that we forget to exist.

2 · The experience

A moment everyone knows

An ordinary day: woken by an alarm, optimised commutes, work in a constant flow, messages processed on an assembly line, an evening filled by a screen that knows better than we do what we will "like". Nothing dramatic — everything is comfortable, efficient, connected. And yet, switching off at night, that diffuse impression: the day went by without me. I functioned. Who lived?

Another sign of the times: we feel more and more like copies. Profiled by algorithms, polled by categories, replaceable at work, summoned to hold the opinions of our "side". The anger rising everywhere — which each of us can feel within too — comes partly from this: from the humiliation of being treated, and of treating oneself, as a number in a series. Jaspers described this world... in 1931. Before screens, before algorithms. That is what makes his diagnosis so precious: he saw the root, not merely the symptoms.

3 · The thought

What Jaspers makes of it

In Man in the Modern Age (1931), Jaspers describes the coming of what he calls the order of mass existence: to feed, heal and organise millions of human beings, a gigantic technical and bureaucratic apparatus is required. That apparatus is an achievement — Jaspers, a physician, despises neither science nor technology. But it has one law: in order to function, it must make everything calculable, ourselves included. It manufactures functions, profiles, averages. Within it the human being becomes pure Dasein (mere life that wants to last and consume), taken in hand from birth to death — and the question "who am I, irreplaceably?" no longer has anywhere to be asked.

The subtlest part is the complicity that sets in. The apparatus does not oppress us at first: it relieves us. It spares us decisions, fills the void, supplies ready-to-wear opinions and bottomless entertainment. Fleeing the anxiety of existing into functioning and distraction — that is the permanent temptation, and no dictatorship is needed for it. After Hiroshima, Jaspers hardened the diagnosis (The Atom Bomb and the Future of Man, 1958): technology now gives humanity the means of its own abolition. An engineer's reason will not suffice; what is needed is what he calls a change in man himself — a conversion of consciousness, each person becoming genuinely responsible.

But — and this is Jaspers through and through — the diagnosis is not a condemnation. The age of the masses is also the first in which all human beings can reach knowledge, communicate across borders, discover a common history (the Axial Age — that simultaneous awakening of humanity around 500 BC — becomes at last visible to everyone). The situation is not a fate: it is our collective boundary situation (that wall which can be neither fled nor changed, only assumed) — and like every boundary situation, it can lull or awaken. Everything, he says, is decided in what each person, irreplaceably, chooses to make of it.

4 · Resonance

Living with this idea

First, do not mistake the battle. It is not about hating technology or going back to candlelight — there is nothing nostalgic about Jaspers. It is about putting the apparatus back in its place: a means, never a measure. A practical question to ask often: in this activity — this news feed, this optimisation, this meeting — am I using the tool, or is the tool using me?

Second, cultivate the acts the machine cannot perform in our place — that is the very definition of existence (Existenz: what I can authentically be). A decision fully assumed without a poll. A true conversation, face to face, without a screen (communication). An hour of silence, of slow reading, of presence to what serves no purpose — a sky, a piece of music (ciphers, those signs in which the absolute lets itself be sensed). These are not "wellness breaks": they are resistance in the precise sense — the places where one becomes someone again. In the age of algorithms, every act of this kind is a small victory of the human being over the series.

Not to be confused

What it is not

  • A condemnation of technology: Jaspers, a physician, owed his life to science — he refuses the romantic contempt for machines.
  • Nostalgia for the good old days: the age of the masses is also the first chance of a genuinely united humanity.
  • Catastrophism: the main danger is not collapse, but comfortable sleep.
  • A fate: the situation is not destiny — everything is decided in what each of us makes of it.

What it is: a lucid diagnosis of the age of the apparatus and the masses — our collective boundary situation, which can lull or awaken.
