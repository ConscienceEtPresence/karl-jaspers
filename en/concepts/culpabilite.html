Concept · Practical philosophy

Guilt

die Schuldfrage

1 · In one sentence

The essential

"Guilty" does not mean one single thing: Jaspers distinguishes four kinds of guilt — criminal, political, moral, metaphysical — each with its own judge. To confuse these planes is either to accuse everyone or to accuse no one; to distinguish them is finally to be able to answer for oneself.

2 · The experience

A moment everyone knows

A scandal breaks — in a company, an institution, a country. At once two choruses rise. "All guilty!" cries one: the executives, the employees, the customers, those who knew, those who should have known. "I did nothing!" answers the other: I broke no law, I was only following orders, everyone did the same. The two choruses exhaust themselves against each other, and nothing moves forward — not justice, not truth, not anyone.

On a smaller scale, everyone knows this fog from the inside. The workplace bullying I witnessed and said nothing about; the murky part of a family inheritance I benefit from; the ecological disaster my ordinary life contributes to. Am I guilty? The word seems both too strong and too weak. A tool was missing to cut through this fog — Jaspers forged it in 1946, in a Germany in ruins, for the heaviest question of the century.

3 · The thought

What Jaspers makes of it

Criminal guilt, first: I broke a law, through specific acts. Its judge is the court; its answer, punishment. It is strictly individual — no one is a criminal by belonging. Political guilt, next: I answer for the deeds of the state whose citizen I am, even if I committed nothing myself — for I lived under that order, profited from it or failed to prevent it. Its judge is the victor, or history; its answer, reparation. It is collective, but it is not an inherited shame: it is a responsibility to be assumed.

Moral guilt: my own acts — including obedience, silence, the small daily cowardices — fall under my conscience. "An order is an order" is never an absolution: the judge here is my conscience, enlightened by communication with those who love me enough to be frank (that "loving struggle" which Jaspers makes the condition of any truth about oneself). Its answer: repentance and renewal. Finally, metaphysical guilt, the deepest: there exists a solidarity among human beings as human beings, by which I am affected by every injustice committed in my presence in the world. If I survive where others were murdered, and did not risk everything to prevent it, something in me knows it — not as a punishable offence, but as a wound of being. Its judge, says Jaspers, is neither the court nor conscience: it is God, or whatever each of us places under that name — Transcendence (that ground of Being before which existence knows itself held).

Once the tool is forged, everything falls back into place. Criminals can be judged without condemning an entire people (Jaspers rejected the thesis of collective criminal guilt). Political responsibility can be assumed without being crushed by an indistinct shame. Moral self-examination is owed without waiting for a court to do it — the court has no right to. And metaphysical guilt can be allowed to do its work: not to destroy us, but to transform us — Jaspers saw in it the source of a new humility, alone capable of refounding a community after disaster.

4 · Resonance

Living with this idea

This 1946 tool serves every day. Before each "affair" — national or office-sized — ask the four questions in order: who committed acts (criminal)? who must repair on behalf of the collective (political)? what does my conscience say of my own part — silences included (moral)? and what does this teach me about my solidarity with all (metaphysical)? This simple sorting makes us at once more just toward others — no more wholesale condemnations — and more demanding toward ourselves: no more cheap absolutions.

For here is the most fruitful reversal: guilt faced squarely is a boundary situation (one of those walls that can be neither fled nor changed — see that page), and like every boundary situation it can either lull us or awaken us. Denial lulls; vague shame crushes; but guilt distinguished and assumed transforms. Jaspers demonstrated it at the scale of a country; each of us can verify it at the scale of a life: our acknowledged faults are often the exact place where we became better — not despite them, but through them.

Not to be confused

What it is not

  • Diffuse shame: feeling vaguely guilty about everything means being responsible for nothing — Jaspers distinguishes precisely to make responsibility possible.
  • Collective criminal guilt: no people, family or group is criminal as a whole — crime is always individual.
  • A weapon against others: the four guilts serve first for self-examination, not for accusing others.
  • Morbid guilt-tripping: the goal is not to be crushed, but to be transformed.

What it is: four distinct planes — criminal, political, moral, metaphysical — each with its own judge, so as to answer for oneself justly.
