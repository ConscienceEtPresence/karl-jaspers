Concept · Illumination of Existenz

Freedom

Freiheit

1 · In one sentence

The essential

I am not free the way a stone is grey — a property one possesses. I become free every time I truly choose; and in that choice, it is myself I am choosing.

2 · The experience

A moment everyone knows

We use the word "free" for very different things. Free like a Saturday with no obligations. Free like a country without dictatorship. Free like a choice among thirty brands of cereal. Yet everyone senses that the real question lies elsewhere. One can have all of that — time, rights, options — and feel deeply captive: of one's fears, one's persona, other people's gaze, one's own history.

Conversely, we sometimes feel most free precisely where, from the outside, we have the least choice. The person who decides to tell the truth when lying would be so easy. The one who stays at a dying friend's bedside when everything in them wants to flee. The one who, like Jaspers refusing to divorce Gertrud under Nazi threat, has "no choice" — and yet, in that non-choice, is freer than all his persecutors. That is the enigma Jaspers wants to illuminate.

3 · The thought

What Jaspers makes of it

First move: Jaspers withdraws freedom from the realm of provable things. Science will never find freedom in the brain, and rightly so: it knows only objects and causes, and freedom is neither an object nor a causal link. But the reverse is also true: no science will ever prove that I am not free. Freedom cannot be demonstrated; it is attested — in the very act of choosing. It is the same movement as with Existenz (what I can authentically be, which never becomes an object): freedom is precisely Existenz in action.

Second move: distinguishing real freedom from its counterfeits. Arbitrariness — doing whatever I want because I can — is not freedom but its shadow: a weathervane is not free. Nor is the mere "consumer's choice" among prefabricated options. For Jaspers, the genuinely free choice is the one in which I choose myself: where my decision does not merely select an option but decides who I shall be from now on. Such choices are rare, and they can be recognised by one sign: no calculation, no poll, no expert can settle them. No one can make them in my place.

Third move, the most surprising: at the summit of freedom stands obligation. When someone says "I could not have done otherwise" after an act of courage or faithfulness, they are not describing a constraint: they are describing an inner necessity, an unconditional demand. And Jaspers notes the paradox: in those moments I experience not having created myself — my freedom is as if given to me. That is why he can write that where I am truly free, I touch what exceeds me: Transcendence (that ground of Being which is never an object in the world). Freedom, then, is not the power to do without everything: it is the place where a human being receives themselves.

4 · Resonance

Living with this idea

First, this thought disarms the universal excuse: "that's just how I am." My temperament, my past, my milieu are real — Jaspers denies no partial determinism. But none of them speaks the last word. At any age, in any condition, there remains that millimetre of play where I can answer differently. Jaspers's freedom does not say "you can do anything"; it says "you can always do something — and that something makes you."

Second, it invites a very concrete sorting: among all my "choices" this week, which were mere preferences (let habit or calculation settle those, fine) — and which one, perhaps, was a choice in which I myself was at stake? Learning to recognise those moments, and not to flee them into distraction or procrastination, is already a practice. Freedom cannot be stored: it renews itself at each decision, as a flame exists only by burning.

Not to be confused

What it is not

  • The absence of constraints: one can enjoy every right and remain captive to one's fears; Jaspers's freedom is decided elsewhere.
  • Arbitrariness ("doing whatever I want"): the weathervane is not free — caprice is freedom's shadow, not its reality.
  • Consumer choice: selecting among prefabricated options does not commit who I am.
  • A demonstrable fact: no science will ever prove or refute my freedom — it is attested in the act.

What it is: the rare choice in which I choose myself — and discover, paradoxically, that I receive myself.
