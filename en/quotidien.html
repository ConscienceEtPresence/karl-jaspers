When it happens to you

Jaspers in everyday life

Eight situations — and what a serious philosophy can really do about them

A philosophy good only for understanding philosophy would not be worth much. Jaspers did not want to manufacture specialists: he wanted to awaken someone. Here, then, are eight situations almost everyone goes through — and what his thought actually changes in them. Each is built the same way: the lived experience, then what Jaspers will not say (because false consolations must be cleared away before anyone can speak), then what he offers.

One thing first, very clearly

This is not therapy, and Jaspers would be the first to say so — he was a psychiatrist before he was a philosopher, and he never confused the two. If something is heavy, lasting, if you no longer sleep, if you are thinking of ending it: it is not a philosopher you need, it is a doctor, and now. Philosophy does not heal. It illuminates — which is not the same thing, and replaces nothing.

“I have lost someone”

This is not a difficulty. It is a wall. Advice slides off, ready-made phrases wound, and the world carries on as if nothing had happened — which may be the most unbearable part of all.

What Jaspers will not say

That it has a meaning. That it is an ordeal that will make you grow. That time heals. Jaspers never consoled that way — he lived eight years with poison in his pocket; he knew what such phrases are worth.

What he offers

A name, first: this is a boundary situation. Not a problem to be solved — there is nothing to solve, and that is precisely why nothing works. Then one distinction, but a decisive one: there are two ways through this. Veiling your face — numbing, filling, not thinking about it. Or with open eyes. The second does not hurt less. It does something else: it settles. False urgencies fall away. What matters becomes simple. And Jaspers adds this, not as consolation but as fact: no one gets through it alone. One single person to speak truly with changes everything.

“My boss humiliates me”

In front of the others, often. And the worst is not the anger — it is what comes after: the shame of having taken it, of having said nothing, of going home with that.

What Jaspers will not say

That you should take a step back. That it isn't serious. That it says more about him than about you. Those phrases are true and perfectly useless — they ask you not to feel what you feel.

What he offers

Jaspers looks elsewhere. What was done to you, he would say, is that you were treated as an object — a function, an output, a cog. And your anger is not touchiness: it is the part of you that knows it is not one. He calls it dignity: it depends neither on your position, nor your results, nor his judgement — that is its very definition. Then comes the real question, and it is hard: in the second that follows, what do I do? Staying silent out of calculation and staying silent while knowing it are not the same act — even if the camera sees no difference. The second one, one day, speaks.

“I feel empty”

Everything is fine, though. Work, health, people around you. Nothing is missing — and an objectless sadness rises on Sunday evening, which no one can name.

What Jaspers will not say

That it is a depression to be treated. That you lack gratitude. That you need a project. Careful here: Jaspers was a psychiatrist, and he did not confuse things. If it is heavy, lasting, if you no longer sleep — see a doctor, not a philosopher. He would have said so first.

What he offers

But if it is not that, then he offers a surprising reading: this emptiness is not a breakdown. It is a signal. All your vital needs are met — and that is precisely why the question rises. It can appear only when survival no longer fills the whole space. Something in you is not fed by what feeds your life. That something has no other name than you. The Sunday emptiness is not the illness: it may be the only hour of the week when no one is speaking in your place.

“I cannot decide”

You made the lists. You asked people. You read, compared, weighed. And you are exactly where you were six months ago.

What Jaspers will not say

That you lack information. That you should listen to your heart. That you need a table with two columns.

What he offers

Jaspers would say something more disturbing: if no datum settles it, perhaps no datum can. There are choices that cannot be calculated — not because they are too complicated, but because they are not of a kind calculation touches. The sign is sharp: no one can make it in your place, and you know it. Seeking still “more information” is then no longer prudence: it is the most respectable way of not deciding. What you lack is not a fact. It is an act — and it will come from nowhere but you.

“I did something unforgivable”

Not a mistake. A wrong. Something you did, or let happen, that no explanation repairs.

What Jaspers will not say

That you must forgive yourself. That everyone makes mistakes. That the past is the past. On this ground Jaspers is rarely hard — and that is exactly what makes him useful.

What he offers

He begins by distinguishing, because “guilty” means four things. Did I break a law? That is for a court to say, not for my anxiety. Did I profit from an unjust order? That is political, and it can be repaired. Is it my conscience speaking? Then it alone is judge, and no court has the right to enter there. Or is it deeper still — the wound of having survived, of having been there, of not having prevented it? That fourth one is not punished: it transforms. And here is what Jaspers adds, which is the heart of it: guilt faced squarely is a boundary situation. Denial lulls, vague shame crushes — but guilt distinguished and assumed makes you someone. Our acknowledged faults are often the exact place where we became better. Not despite them. Through them.

“I no longer believe in anything”

Not God, not progress, not the big words. You are not bitter — it is calmer than that. Simply, nothing holds any more.

What Jaspers will not say

That you must find faith again. That science is enough. That everything is equal, so you may as well enjoy yourself.

What he offers

Look first at what you do, says Jaspers, not at what you say. You get up for your children. You keep a promise that costs. You refuse an injustice that would suit you. None of that is demonstrable — and you do it anyway. So you already believe: that the future deserves commitment, that a word has weight, that justice is worth more than profit. He calls it philosophical faith: not a religion, not a dogma, not a vague “spiritual but not religious” — a trust won by thinking, firm enough to carry a life, honest enough to include doubt. No longer believing in ready-made answers is not the end of faith. It may be its beginning.

“Nobody understands me”

You are surrounded. You talk all day. And you come home in the evening with the precise feeling that no one, today, has met you.

What Jaspers will not say

That you should go out more. That you are too demanding. That you should make an effort.

What he offers

Jaspers takes this solitude seriously — and he cuts it in two. There is an irreducible solitude: no one can decide, suffer or die in your place, and no relationship will change that. That one must be accepted; fleeing it is what makes people clingy. And there is the other one, the one that suffocates you: the lack of one single relationship in which you dare to be entirely true. Not ten. One. He calls it the loving struggle: two people who question each other without complacency and without trying to win. And he asks the uncomfortable question: that relationship, in my life — where is it? If the answer is “nowhere”, that is no condemnation — it is a compass. It requires less time than honesty.

“I have failed at everything”

A life's project, a marriage, a business. You had given everything, and it did not hold.

What Jaspers will not say

That we learn from failure. That you must bounce back. That it was a blessing in disguise. Jaspers does not recycle failure into a success method — it is the exact opposite of what he says.

What he offers

He begins with an observation about structure, not mood: everything human beings build eventually meets its limit. Works, loves, knowledge, life itself. This is not your bad luck — it is the horizon of every road. Then he reverses it: if every construction eventually breaks, it is because nothing finite is the absolute. Failure does not prove that nothing matters; it shows that what mattered was not in the result. And he adds the one sentence that counts here: what decides is not whether we fail or succeed, but the way in which we fail. Careful — he speaks only to those who genuinely built, loved, risked. Foundering says nothing to the one who attempted nothing.

One last word

One last word

You will have noticed that none of these answers solves anything. That is deliberate, and it is Jaspers entire: before a wall, there is nothing to solve. What he offers is more modest and more demanding — a name for what is happening to you, a distinction that cuts through the fog, and the possibility of going through it with open eyes rather than veiling your face. It is not a consolation. It is a companionship.
