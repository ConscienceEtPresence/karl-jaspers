Notion · To go further

The demonic

das Dämonische

1 · In one sentence

The definition

The obscure, excessive, destructive forces that run through people, collective passions and history. Jaspers refuses to believe that all human reality can be made transparent to reason.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

There are things people do that no explanation covers. Not miscalculations, not misunderstood interests: frenzies, gratuitous cruelties, absurd loyalties, crowds that tip over within an hour.

One can always produce an explanation afterwards — psychological, social, economic. It will be partly true, and you will know something is missing. The demonic is the name of what is missing: not what has not yet been explained, but what, in human beings, resists explanation.

After a fire, the investigator reconstructs everything: the seat, the spread, the materials. The report is exact and lies about nothing. It does not say why someone stood and watched it burn.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

1933. Refined professors, nourished on Goethe and Bach, fall in behind a brutal regime. We explain: the crisis, unemployment, the humiliation of Versailles, fear of communism, career opportunism. All of it true, documented, measurable.

And all of it insufficient — Jaspers watched from too close to be satisfied. There was something else: an intoxication, a relief at handing oneself over, something that took pleasure. None of those causes accounts for that pleasure.

Careful not to misread: naming it “demonic” excuses no one. No devil took them — that would strip them of responsibility, and Jaspers never grants that. It is an observation of lucidity: something in human beings wills their own ruin, and no science will abolish it by explaining it.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

This concept is a guard-rail against the optimism of reason. Jaspers's whole philosophy defends reason — against scientism, against dogma, against nihilism. The demonic is the price he pays to stay honest: acknowledging that this reason will never exhaust its object, because its object is man.

It belongs to a series of refusals. The refusal to believe that everything can be proven — that is non-knowledge. The refusal to believe that everything can be understood — that is the demonic. The first bounds science; the second bounds interpretation. Both say the same thing from two angles: man is not transparent, neither to measurement nor to meaning.

And this is also what forbids political naivety. If the demonic exists, no organisation, no education, no progress will immunise a society. What happened in 1933 can return — not because human beings are evil by nature, but because something in them does not answer to good reasons. Vigilance is never secured. It is a sober thought, and Jaspers had his reasons for being sober.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • The devil or the supernatural: no being is involved. It is a dimension of man, not a visitation.
  • An excuse: naming the demonic relieves no one of responsibility — Jaspers never grants that.
  • The irrational to be celebrated: he does not admire it. He observes that it exists, which is the opposite of praise.
  • A provisional gap: it is not what has not yet been explained. It is what, in man, resists explanation.

What it is: the part of man that lets itself be neither measured nor understood — and the refusal to believe that reason, however vast, will finish the job.

Where to read it in Jaspers: Psychology of Worldviews (1919); Man in the Modern Age (1931); as an echo: The Question of German Guilt (1946).
