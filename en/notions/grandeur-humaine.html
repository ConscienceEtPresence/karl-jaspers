Notion · To go further

Genius and human greatness

das Genie · die menschliche Größe

1 · In one sentence

The definition

The capacity of a human being to reach an exemplary depth of thought, creation or existence. In The Great Philosophers, Jaspers is less interested in doctrines than in the forms of greatness they embody.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

Two ways to miss a great mind. Worship it: make it an inexplicable genius, a freak of nature before which one can only fall silent. Or reduce it: explain the work by the illness, the childhood, the neurosis — and there, the mystery is dissolved, only a case remains.

Jaspers refuses both, and for the same reason: in both cases the work stops concerning us. Worshipped, it is out of reach. Explained, it has nothing left to say. The right question is another: what does this life open for mine?

A summit is not there to be contemplated from the valley, nor to be reduced to its geology. It is there to show that one can climb — and to indicate, to those who will never go that high, in which direction the slope is steep.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

Jaspers wrote on Nietzsche, whose illness he knew better than anyone — he was a psychiatrist. He could have done what his colleagues did: read the work as the symptom of a general paresis, date the ideas by the stages of the infection. That was the fashionable genre.

He did not, and his reason is sharp: even if the illness explains when and how he wrote, it will never say whether what he wrote is true. A thought is not refuted by the biography of the one who had it — otherwise any philosopher could be liquidated with their medical file.

And he makes the opposite gesture in The Great Philosophers: he places Socrates, the Buddha, Confucius and Jesus side by side. Not to compare doctrines — they are incompatible — but because each embodies a form of greatness, and a form can be looked at from any life whatsoever.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

What interests Jaspers is never performance. Greatness, for him, is not measured by what a person obtains — success, fame, influence — but by what they open for others. A great philosopher is not the one who was right: it is the one before whom a human possibility becomes visible.

Hence a decisive shift. We do not read the great in order to adopt their answers — those contradict one another, and rightly so. We read them to see what an existence is capable of. That is why Socrates and the Buddha can sit in the same book without our having to choose: they are not two competing theses, they are two forms.

There remains the guard-rail, and it is essential: this admiration must never become a cult. The great person is no authority — deferring to them would be exactly what their greatness forbids. Jaspers makes it a rule of reading: one appropriates a thought by fighting it, never by reciting it. And that holds for himself: turning Jaspers into a philosophical saint is to betray him with the clearest conscience in the world.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • The cult of genius: admiration that places someone out of reach is a polite way of refusing to be concerned.
  • Reduction to a symptom: illness may explain when and how someone wrote; it never says whether it is true.
  • Prestige: greatness is not measured by the success obtained, but by what it opens for others.
  • An authority: deferring to a great person is exactly what their greatness forbids.

What it is: an exemplary form of existence — not to be worshipped nor explained, but looked at in order to see what a human life is capable of.

Where to read it in Jaspers: The Great Philosophers (1957); Nietzsche (1936); Strindberg and Van Gogh (1922).
