Notion · To go further

Facticity

die Faktizität

1 · In one sentence

The definition

Everything given to me without my choosing it: my birth, my body, my era, my family, my past, my death. Freedom does not abolish it — freedom appears in how I take it on.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

Make a list of what you did not choose. The century you were born into. The country. The language. Your parents. Your body, its size, its health, what it can and cannot take. The events that fell on you. And, at the end, your death. The list is long — longer, no doubt, than the list of what you chose.

That is facticity: the given, the already-there, what there is nothing to negotiate about. Acknowledging it is neither pessimism nor resignation. It is simply looking at the cards in your hand rather than the ones you would have liked.

No player chooses their hand. They did not decide to be dealt those cards, and complaining will not change them one bit. What they decide is how they play them. And we have all seen mediocre hands played beautifully, and superb hands wasted.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

Two people receive the same diagnosis, on the same day, at the same age. Same illness, same prognosis, same possible treatments. The facticity is rigorously identical.

Six months later they are not living the same thing. One has closed up: it is unfair, why her, life is absurd. The other — being neither braver nor wiser — has made something else of the same datum: she settled what needed settling, said what she had never said, cut away what did not count.

Careful not to moralise this scene: the second has no merit to claim, and the first has nothing to reproach herself with. Jaspers orders no one to be admirable. He observes only this: the facticity was the same, and two existences came out of it. What separates them was not in what was given.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

Facticity is the point where Jaspers parts from both camps. To the determinisms — of genes, milieu, history — he concedes everything: yes, those determinations are real, massive, and no one escapes them. From the philosophies of sovereign freedom he takes away their dream: no, there is no subject choosing from nowhere, without a body and without a date.

What remains between the two is narrow, and everything is decided there: the relation I take up with what is given to me. No camera films it, no datum contains it — and yet two people in the same situation do not do the same thing. This is no proof of freedom; Jaspers would say there is none. It is what he calls its attestation.

One word must be added, or the notion stays dry: facticity is not only what limits me, it is what makes me real. A being without facticity — without a body, without an era, without death — would have nothing to decide, nothing to lose, nothing to love. It is because I am dated and mortal that my choices weigh something. The limit does not oppose freedom: it gives it its weight.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • An excuse: “that's just how I am” describes facticity and dodges the only question left — what am I making of it?
  • Fate: the given does not say what will come. It says only where one starts from.
  • Something unimportant: facticity is massive and serious. Minimising it (“it's all in your head”) is another way of lying.
  • Historicity: close but distinct — facticity is the raw given; historicity is that given insofar as I take it up and carry it on.

What it is: the cards I did not choose — massive, serious, and exactly what gives weight to the one thing that is mine: how I play them.

Where to read it in Jaspers: Philosophy II — Illumination of Existenz (1932), on situation and boundary situations.
