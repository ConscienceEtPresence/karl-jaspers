Notion · To go further

Intellectual integrity

die Redlichkeit

1 · In one sentence

The definition

Distinguishing without cheating what I know, what I infer from it, what I believe and what I do not know — and never disguising one as another. The virtue Jaspers demands before all others.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

Imagine someone telling you: “it's proven.” Three cases hide behind that sentence. Sometimes it is true — there is a measurement, a method, it can be checked. Sometimes it is an inference: they reasoned from something else, and that can be argued with. And sometimes it is simply what they believe very strongly, dressed up in the word “proven” to silence objection.

Intellectual integrity means refusing that third case — including in oneself. This is not modesty: one can be entirely honest and assert very strong things. It is simply the prohibition on granting oneself more warrant than one has.

An honest cashier does not mix the till with their own wallet. They may be rich or poor, generous or mean — that is not the question. The question is that at every moment they know whose money is whose. Integrity is that, but with your ideas: knowing at all times which are proven, which are borrowed, and which are yours alone.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

A friend tells you: “people never change.” Test the sentence with the tool. Is it knowledge? No — no study establishes that. Is it an inference? Partly: they have seen people fail to change. Is it a belief? Yes, largely: it is a worldview, and it may happen to suit them.

None of this makes them dishonest. What would decide is what they do when you say so. If they answer “true, that's what I believe” — they are honest. If they answer “no no, it's a fact” — something has just happened: a belief has put on the white coat of knowledge. Jaspers spent his life hunting that precise moment, in scientists as in believers, in others as in himself.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

This word is not decorative in Jaspers: it is the condition of everything else. Look at the architecture. His philosophical faith holds only because it knows it is not a knowledge — without integrity it would become dogma. His refusal of scientism is exactly the same gesture reversed: science is honest when it says where it stops; it becomes superstition when it decrees that nothing exists beyond. And communication would be impossible without it: truly speaking with someone presupposes that neither is bluffing about their hand.

One must see where this demand came from. It was born in the clinic. The young Heidelberg psychiatrist watched colleagues explain whole lives by a single theory — biological, psychoanalytic, social — with the same assurance in every case. His General Psychopathology (1913) is, at bottom, a book of integrity: here is what each method reaches, here is where it stops, and here is what none of them will ever reach.

The hardest part remains to be said, and Jaspers accepts it: integrity costs. It forbids the two comforts that keep most debates alive — the assurance of the one who knows, and the refuge of the one who will not commit. It obliges you to hold sentences like: “I believe this with all my strength, and I cannot prove it.” It is an uncomfortable position. It is also, for him, the only one that entitles anyone to speak.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • Modesty: it is not self-deprecation. One can be perfectly honest and assert considerable things — provided one says on what grounds.
  • Systematic doubt: integrity does not suspend judgement. It situates it.
  • Neutrality: it does not forbid taking sides — it forbids passing your side off as a fact.
  • Transparency: it is not telling everything about yourself. It is not touching up anything you put forward.

What it is: radical honesty in thought — the one virtue without which neither science, nor faith, nor communication can stand.

Where to read it in Jaspers: General Psychopathology (1913), on the limits of each method; The Perennial Scope of Philosophy (1948); Way to Wisdom (1950).
