Notion · To go further

Understanding and explaining

Verstehen und Erklären

1 · In one sentence

The definition

To explain is to seek the causes of a phenomenon. To understand is to grasp from within what it means to the one living it. Two rigours, two orders — and confusing them is the fault that manufactures scientism.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

Someone is crying. You can explain: tear glands, a hormone level, a brain area firing. All of it true, measurable, verifiable by anyone. You can also understand: he has just learned that his father died. There you measure nothing — you grasp a meaning.

Both are serious. Both can be wrong — a doctor can misdiagnose, and one can misread a person. But they do not work on the same object: one asks why it happens, the other what it means. Neither will ever replace the other.

A score and an emotion. An acoustician can measure everything about a sonata: frequencies, amplitudes, durations. Every figure will be right. Nothing will have been said of what the sonata tells — and the musician who knows cannot prove it by any measurement. Two true knowledges of the same object, which never meet.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

A teenager collapses in class. Three adults react. The nurse explains: hypoglycaemia, he skipped breakfast — and she is right, the blood sugar confirms it. The psychologist understands: he has just been humiliated in front of the others, and humiliation cuts your legs from under you — and he is right too. The teacher hesitates between the two, and that is exactly the right position.

For here is the point: these are not two competing hypotheses of which only one is true. They are two planes. The sugar explains the body falling; the humiliation understands why it is today, here, in front of them. Choosing one against the other is not rigour: it is amputation.

And the hardest part for the adult: understanding cannot be verified like blood sugar. One can be wrong about what a child is living through, and no blood test settles it. The only possible verification is called talking to him.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

This distinction was not born in a philosopher's study: it was born in the Heidelberg psychiatric clinic, in a physician of thirty. Jaspers watched colleagues explain whole lives by a single theory — biological, psychoanalytic, social — with the same assurance every time. His General Psychopathology (1913) finally separates the two gestures, and that separation remains one of the foundations of psychiatry today.

He adds a decisive word: the process. When a psychic transformation can no longer be understood as the meaningful development of the person who came before — when there is rupture, not evolution — understanding runs aground and explanation must take over. That word marks exactly the border between the two methods, and it is a clinical tool, not a philosophical position.

His whole philosophy comes out of this. If man can be explained as an object but must be understood as an existence, then no science will ever exhaust him — not through lateness, but by structure. The psychiatrist's distinction becomes the hinge: it separates consciousness in general (which explains) from spirit (which understands), and it leaves open the place of Existenz, which neither reaches.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • An opposition between science and intuition: understanding has its rules, its errors, its corrections. It is not a feeling.
  • A hierarchy: understanding is not better than explaining. On a lesion, explanation is sovereign and understanding has nothing to say.
  • Two competing hypotheses: they do not answer the same question. “Why it happens” and “what it means” can be true together.
  • A provisional border: no progress will make a measurement state a meaning. It is not a lack of power, it is a difference of kind.

What it is: the two rigorous gestures of knowledge about human beings — one seeks causes, the other grasps meaning. All of Jaspers's philosophy lies in refusing to confuse them.

Where to read it in Jaspers: General Psychopathology (1913) — where it all begins; then Philosophy (1932) and Von der Wahrheit (1947).
