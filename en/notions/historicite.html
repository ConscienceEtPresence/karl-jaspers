Notion · To go further

Historicity

die Geschichtlichkeit

1 · In one sentence

The definition

The fact that I become myself only from an origin, a language, an era and ties I did not choose. Existenz is never an abstract freedom hovering outside time.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

No one starts from zero. You were born somewhere, on a date, into a language, from a family, amid ideas already in circulation. None of it was submitted for your approval. And yet it is with that, and only with that, that you can become someone.

Historicity is that simple fact taken seriously. Not as a prison — “I am the product of my milieu, nothing to be done” — but as ground. One walks only on ground. The dream of a freedom with no starting point is not a larger freedom: it is a freedom that touches nothing.

A tree did not choose its ground. Too much wind, too much limestone, a rock to the left — none of it negotiable. And yet it is this tree, bent this particular way, that exists. No tree grows “in general”. A tree that had grown everywhere would have grown nowhere.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

You did not choose your mother tongue. It was put into your mouth before you could judge it, with its words, its turns of phrase, its reticences — and what it cannot say, you will struggle to think. That is a real limit.

And it is also the only thing that lets you speak at all. A “neutral” language, belonging to no one's history, does not exist — and if it did, it would say nothing alive. What limits you is exactly what makes you capable.

Now watch what is at stake. You can undergo that language: speak as people speak around you, never questioning it. Or you can inhabit it: take it up, bend it, say in it something no one had said. Same situation, two lives. There, exactly there, lies the question of historicity.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

Jaspers holds together two things philosophy usually separates. Against philosophies of abstract freedom: there is no Existenz “in general”, choosing from nowhere. I always decide here, with this body, in this century, with these words. Against determinisms: this situation is not my cause, it is my material. What decides is not what is given to me, but the relation I take up with what is given.

Hence a reversal I find among the finest in his thought. What looks like a limitation — being dated, situated, particular — is the very condition of depth. The person who would belong to every tradition inhabits none; the one who would be from everywhere is from nowhere. We touch the universal only through an absolutely singular history. That is true of people, and true of civilisations: the Axial Age is no abstract world-thought, it is five centres, each in its own language, each at home.

He proved it in his own life. He chose neither his illness, nor his country, nor the year 1933. He chose what he would make of them. An abstract freedom would have had nothing to decide — and nothing to lose.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • History as a discipline: historicity is not knowledge of dates. It is my condition of being situated.
  • Determinism: my situation does not produce me — it gives me something to take on. The relation I take up with it is not in what is given.
  • Relativism: that every truth is lived from a situation does not mean all are equal. We seek the universal — but reach it only by a path.
  • Identitarian rootedness: taking on your history is not locking yourself into it. An inhabited origin opens; a recited origin is a shell.

What it is: the ground of every real existence — not what prevents me from becoming myself, but that from which alone I can.

Where to read it in Jaspers: Philosophy II — Illumination of Existenz (1932); The Origin and Goal of History (1949).
