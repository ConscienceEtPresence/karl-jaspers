Notion · To go further

Absolute consciousness

absolutes Bewusstsein

1 · In one sentence

The definition

The inner certainty by which Existenz experiences an unconditional demand — in conscience, love, fidelity, decision. Certain for me, and never imposable on anyone.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

There are things you are absolutely sure of, and will never be able to prove. That betraying this friend would mean losing yourself. That what you did was right, even though everyone said otherwise. It is not an opinion — you are not attached to it, you live by it.

The word “absolute” is not arrogant here. It does not mean “true for everyone”. It means: unconditional — I cannot give it up without ceasing to be myself. It is a certainty that needs no proof, and will never supply one.

A colour-blind person will never see what you call red, and no demonstration will change that. You see it — without being able to show it. The difference is that nobody goes to war over red.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

A man refuses to denounce someone. He is asked why. He tries: “it's wrong”, “you don't do that”, “I couldn't look at myself again”. None of these sentences is an argument — they are restatements of the same certainty.

And yet he will not yield. You can convince him he is wrong about the facts, the consequences, the risks: nothing will move. What holds him is not at the end of a reasoning, so no reasoning will dislodge it.

Here is the decisive point, and this is where Jaspers parts from everything that resembles him: this certainty binds him. It gives him no rights over others. He cannot say “and you must do the same” — for he can prove nothing, and he knows it. A certainty without proof that made demands of others would be exactly fanaticism.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

This concept answers a formidable objection. If Existenz cannot be proven, what distinguishes it from caprice? Answer: nothing, from outside. Seen from without, a man who refuses out of conviction and a man who is merely stubborn look exactly the same. That is why Jaspers seeks no external criterion — there is none.

But there is a difference, and it is internal. Absolute consciousness does not assert: it obliges the one who feels it. It does not enlarge him, it puts him in debt. Remember the paradox of freedom: at the moment I am most myself, I do not feel I invented my demand — I feel I receive it. Caprice feels powerful. Absolute consciousness feels bound.

Hence the line Jaspers never crosses, and which holds for his whole philosophy: an existential certainty is unconditional for me and non-compelling for others. The day it claims to compel, it has disguised itself as knowledge — and there, exactly there, dogmatisms are born. The fanatic is not the one who believes too strongly: it is the one who mistakes their certainty for a proof.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • A proof: it is not one and seeks none. It binds the one who feels it; it compels no one.
  • A strong opinion: an opinion is defended, negotiated, forgotten. This one cannot be let go without losing oneself.
  • A universal truth: “absolute” means unconditional — not valid for everyone.
  • Fanaticism: its exact opposite, and its closest counterfeit. The fanatic demands of others what only a proof could demand.

What it is: the certainty without proof that holds me — unconditional for me, with no rights whatever over anyone else. That asymmetry is all that separates it from fanaticism.

Where to read it in Jaspers: Philosophy II — Illumination of Existenz (1932); The Perennial Scope of Philosophy (1948).
