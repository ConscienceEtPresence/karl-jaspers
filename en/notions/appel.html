Notion · To go further

The appeal

der Appell

1 · In one sentence

The definition

The form Jaspers's speech takes: it does not demonstrate Existenz, it addresses it. A philosophical text does not merely transmit information — it awakens, or it fails.

2 · Put simply

If it were explained to you at fifteen

There are two ways of writing something true. You can inform: here are the facts, check them. The reader receives, and nothing is asked of them — they need not even be anyone in particular in order to understand.

And you can appeal: here is what I see, what will you do with it? There, the text is not complete without you. It leaves you free to do nothing — and that is why it can fail. This is how Jaspers writes, and it is not a style: it is a logical consequence of what he thinks.

A road sign and an invitation do not work the same way. The sign says “Lyon, 40 km” — it is right whether you are going to Lyon or not. The invitation says “come” — and it only means anything if someone can refuse. A sign that pleaded would be ridiculous; an invitation that compelled would no longer be an invitation.

3 · An everyday example

The notion in action

Take this sentence from the site: “which decision in my life could have been made only by me — and when was the last one?” That is not information. There is nothing to check, nothing to memorise, nothing to repeat in an exam.

And yet, if you stopped for a second, something happened that ten pages of explanation would not have produced. If you did not stop, the sentence did nothing — and it has no way of forcing you.

That is exactly Jaspers's wager: he accepts failing. A philosopher who wanted to guarantee his effect would have to compel — and compelling Existenz is a contradiction in terms, since it is nothing other than what answers freely.

4 · In depth

What Jaspers really makes of it

This concept follows directly from the structure. Existenz never becomes an object: it therefore cannot be described, proven or taught. What is left to a philosopher who speaks of it anyway? He cannot know it — he can illuminate it. That is Jaspers's exact word, and the title of his second volume: illumination of Existenz. One does not illuminate in order to show an object; one illuminates so that someone may see.

This explains the form of his whole work, which often disconcerts. Why does he never conclude? Why these sentences that circle without ever grasping? It is no failure of rigour — it is rigour itself: to grasp would be to objectify, and to objectify would be to miss what he is speaking of. The only honest mode of access is indirect.

And from this follows a consequence on which he never compromises: no one can be awakened by force. An appeal that compelled would no longer be an appeal — it would be propaganda. This is why there is in him no school, no disciples, no self-development method: the only thing he can do is make possible what he himself will never produce.

5 · Not to be confused · Reading Jaspers

The right word, and where to read it

  • A slogan: the slogan wants assent without passing through freedom. The appeal aims at freedom precisely.
  • A moral exhortation: the appeal orders nothing and does not say what to do. It opens a possibility.
  • A demonstration: it does not prove — if it proved, it would not need you.
  • Rhetoric: rhetoric seeks its effect at any cost. The appeal accepts producing nothing at all.

What it is: the only honest way of speaking about what cannot be proven — a speech that leaves you entirely free, and can therefore fail.

Where to read it in Jaspers: Philosophy II — Illumination of Existenz (1932); Reason and Existenz (1935).
